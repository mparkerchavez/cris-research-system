## Initial Observations

This article introduces a critical conceptual framework for understanding taste in an AI-augmented world by distinguishing between personal taste (subjective preference derived from accumulated experience) and cultural taste (socially constructed standards of "good taste"). The author argues that as AI tools reduce execution barriers, taste becomes an increasingly important competitive differentiator. However, the confusion surrounding taste stems from conflating these two distinct forms, both of which operate differently and require different cultivation strategies.

The piece emphasizes that taste is not innate but developed through active engagement and deliberate reflection—what the author calls "reading like a writer, using like a builder." The most critical insight is that tastemakers function as cultural reference points (the "oboe" to which the orchestra tunes), suggesting that those who develop strong personal taste combined with the ability to articulate their discernment can shape broader cultural standards. This has significant implications for AI adoption and workforce transformation, as it suggests that AI literacy requires not just technical skill but the ability to recognize, articulate, and refine preferences.

The article identifies a potential positive side effect of AI tools: they may transform users from passive consumers into active creators by forcing engagement with previously hidden design decisions and craft choices. This suggests that widespread AI adoption could accelerate skill formation in discernment and creative practice—but only if users engage intentionally with their tools rather than treating them as mere execution engines. The unresolved tension around objective versus subjective quality suggests ongoing measurement and evaluation challenges in assessing creative and professional output in AI-augmented contexts.
