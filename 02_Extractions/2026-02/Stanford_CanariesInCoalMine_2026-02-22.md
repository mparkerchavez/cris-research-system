# Stanford: Canaries in the Coal Mine — Entry-Level AI Employment Disruption

## Metadata
- **Source:** Stanford University
- **Authors:** Erik Brynjolfsson, Bharat Chandar, Ruyu Chen
- **Published:** 2025-11-13
- **Processed:** 2026-02-22
- **Type:** Academic Paper (PDF)
- **Original Location:** 01_Raw_Inputs/2026-02/2026-02-15_to_21/Stanford_Canaries-in-the-Coal-Mine_Nov25.pdf
- **Data Source:** ADP payroll records (3.5–5 million workers monthly, Jan 2021–Sep 2025)

---

**DP1:** Entry-level workers in AI-exposed roles experienced sharper employment declines than older workers following generative AI adoption.
- **Anchor:** "Early-career workers (ages 22-25) in AI-exposed occupations experienced 16% relative employment declines, controlling for firm-level shocks, while employment for experienced workers remained stable"
- **Citation:** (p. 1, Abstract)
- **Tags:** #entry-level-hiring-disruption, #hiring-transformation, #workforce-transformation, #competitive-disruption

**DP2:** Software developers aged 22-25 saw employment decline 20% from peak in late 2022 to September 2025, while older cohorts continued growing.
- **Anchor:** "By September 2025, employment for software developers aged 22-25 declined nearly 20% compared to its peak in late 2022"
- **Citation:** (p. 9, Section 4.1)
- **Tags:** #entry-level-hiring-disruption, #hiring-transformation, #deployment-bottleneck

**DP3:** Young workers in AI-exposed jobs faced 6% employment decline from late 2022 to September 2025, while workers 35+ saw 6-9% increases.
- **Anchor:** "Workers aged 22 to 25 have experienced a 6% decline in employment from late 2022 to September 2025 in the most AI-exposed occupations, compared to a 6-9% increase for older workers"
- **Citation:** (p. 3, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #entry-level-hiring-disruption, #workforce-transformation

**DP4:** Labor market adjustments manifested primarily through employment reductions rather than wage decreases, suggesting wage stickiness.
- **Anchor:** "Adjustments occur primarily via employment rather than compensation, with employment changes concentrated in occupations where AI automates rather than augments labor"
- **Citation:** (p. 1, Abstract)
- **Tags:** #measurement-framework-reckoning, #workflow-transformation

**DP5:** Employment declined significantly in occupations where AI performs automative tasks, but remained stable or grew where AI is augmentative.
- **Anchor:** "Entry-level employment has declined in applications of AI that automate work, but not those that most augment it"
- **Citation:** (p. 3, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #agentic-workflows, #workflow-transformation, #autonomous-systems, #cognitive-offloading

**DP6:** Overall compensation showed minimal variation by age or AI exposure quintile despite large employment divergences, indicating wage stickiness.
- **Anchor:** "We find little difference in annual salary trends by age or exposure quintile, suggesting possible wage stickiness"
- **Citation:** (p. 4, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #measurement-framework-reckoning, #policy-gap

**DP7:** LLM adoption at work reached 46% among U.S. adults by mid-2025, with 40% of population age 18-64 using generative AI.
- **Anchor:** "LLM adoption at work among U.S. survey respondents above age 18 reached 46% by June/July 2025"
- **Citation:** (p. 2, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #adoption-barriers, #market-demand, #executive-awareness-gap

**DP8:** AI capabilities improved dramatically in coding tasks, rising from 4.4% to 71.7% on SWE-Bench between 2023 and 2024.
- **Anchor:** "AI systems improved from solving 4.4% of coding problems to 71.7% on SWE-Bench, a widely used benchmark for software engineering"
- **Citation:** (p. 2, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #model-capabilities, #capability-overhang, #self-acceleration

**DP9:** Current AI systems could match or exceed performance of up to 47% of industry professionals on benchmarks of economically valuable tasks.
- **Anchor:** "Current systems could match or outperform up to 47 percent of industry professionals on a predefined benchmark of economically valuable tasks"
- **Citation:** (p. 2, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #model-capabilities, #productivity-impact, #capability-overhang

**DP10:** Firm-time fixed effects revealed 15 log-point relative employment decline for 22-25 year-olds in highest AI exposure quintile, controlling for aggregate firm shocks.
- **Anchor:** "For workers aged 22-25, we find a 15 log-point decline in relative employment for the most AI-exposed quintiles compared to the least exposed quintile, a large and statistically significant effect"
- **Citation:** (p. 4, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #entry-level-hiring-disruption, #risk-governance, #measurement-framework-reckoning

**DP11:** Employment decline patterns held across both teleworkable and non-teleworkable occupations, excluding outsourcing as primary explanation.
- **Anchor:** "Young workers more exposed to AI face slower employment growth in both teleworkable and non-teleworkable occupations"
- **Citation:** (p. 15, Section 4.6)
- **Tags:** #hiring-transformation, #workforce-transformation, #policy-gap

**DP12:** AI exposure effects on young workers emerged sharply starting late 2022/early 2023, coinciding with ChatGPT proliferation.
- **Anchor:** "The patterns we observe in the data appear most acutely starting in late 2022 and early 2023, around the time of rapid proliferation of generative AI tools"
- **Citation:** (p. 4, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #temporal-compression, #deployment-stages, #market-demand

**DP13:** Similar employment decline patterns observed in LinkedIn and UK data independently, strengthening evidence of AI-driven displacement.
- **Anchor:** "After we first publicly shared our results, work by Hosseini and Lichtinger (2025) and Klein Teeselink (2025) similarly found declines in AI-exposed entry-level employment using LinkedIn data"
- **Citation:** (p. 6, Section 2)
- **Tags:** #hiring-transformation, #entry-level-hiring-disruption, #competitive-disruption

**DP14:** Codified knowledge appears preferentially substitutable by AI versus tacit knowledge accumulated through experience.
- **Anchor:** "AI disproportionately substitutes for workers using codified knowledge... AI may be less capable of replacing tacit knowledge, the idiosyncratic tips and tricks that accumulate with experience"
- **Citation:** (p. 5, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #skill-formation, #workforce-readiness, #cognitive-offloading

**DP15:** Young workers supply proportionally more codified knowledge than tacit knowledge, exposing them to greater task replacement.
- **Anchor:** "As young workers supply relatively more codified knowledge than tacit knowledge, they may face greater task replacement from AI in exposed occupations"
- **Citation:** (p. 5, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #workforce-readiness, #skill-formation, #equity-paradox

**DP16:** Hiring reduction at junior level emerged as lowest-friction adjustment margin due to wage ladder structures and employee mobility concerns.
- **Anchor:** "Reduced hiring may also be the lowest-friction adjustment margin, compounded by inefficient incentives to train entry-level workers who may move firms"
- **Citation:** (p. 5, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #workflow-optimization, #hiring-transformation, #organizational-transformation

**DP17:** Employment decline patterns persisted across both high and low college-share occupations, challenging COVID-19 education disruption explanation.
- **Anchor:** "They hold for both occupations with a high share of college graduates and ones with a low college share, suggesting deteriorating education outcomes during COVID-19 do not drive our results"
- **Citation:** (p. 4, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #workforce-readiness, #hiring-transformation, #policy-gap

**DP18:** For non-college workers, experience provided less buffering against AI disruption than for college-educated workers, with divergence visible through age 40.
- **Anchor:** "For non-college workers, we find evidence that experience may serve as less of a buffer to labor market disruption, as low college share occupations exhibit divergent employment outcomes by AI exposure up to age 40"
- **Citation:** (p. 4, Section 1)
- **Tags:** #equity-paradox, #workforce-transformation, #entry-level-hiring-disruption

**DP19:** Results robust to excluding technology occupations and information technology firms, indicating AI effects span beyond tech sector.
- **Anchor:** "Results are quite similar, consistent with the above case studies showing employment changes across various occupations"
- **Citation:** (p. 13-14, Section 4.6)
- **Tags:** #competitive-disruption, #hiring-transformation, #market-demand

**DP20:** Occupations with high interest rate exposure negatively correlated with AI exposure, weakening interest rate shock explanation for youth employment decline.
- **Anchor:** "AI exposure and interest rate exposure are negatively correlated overall, with occupations such as construction having high interest rate exposure and low AI exposure"
- **Citation:** (p. 15, Section 4.6)
- **Tags:** #risk-governance, #measurement-framework-reckoning, #policy-gap

**DP21:** Employment patterns consistent across gender, suggesting diverging prospects for men versus women do not drive findings.
- **Anchor:** "Results are similar, suggesting that diverging prospects for men and women are not driving our findings"
- **Citation:** (p. 15, Section 4.6)
- **Tags:** #equity-paradox, #workforce-transformation, #measurement-framework-reckoning

**DP22:** Anthropic Economic Index enables distinction between automative and augmentative AI uses based on millions of Claude conversation queries across tasks.
- **Anchor:** "This index reports the estimated share of queries pertaining to each O*NET task based on several million conversations with Claude, Anthropic's generative AI model"

**DP23:** Young workers in occupations with highest automation shares experienced sharply declining employment, while augmentation exposure showed muted correlation.
- **Anchor:** "Panel B likewise shows that the occupations with the highest estimated automation shares have experienced declining employment for the youngest workers. In contrast, Panel C indicates that occupations with highest augmentation shares have not experienced a similar pattern"

**DP24:** Economic adjustment via technology historically follows heterogeneous impact pattern, with some workers benefiting more than others over time.
- **Anchor:** "Adoption of new technologies typically leads to heterogeneous effects across workers, resulting in adjustment periods as workers reallocate from displaced forms of work to new forms with growing labor demand"
- **Citation:** (p. 16, Section 5)
- **Tags:** #temporal-compression, #organizational-transformation, #workforce-transformation

- **Anchor:** "Future work would benefit from better firm-level AI adoption data, which would provide sharper variation for estimating plausible causal effects of AI on employment"
