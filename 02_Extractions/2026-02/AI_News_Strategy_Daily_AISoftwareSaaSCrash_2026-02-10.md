## Initial Observations

This analysis reframes the $285B software market repricing from "AI kills software" narrative to "AI collapses access-based pricing models" thesis. The speaker distinguishes between:

1. **Market repricing event** (Thomson Reuters stock drop)—temporary, potentially reversible
2. **Operating event** (KPMG fee negotiation)—structural, precedent-setting, irreversible

The critical insight is that Jensen Huang's defense of software infrastructure (AI needs databases, APIs, middleware) is true but orthogonal to the actual threat: the per-seat pricing model that funded enterprise software empires. Thomson Reuters' data (case law) remains irreplaceable; the pricing mechanism (per Westlaw login) becomes indefensible.

This parallels individual knowledge worker dynamics: existing expertise and domain knowledge aren't commoditized, but the assumption that you can sell labor at historical rates without AI-driven workflow redesign is broken. The window to rethink (both for SaaS vendors and individuals) is compressing faster than update cycles can track.

The structural concern is transformation risk, not obsolescence: survival requires simultaneous maintenance of legacy systems while building agentic-first replacements—a capital allocation challenge that has killed companies historically.
