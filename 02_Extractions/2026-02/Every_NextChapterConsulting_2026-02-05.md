## Initial Observations

This article provides a practitioner's view of AI adoption that complements the Section study nicely. While Section focused on workforce proficiency measurement, Every focuses on implementation methodology and industry-specific patterns.

The four-level maturity model is practical and actionable, though I notice it maps roughly to Section's categories (Level 1 = Experimenter, Levels 3-4 = Practitioner/Expert). The key insight is that most organizations are stuck at Level 1 despite wanting Level 4.

The "allocation economy" thesis is provocative—it suggests AI shifts the value from knowing things to managing resources effectively. This aligns with the Section finding that leaders need to develop new capabilities, not just technical skills.

Important nuance: Every emphasizes that the best starting point for AI implementation isn't always the highest-value work. In their hedge fund example, enabling back-office functions (compliance, ops, recruiting) provided bigger gains than starting with investors. This suggests a strategic sequencing question: where are the bottlenecks?

The documentability heuristic is useful: if you can write down how to do something, you can probably automate it. If not (like "what makes a good investment"), it resists automation. This maps to Every's focus on finance and tech—industries with rich data and documented processes.

The time savings examples are concrete: 5-10 hours/week for recruiters, 50 hours per investment memo, week-long analysis compressed to minutes. These align with Section's finding that practitioners save 8-12 hours/week.

Notable: the pace of change is so fast that year-old practices "feel ancient." This reinforces Section's point about the rising proficiency bar.

The "master prompt as job description" is an elegant starting point. It mirrors how you'd onboard a human, which makes sense given the "managing a junior intern" framing.
