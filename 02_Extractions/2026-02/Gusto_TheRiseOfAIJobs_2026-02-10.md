## Initial Observations

This research report provides empirical grounding for AI hiring transformation at the small business level. Key patterns include:

1. **Exponential Growth with Concentration**: While AI hiring remains statistically rare (0.08% of 2025 hires), the 8x growth since 2019 and doubling year-over-year trajectory indicates inflection point dynamics.

2. **Title Evolution as Deployment Proxy**: The shift from "AI Trainer" (data annotation, model training) → "AI Engineer" (native AI product development) → "AI Intern" (junior pipeline building) reflects progression from experimental to production to scaled-operational stages.

3. **Company Size Decoupling**: The concentration of hiring in sub-20-person firms and micro-enterprises suggests that AI capability doesn't require organizational scale—contradicting traditional enterprise technology adoption patterns.

4. **Sectoral Bifurcation**: The 83% concentration in Tech + Professional Services creates a two-tier labor market—high-skill, high-wage AI roles concentrated in tech hubs and consultancies; traditional sectors largely untouched.

5. **Geographic Lock-in**: The disproportionate concentration in tech hubs (21% in top 5 cities) suggests that AI labor market development has strong path-dependent and clustering characteristics, potentially amplifying regional inequality.

6. **Maturation Indicators**: The emergence of internship roles suggests that AI engineer positions are transitioning from specialist scarcity to structured, trainable roles—but only in concentrated geographic/sectoral pockets.

7. **Methodology Limitation**: The definition using explicit "AI" job title language likely undercounts actual AI capability adoption, as many roles incorporating AI tools don't label them as such (e.g., "Data Analyst" using AI tools).

The data suggests AI hiring is real but highly concentrated—geographically clustered in tech hubs, sectually biased toward Tech/Professional Services, and still early-stage despite rapid acceleration.
