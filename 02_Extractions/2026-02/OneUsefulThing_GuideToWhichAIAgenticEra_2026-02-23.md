## Initial Observations

Mollick's guide signals a critical transition point in AI adoption frameworks. Rather than comparing models primarily on benchmark performance, the author establishes that practical utility is now determined by a three-component architecture where the harness (tooling and autonomy layer) often matters more than the underlying model capability. This represents a fundamental inversion from the ChatGPT-era mentality where "which model is best?" dominated user decision-making. The emphasis on harness-driven differentiation explains why companies like Anthropic and OpenAI have diverged strategically — investing heavily in application-specific tools (Claude Code, Claude Cowork, OpenClaw) rather than competing purely on model architecture improvements.

The document reveals critical adoption barriers that remain largely invisible to casual observers. The user must navigate not merely model selection but also deliberately obscured interface choices (GPT-5.2 defaulting to weaker variants, Gemini requiring menu navigation for Deep Think access). This suggests companies are maintaining complexity as a feature gate, requiring paid subscribers to discover advanced capabilities. The $20/month barrier is positioned as essential not just for capability access but as the threshold where users gain agency in model selection — a shift from passive consumption to active configuration.

Mollick's practical example with Claude Code building 80-volume GPT-1 implementations autonomously, plus the recursive observation that Claude Cowork was built by Claude Code itself, signals genuine acceleration in agentic capability. These examples transcend marketing claims and demonstrate real workflow elimination at scale. The positioning of management over prompting as the emerging competency implies organizational structures themselves will need transformation — QA processes designed for human output verification are inadequate for autonomous agent monitoring and steering.

The security-capability tradeoff flagged in the OpenClaw section reveals an unresolved tension: agentic architecture requires broad system access to maximize utility, but this access creates attack surfaces. Cowork's VM-based isolation approach suggests architectural solutions exist, but they impose overhead that conflicts with seamless integration. This tension will likely drive governance, procurement, and policy decisions within enterprises more than raw capability benchmarks.
