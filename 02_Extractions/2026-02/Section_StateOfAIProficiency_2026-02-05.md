## Initial Observations

This is one of the most comprehensive workforce AI proficiency studies I've seen, and the findings are sobering. The "use case desert" concept perfectly captures what I've observed: companies invest in tools and training, but that only gets people to basic competency (experimenter level). The leap to meaningful work applications (practitioner level) requires something fundamentally different.

The executive awareness gap is particularly striking—C-suite thinks they're 2-3x further along than frontline employees report. This creates a dangerous situation where leaders may think the problem is solved when it's not.

Key insight: Foundation investments (policy, tools, training) are necessary but not sufficient. They move people from 0→1 (novice→experimenter) but don't solve 1→10 (experimenter→practitioner). That jump requires personalized, role-specific use case development and continuous reinforcement—not one-time training.

The Moderna example is instructive: 4-5 years, dedicated head of AI, appropriate budget, thousands of lunch-and-learns. This is a multi-year transformation, not a quarterly initiative.

Also notable: the proficiency bar keeps rising. What counted as "proficient" in 2024 (good prompting) is just table stakes in 2026 (meaningful use cases). This creates a treadmill effect where organizations must continuously invest just to maintain relative capability.

The emotional dimension matters: junior employees are anxious while seniors are excited. This isn't just about skills—it's about agency, support, and perceived job security.
