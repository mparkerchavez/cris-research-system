## Initial Observations

This interview is remarkably candid about structural shifts in design careers that other sources hint at but don't fully articulate. The tension between "do more with less" and "don't burn teams out" gets named explicitly (8:24). The return of VP-level leaders to IC work (43:09) is a striking signal—not just that tools matter, but that hands-on experience is becoming definitionally required for leadership credibility.

The compressed timeline patterns keep appearing: 5-day sprints → 1 day, 90-day onboarding → 3-week structural changes, years of learning → one Saturday hour. This isn't just about speed—it's about what Hearth calls the "great jump ball" where 20-year veterans and 2-year practitioners are equally novice at the new paradigm.

The "hire smart people and get out of their way" → active engagement shift feels like it maps onto the broader pattern we're seeing: AI doesn't make senior people less necessary, it makes their active participation MORE necessary because the work itself is more ambiguous and the tools are moving too fast for anyone to master alone.

The Vercel team size (7-8 people) is shocking given the company's profile and suggests radical compression is already here, not coming. If this is the benchmark, most design teams are 3-5x oversized for the new operating model.

The "craft is learnable" vs "pivot to PM" dichotomy (33:27) is refreshingly binary. She's not softening it—if you're a designer who doesn't want to learn craft, you should leave design. This aligns with the broader "role convergence but with higher standards" pattern we're seeing across extractions.
