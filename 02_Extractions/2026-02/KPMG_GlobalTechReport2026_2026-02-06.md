## Executive Summary

The KPMG Global Tech Report 2026 examines technology maturity, ROI realization, and organizational readiness across 2,500 tech executives representing eight industries globally. The report emphasizes that while tech maturity expectations are high, execution remains challenged by technical debt, talent shortages, and the need for adaptive strategies in a rapidly evolving landscape.

## Initial Observations

The KPMG Global Tech Report 2026 reveals a critical paradox in the Intelligence Age: while organizational ambitions for technology maturity are historically high, execution capabilities remain constrained by structural and cultural barriers. The data indicates that only 11% of organizations have achieved full tech maturity today, yet 50% expect to reach that level by end of 2026—a gap suggesting either overly optimistic projections or potential barriers that executives underestimate. This represents the classic #deployment-bottleneck phenomenon where the gap between ambition and capability creates execution risk.

The report highlights a critical inflection point in AI deployment. Organizations are transitioning from experimentation phases to at-scale implementation, with 88% already investing in agentic AI. However, this rapid adoption occurs amid persistent challenges: 69% acknowledge making security and scalability trade-offs, 53% face talent shortages, and 55% struggle to demonstrate AI value to stakeholders. The measurement crisis in AI ROI (58% acknowledge inadequacy of traditional metrics) suggests that organizations are scaling technologies without clear visibility into business value delivery—a particularly acute risk for business-critical systems.

High performers demonstrate that the path through these challenges exists but requires disciplined execution. They maintain 4.5x ROI despite lower relative investment, retain 50% permanent human staff (vs. emerging automation trends), and exhibit 70% high resilience to market changes. Their success appears grounded in specific differentiators: resolved technical debt (8% vs. 45% for others report it as blocking investment), centralized decision-making (91% vs. 78% overall), and strategic clarity on AI deployment (2% vs. 34% have fragmented AI programs). The quantum computing and AGI preparedness sections introduce a forward-looking risk dimension that most organizations appear unprepared for, with 41% admitting inadequate quantum threat preparation.

## Key Themes Across Document

1. **Execution Reality Gap:** High ambitions (50% expect top maturity in 12 months) vs. current constraints (11% fully scaled, 53% lacking talent)

2. **Tech Debt as Structural Barrier:** 69% acknowledge security/scalability trade-offs; 63% report tech debt costs blocking new investments

3. **AI ROI Measurement Crisis:** 58% acknowledge traditional metrics insufficient; 55% cannot demonstrate value to stakeholders; only 24% achieving ROI across multiple use cases

4. **Agentic AI Transition Point:** 88% investing in agents, 92% expect agent management as critical skill, but 32% have fragmented agent implementations

5. **Resilience Through Discipline:** High performers succeed through resolved tech debt, centralized decision-making, clear AI strategies, and balanced human-AI workforce planning

6. **Emerging Quantum/AGI Risk:** 41% unprepared for quantum threats; minimal discussion of AGI/ASI preparation despite timeline uncertainty

## Research Methodology Notes

- **Sample Size:** 2,500 tech executives
- **Geographic Scope:** 27 countries across Europe/Middle East/Africa (43%), Asia-Pacific (28%), Americas (29%)

- **High Performer Definition:** Organizations in top 2 maturity stages in 5+ technology categories, highest process maturity in 5+ functions, and 200%+ ROI on digital tech investments (represents 5% of sample)
