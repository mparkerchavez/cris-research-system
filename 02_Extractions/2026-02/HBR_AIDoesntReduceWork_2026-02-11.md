## Initial Observations

This article presents a critical counter-narrative to dominant AI productivity discourse, based on empirical eight-month ethnographic research at a technology company. Rather than the anticipated "productivity paradox" of workers gaining free time, the study reveals an "intensification paradox" where AI enables unsustainable work expansion through three interconnected mechanisms: task expansion via reduced friction, boundary erosion between work and non-work, and cognitive load from multitasking.

The research is particularly valuable for identifying how voluntary adoption creates organizational blindness—leaders may celebrate productivity gains while missing hidden costs of burnout and cognitive strain. The disconnect between measurable productivity outputs and worker wellbeing outcomes represents a critical measurement failure in the AI adoption narrative.

The proposed "AI practice" framework shifts accountability from individual self-regulation to organizational governance structures. By institutionalizing intentional pauses, sequencing, and human grounding, organizations can preserve the cognitive and creative capacity that continuous acceleration threatens. This frames AI governance not as productivity maximization but as sustainability optimization.

The article implicitly raises questions about whether current metrics capture real productivity or merely surface acceleration, and whether organizations are equipped to recognize the silent workload creep that current performance measurement frameworks obscure.
