## Initial Observations

This position paper argues that current LLMs, despite exceptional capabilities in deduction and induction, fundamentally lack the cognitive mechanism for scientific invention: abduction. The author, Tom Zahavy from Google DeepMind, uses Einstein's formulation of General Relativity as a computational case study to demonstrate three critical limitations:

1. **The Induction Limitation:** The "creativity as data compression" framework cannot account for discoveries made in data-scarce environments. General Relativity was formulated before empirical validation (Eddington's 1919 experiment), contradicting compression-driven learning models that require error signals.

2. **The Deduction Limitation:** While modern AI excels at formal theorem proving (AlphaProof, Gemini achieving gold-level IMO performance), deduction is a downstream process—it unfolds consequences from premises but cannot generate those premises. It is "verification of intent" rather than invention of intent.

3. **The Abduction Bottleneck:** The missing cognitive mechanism is manipulative abduction—embodied simulation grounding abstract symbols in physical sensation. Einstein's "happiest thought" (the elevator thought experiment) demonstrates this: direct physical simulation of acceleration conditions led to the equivalence principle hypothesis, not data analysis or logical derivation.

The paper challenges the reductionist premise that scientific discovery equals induction + deduction. Instead, it proposes that physically consistent, multimodal world models offering sensory grounding can bridge the abduction gap. Current world model architectures (Veo, Genie) show promise, particularly Genie's action-controllability enabling counterfactual intervention—the ability to "conceptually cut the cable" and test hypothetical physical variations.

The practical implication is substantial: building AI capable of true scientific invention requires moving beyond systems that merely read scientific literature toward systems that can perceive and interact with physical reality, holding strong priors about world structure and running counterfactual simulations to test intuitions against physics.

The paper concludes that while the necessity of the Abductive Jump is universal across disciplines, the nature of simulation must adapt: for physics, the substrate is material reality; for mathematics, it is the abstract landscape of formal systems.

## Relevance to CRIS Research Focus

This paper directly addresses **model capability limitations** (what frontier LLMs can and cannot do) and proposes that **agentic creativity and scientific discovery** require embodied world models beyond current transformer architectures. It contributes to the CRIS research on AI strategy around:

- Frontier model limitations in hypothesis generation and scientific reasoning
- The infrastructure gap: moving beyond language-only to multimodal, physically grounded systems
- Implications for enterprise AI: if LLMs cannot discover novel frameworks, their role is verification and optimization within existing paradigms, not true innovation
- World models as the critical next architectural shift enabling qualitatively different AI capabilities
