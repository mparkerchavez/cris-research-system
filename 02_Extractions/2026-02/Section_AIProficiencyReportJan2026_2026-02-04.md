## Initial Observations

The AI Proficiency Report reveals a fundamental and widening gap between organizational leadership's perception of successful AI deployment and the actual state of enterprise AI adoption. While companies have invested heavily in AI access, tool provision, and safety training—increases of 16-17% since March 2025—these foundational investments have not translated into meaningful business value. The report identifies a critical proficiency collapse: 70% of workers remain "AI experimenters" performing only basic tasks (summarization, email rewriting), less than 3% achieve "practitioner" or "expert" status capable of generating ROI, and 85% of the workforce lacks value-driving use cases entirely. This suggests that the problem is not awareness or access but rather the absence of structured pathways for workers to identify and implement high-impact applications in their specific domains.

The report uncovers a particularly acute equity issue centered on individual contributors, paradoxically the employees with the greatest opportunity to benefit from automation. Despite handling the most repetitive and automatable work, ICs receive the least manager support, tool access, and training. Manager support for IC AI usage has declined 11 percentage points since May 2025, contributing to elevated anxiety (74%) and minimal trust. This stands in stark contrast to C-suite adoption patterns—where 57% use AI daily with 94% trust levels—creating a 32-percentage-point gap in perceived access to clear tools and processes. The report frames this as a deliberate inversion of priorities and calls for immediate structural realignment.

Industry and functional analysis reveals significant competitive disparities. Technology, finance, and consulting firms demonstrate proficiency scores of 35-42 while healthcare, retail, and education lag at 27-29. Most striking is the systematic underutilization of AI for highest-impact functions: 87% of product managers avoid prototyping with AI despite its obvious applicability, 54% of engineers skip code generation, and customer service—despite maximum transformation potential—ranks last across all proficiency metrics. The report positions the path forward not as increased tool investment but as fundamentally restructuring AI adoption around measurable use case development, function-specific playbooks, manager accountability for IC enablement, and continuous progression pathways that recognize the rapidly rising capability bar of frontier models.
