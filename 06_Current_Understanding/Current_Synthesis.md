# Current Synthesis

**Last Updated:** February 28, 2026

## Summary

Enterprise AI has crossed a threshold where the binding constraints are no longer technological but organizational, institutional, and psychological. The evidence from February 2026 (155+ extractions, approximately 3,000+ data points across 38 sources in the final week alone) reveals three converging realities that have deepened since the February 22 synthesis, plus two emergent dynamics that reframe the entire adoption landscape.

First, specification has evolved from an individual skill to a four-layer organizational discipline. User-written prompts represent 0.02% of the total input an agent processes. The remaining 99.98% is organizational infrastructure: context files, workflow configurations, tool parameters, constraint architecture, and evaluation design. The four cumulative layers (prompt craft, context engineering, intent engineering, and specification engineering) form a discipline stack where each layer requires deeper organizational engagement than the last. Klarna's customer service agent resolved 2.3 million conversations by speed metrics while destroying customer relationships. Microsoft Copilot achieved 85% pilot adoption that collapsed to 5% at enterprise scale. These are not capability failures. They are specification failures, and they demonstrate that organizations treating AI deployment as a technology problem will systematically fail at scale.

Second, the demand-side trust deficit has become the most empirically validated constraint on AI value capture. Fifty-eight percent of Americans distrust AI systems, and this figure has not changed since September 2025 (YouGov, February 2026). Six months of accelerating capability produced zero shift in public sentiment. The US ranks dead last internationally on AI trust. Ninety-eight billion dollars in data center projects have stalled due to grassroots organizing. Eighty-eight percent of organizations report AI deployment, yet 33% of employees experience severe multi-dimensional anxiety about it. The critical finding is that approximately 80% of this opposition is implementation-specific and governable, but CEO rhetoric framing humans as inefficient compute actively hardens the 75% centrist majority that has not yet polarized. The window for governance-credible trust building is narrowing.

Third, adoption bifurcation has crystallized into five measurable axes operating simultaneously: organizational (34% deep transformers, 30% moderate implementers, 37% surface-level users per Deloitte's 3,235-respondent survey), temporal (two-hour AI decision cycles versus 18-month enterprise deployment timelines), workforce (back-office roles contracting 20% while coordination roles expand 30% or more), entrepreneurial (532,000 new business applications filed in January 2026 with formation costs down 70-80%), and skill-level (31-point senior-to-junior hiring gap across design and engineering). Block's restructuring established a new efficiency benchmark: $2 million or more in gross profit per person, four times the pre-COVID standard. The capability-dissipation gap names the mechanism behind the implementation chasm: the asymmetry between AI's exponential capability curve and society's slow, shallow absorption curve is not a temporary lag but a structural feature of institutional change. Two new dynamics compound the picture. The anxiety-driven adoption paradox reveals that fearful employees use AI at higher rates (65% versus 42%), creating usage metrics that mask burnout rather than signal value. And narrative authority now overrides metric authority in AI-related capital allocation, with a single zero-information blog post destroying over $300 billion in SaaS market value while Nvidia's blowout earnings confirmed supply-side confidence. The competitive window remains 18 to 24 months for organizational readiness, with a governance window bounded by late 2026 for organizational infrastructure and 2028 for political trust.
