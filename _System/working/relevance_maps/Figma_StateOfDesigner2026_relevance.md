## 1. CORE CAPABILITY ENDURES
**Active Idea:** Human skills valuable; expression changes through AI augmentation

**Primary DPs:** DP4, DP5, DP16, DP18

**Mapping:**
- **DP4** (Visual design as #1 in-demand skill, 58% vs. 54% for AI-use, 37% for AI-driven product design) proves craft demand persists beyond AI automation of surface work. This is Direct evidence that core capability (visual/craft excellence) is enduring.
- **DP5** (Craft defined as visual polish + emotional resonance correlates with designer happiness and business outcomes) shows the expression of that capability has shifted—no longer pure utility execution, but emotional/strategic weight.
- **DP16** (AI accelerates exploration while designers retain creative decision-making) explicitly models the capability-expression split: core design judgment survives; pipeline accelerates.
- **DP18** (Design excellence anchors resilience by maintaining focus on human-centered outcomes over tooling) validates that the capability is independent of tool iteration.

**Relevant Open Threads:** None directly; supports resolution of Skill Portability

**Confidence:** High (72% adoption; 89% faster; 91% quality improvement indicate augmentation, not replacement)

## 2. DESIGN'S VALUE IN NEAR-ZERO-COST BUILDING
**Active Idea:** Design value rises as building costs approach zero; craft as competitive moat

**Primary DPs:** DP9, DP12, DP15, DP3

**Mapping:**
- **DP9** (AI democratization; non-designers entering design process; designers shifting to strategic thinking) is the clearest signal. As implementation costs collapse (AI tooling), the scarcity shifts from execution to judgment/strategy. Role boundary erosion is the market signal that design is being reconstituted at higher value.
- **DP12** (Product builders tripled AI spending to support high-value work; 57% say AI helps them spend more time on high-value work) shows the market willingness to pay for design leadership even when execution is commoditizing.
- **DP15** (Craft-first commitment predicts business outcomes and growth rate perception) demonstrates that craft investment outperforms cost-cutting strategies in market perception and actual growth.
- **DP3** (89% faster, 80% better collaboration, 91% higher quality output) quantifies the cost reduction in execution; the gap between cost and value is expanding.

**Relevant Open Threads:** Design's Role in Enterprise Timeline; Specification Bottleneck

**Confidence:** High (role boundary shift + market spend allocation confirm moat thesis)

## 3. SPECIFICATION BOTTLENECK
**Active Idea:** Spec-writing limits output; clarity/direction critical constraint

**Primary DPs:** DP17, DP14, DP7, DP4

**Mapping:**
- **DP17** (Lack of clarity and direction is the #1 performance barrier, alongside micromanagement and unrealistic timelines) is Direct evidence. Designers explicitly identify specification/directional clarity as the binding constraint—not tool skill or capability.
- **DP14** (Designers seek stability and connection when boundaries shift; lack clear guidelines for role redefinition) shows the specification problem extends beyond task definition to organizational role clarity. This is a meta-spec problem.
- **DP7** (87% say autonomy helps; 91% say clear goals help—neither replaces the other) proves the specification-autonomy duality. Clear specs do not reduce the need for autonomy; they are independent requirements.
- **DP4** (Visual design/polish remains #1 demand despite AI automation) can also be read as evidence that designers are not being asked to specify higher-order problems—they're still being pulled into execution, signaling spec failure upstream.

**Relevant Open Threads:** This likely underpins multiple threads—Specification Clarity; Orchestration Consolidation; Governance Window

**Confidence:** Very High (explicitly stated in DP17; corroborated by role boundary erosion and autonomy-clarity duality)

## 4. ADOPTION BIFURCATION
**Active Idea:** Leaders and laggards diverging rapidly; regional and organizational stratification

**Primary DPs:** DP10, DP11, DP2, DP6

**Mapping:**
- **DP10** (Designer sentiment splits evenly: 36% improvement, 35% decline, 29% stagnation) shows no modal outcome; the profession is exhibiting bifurcated trajectories rather than consensus.
- **DP11** (Regional divergence: Middle East/APAC/LATAM 56-63% satisfaction vs. North America/Europe 38%) is Geographic Stratification evidence. This is not random variation—it correlates with market maturity, labor cost pressure, and adoption timing.
- **DP2** (Designers leaning into AI are 25% more likely to report happiness than non-adopters) confirms the happiness bifurcation within regions. This is not just geography; it's adoption posture within same market.
- **DP6** (67% of designers at companies increasing craft emphasis are happier; 60% when leadership pays attention) shows organizational leadership choice is creating stratification independent of geography.

**Relevant Open Threads:** Enterprise Timeline (adoption bifurcation across enterprise maturity levels); Measurement (how to quantify bifurcation momentum)

**Confidence:** Very High (DP10 + DP11 + DP2 + DP6 all point to stratification mechanisms)

## 5. TRUST & ORGANIZATIONAL CLARITY
**Active Idea:** Trust as scaling constraint; clarity as performance binding constraint

**Primary DPs:** DP13, DP6, DP14, DP17, DP7

**Mapping:**
- **DP13** (43% see AI as helpful augmentation; 25% express job security concerns; implicit 32% ambivalent) reveals trust distribution. The 25% concern floor represents the demand-side trust deficit even in positive-sentiment contexts. This is the scaling constraint—not engineering, but confidence.
- **DP6** (Leadership attention to craft correlates with 60% higher happiness) is organizational trust materialized. When leadership demonstrates investment in creative outcome rather than just output, trust increases.
- **DP14** (Designers seek stability and connection amid shifting boundaries) is the human cost of trust deficit. Role boundary erosion without clarity creates psychological friction that outweighs productivity gains.
- **DP17** (Lack of clarity/direction #1 barrier) and **DP7** (clear goals + autonomy both required) together show that clarity IS trust operationalized. Specification clarity = trust signal.

**Relevant Open Threads:** Trust Architecture; Self-Acceleration (does trust enable faster adoption?); Governance Window (role clarity creates trust)

**Confidence:** High (trust deficit implicit in DP13; trust mechanisms explicit in DP6, DP14, DP17)

## 6. SKILL FORMATION & ROLE TRANSITION
**Active Idea:** Skill portability; generalist return; role boundary erosion; specification clarity

**Primary DPs:** DP1, DP3, DP9, DP16, DP4, DP5

**Mapping:**
- **DP1** (72% adoption; 98% increased usage year-over-year) shows AI tooling is no longer "optional skill"—it's baseline capability. This is rapid skill formation at scale.
- **DP9** (AI democratization; non-designers entering design; designers moving to strategic thinking) directly models skill portability failure and generalist return. When PMs and developers can do execution design, designer skill advantage concentrates in judgment/systems thinking (generalist return).
- **DP3** (89% faster, 80% better collaboration, 91% better quality) suggests the skill surface has changed—speed + collaboration are now the performance metrics, not craft depth alone.
- **DP16** (AI accelerates exploration; designers retain creative decision-making) shows the skill boundary has shifted from execution to curation/judgment.
- **DP4** + **DP5** (Craft skills remain #1 demand; craft correlates with happiness and outcomes) indicates new skill hierarchies: craft judgment > execution speed > AI-use fluency.

**Relevant Open Threads:** Skill Portability (What design skills transfer to agent-native roles? DP9 suggests: judgment, specification, systems thinking); Generalist Return (Does designer generalism outperform specialization in post-AI context?)

**Confidence:** Very High (DP1 + DP9 + DP3 + DP16 collectively model skill formation; DP4 + DP5 rank new hierarchy)
