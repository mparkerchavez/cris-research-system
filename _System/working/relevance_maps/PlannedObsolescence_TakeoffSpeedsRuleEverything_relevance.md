## 6. Critical Patterns and Strategic Implications

### Primary Pattern: Takeoff Speed as Unifying Variable

The extraction reveals that Cotra's framework solves a critical communication problem in the AI strategy space. When parties claim "AGI by 2027" or "AGI by 2035," they often appear to disagree on timeline. The actual disagreement is on takeoff speed: fast takeoff advocates say the 2027 timeline implies existential risk within 2-3 years; slow takeoff advocates say the same timeline permits 5-10 year buffer; no-takeoff advocates say the timeline doesn't matter because feedback loop is negligible. Same timeline, radically different implications. This perfectly aligns with the Active Idea "2026 Bifurcation"—the strategic divergence this year isn't timeline prediction but acceleration assumption.

### Secondary Pattern: Takeoff Speed is Infrastructure-Grounded

Unlike abstract arguments about superintelligence capability, Cotra operationalizes takeoff speed in physical terms: "How many years of progress in hard tech (hardware, batteries, materials, robotics, spacecraft) per calendar year?" This grounds the debate in infrastructure constraints. If AI automates scientific research but can't automate chip fabrication, you don't get fast takeoff. If you automate chip fabrication but run into energy constraints, you get slow takeoff. This directly supports "Infrastructure-Application Divergence"—the physical world acts as constraint on software acceleration, not as irrelevant detail.

### Convergence with Work Intensification Paradox

The source doesn't explicitly address work intensification, but DP2 (R&D acceleration producing orders-of-magnitude speedup) implies that humans cannot keep pace with AI-driven discovery. Every breakthrough triggers new problems faster than humans can absorb. This validates the core tension in the Paradox: more AI capability → more work for humans to oversee/integrate/coordinate, not less. Fast takeoff scenarios simply compress this intensification into months rather than years.

### Strategic Implication: Measurement Framework Becomes Competitive Advantage

Current AI strategy discussions lack a shared measurement axis for "takeoff speed." Cotra's framework ("years of historical progress per calendar year") provides that axis. Organizations that adopt this measurement can:
1. Distinguish scenarios quantitatively (not just "fast/slow/no")
2. Identify which infrastructure constraints are binding (hardware? energy? manufacturing?)
3. Forecast policy effectiveness based on actual acceleration assumptions, not timeline guesses
4. Coordinate with external stakeholders using a common measurement language

This elevates the research question from "what do we believe about AGI risk?" to "what acceleration rate should we plan for, and what does that imply for policy/infrastructure/organization?"
