## 6. Critical Patterns

### Pattern 1: Narrative Authority Overrides Metric Authority
The most striking pattern is the inversion of valuation logic where investor narrative about existential threat (AI killing software, unsustainable capex) **overrides** direct business metrics (Nvidia 75% YoY growth, Salesforce earnings beat, contracted obligations). This appears in DP1 (dual anxiety paralyzing valuation), DP16 (earnings beat ignored), and DP6 (multiple compression despite attractive ratios). Critically, this isn't volatility that resolves—it's framework-level failure where traditional valuation logic (earnings, growth, obligations) becomes disconnected from market pricing. This maps directly to #18 (Demand-Side Trust Deficit) but with a specific mechanism: narrative authority now outweighs metric authority in AI-sector valuation.

### Pattern 2: Infrastructure Trust vs. Application Distrust Bifurcation
A clean bifurcation emerges between two classes of trust: investors trust infrastructure deployment (Nvidia visibility to 2027, data center growth momentum evident in backlog) while simultaneously distrusting application viability (Salesforce facing "AI killing software" narrative, software sector multiple compression, share loss to niche competitors). This isn't a single trust problem—it's a **structural bifurcation** where the same investor class trusts supply-side (Nvidia) and distrusts demand-side (enterprise software). Notably, infrastructure trust is **output-driven** (visibility, backlog, growth rates) while application distrust is **narrative-driven** (existential threat stories). This maps to #6 (Adoption Bifurcation) and #12 (Infrastructure-App Divergence) but reveals the trust mechanism differs between the two classes.

### Pattern 3: Temporal Capability Gradient Creates Asymmetric Enterprise Risk
DP5 establishes a profound capability gradient: software engineers "heavily affected today" while nurses "unaffected for decades." This isn't binary disruption—it's capability-stratified impact where different professional categories face different timelines. Combined with DP2-DP13 (infrastructure visibility extending to 2027), a critical asymmetry emerges: **infrastructure deployment timelines are uniform while capability impact timelines are stratified**. This means enterprises are racing to deploy infrastructure for which use-case clarity varies by profession/capability. The risk isn't capability endurance (it persists unevenly), but rather **specification misalignment**: building infrastructure for capability timelines that don't align. This maps to #8 (Timeline Collision) and surfaces as a new mechanism for #17 (Specification Bottleneck)—the bottleneck isn't "what can AI do" but "which capabilities matter to which departments on which timelines."

### Pattern 4: Institutional Guardrails Enable Market Maturity
DP17 reveals a sophisticated market dynamic: when the Supreme Court rejected Trump's tariff policy (a historically unprecedented constraint on executive authority), markets didn't panic—they'd already priced in institutional constraint. This isn't about tariffs; it's evidence that **market participants trust institutional mechanisms to prevent policy extremes**, enabling them to function during surface-level political volatility. The parallel is striking: investors hold contradictory AI anxieties (DP1) yet continue deploying capital; markets absorb historic policy defeats without dislocation. This suggests institutional guardrails (courts, regulatory review, executive constraint) enable market maturity even under extreme sentiment. For AI adoption, this implies that **adoption velocity isn't primarily sentiment-constrained but governance-constrained**—enterprises need institutional mechanisms (contractual clarity, verification frameworks, accountability structures) to function despite capability uncertainty, just as markets function despite policy chaos.
