## Section 1: PRIMARY IDEA ALIGNMENTS (6/18 Active Ideas)

### 🔴 Demand-Side Trust Deficit (Idea #18)
**Criticality:** CORE
**Primary DPs:** DP4, DP5, DP6
**Relevance Strength:** Critical pathway evidence

- **DP4** (58% distrust AI systems): Directly validates the "distrust" component of demand-side deficit. This is not specialized skepticism but broad population-level erosion of confidence.
- **DP5** (54% say companies invest too much): Evidence of trust deficit manifesting as suspicion of corporate intent. Public perceives misalignment between AI investment velocity and actual stakeholder benefit.
- **DP6** (45% expect negative economic effect): Extends distrust from systems → to institutions → to economic outcomes. Three-layer trust collapse.

**Synthesis Implication:** Demand-side trust deficit is not aspirational or lagging. It's current state consensus and politically stable (unchanged since September 2025 per DP7).

---

### 🟡 Tool-Coworker Duality (Idea #1)
**Criticality:** SECONDARY
**Primary DPs:** DP1, DP2, DP3
**Relevance Strength:** Adoption framing evidence

- **DP1** (63% see job reduction): Americans are centering on AI-as-replacement narrative, not tool-augmentation narrative. This is the duality choice point—population has picked the "coworker threat" frame over "tool enabler" frame.
- **DP2** (63% used AI, but only 23% regularly): Reveals the actual tool-coworker duality problem. Most Americans have sampled the tool but not internalized it as coworker. They're not building relational models with AI systems.
- **DP3** (Younger adults more engaged): The generational split in regular usage suggests younger cohorts ARE developing tool-coworker mental models; older cohorts remain in "external tool" category.

**Synthesis Implication:** The tool-coworker duality isn't just about technical design—it's about usage intensity and cognitive investment. DP2 shows the activation gap prevents most Americans from reaching coworker-level interaction.

---

### 🟡 Adoption Bifurcation (Idea #6)
**Criticality:** SECONDARY
**Primary DPs:** DP2, DP3
**Relevance Strength:** Demographic stratification evidence

- **DP2** + **DP3** together: Clear bifurcation between regular users (23%) and casual/non-users (77%). Generational component compounds this. Not a smooth adoption curve; it's a two-tier market emerging.
- **DP3** specifically: "Younger adults more likely" indicates the bifurcation is hardening along age cohorts. This isn't sampling variation; it's structural divergence in adoption readiness/interest.

**Synthesis Implication:** Adoption bifurcation is not hypothetical. It's already present and the gap between regular users and others is wide enough to suggest different economic and social trajectories.

---

### 🟡 Timeline Collision (Idea #8)
**Criticality:** SECONDARY
**Primary DPs:** DP1, DP7
**Relevance Strength:** Stability evidence (indirectly)

- **DP7** (Job displacement views unchanged since September 2025): Critical temporal marker. 6 months have passed with major AI developments, yet public sentiment on job impact is *stable*, not moving. This suggests either cognitive lag (timeline collision) or genuine belief formation.
- **DP1** (63% consensus on job reduction): The stability of this consensus despite acceleration in AI capabilities suggests timeline collision—public perception is not calibrating to capability growth.

**Synthesis Implication:** DP7 is evidence that public timeline expectations are *not* recalibrating. This supports timeline collision hypothesis: real-world AI acceleration is outpacing sentiment change.

---

### 🟠 Trust & Authenticity (Idea #9)
**Criticality:** TERTIARY
**Primary DPs:** DP4, DP5
**Relevance Strength:** Sentiment evidence (generic)

- **DP4** (58% distrust): Trust and authenticity are foundational. The distrust reading captures both lack of trust in AI systems AND underlying authenticity questions (Is this real? Can I rely on it?).
- **DP5** (54% see overcurrent investment): Authenticity component embedded here—public questions whether corporate investment is authentic concern for employees or extractive cost-optimization.

**Synthesis Implication:** Trust & Authenticity is active but secondary here. The DPs validate the idea exists but don't add new dimensional insight.

### 🟠 Verification Clarity (Idea #15)
**Criticality:** TERTIARY
**Primary DPs:** DP4

## Section 6: SYNTHESIS RECOMMENDATIONS

### Primary Value to Research System
1. **Demand-Side Trust Deficit validation** – DP4, DP5, DP6 provide strong empirical support for this core idea. Public distrust is not situational; it's stable consensus.
2. **Trust Architecture Bifurcation evidence** – DP8 reveals that trust solutions cannot be "one-size-fits-all." Political divergence in AI trust is structural.
3. **Tool-Coworker Duality usage data** – DP2 shows most Americans haven't advanced beyond "tool" mental model. The 23% regular-use ceiling suggests cognitive investment barrier.

### Secondary Value
- **Timeline Collision indirect support** – DP7 (unchanged sentiment despite 6-month acceleration) suggests public timelines are *not* compressing.
- **Adoption Bifurcation structure** – DP2 + DP3 show two-tier emergence (regular vs. non-users; younger vs. older).

### Integration Recommendations
- **Pair with technical escalation data** – Contrast YouGov's stable negative sentiment (DP7) with enterprise acceleration data to measure divergence width.
- **Use DP8 for trust architecture exploration** – Follow up with segmented analysis of what different political cohorts need from trust mechanisms.
- **Layer DP2 into skill portability thread** – Why are 77% not regular users? Is it friction, interest, or structural barriers? Informs skill portability strategy.

### Measurement Gaps Identified
- No data on *what would move* public sentiment (DP7 shows it's stable, but no test conditions offered)
- No disaggregation of distrust reasons (DP4 doesn't separate safety concerns from economic anxiety)
- No baseline on what "trust" means functionally to different segments (DP8 shows political split but not reasoning)
