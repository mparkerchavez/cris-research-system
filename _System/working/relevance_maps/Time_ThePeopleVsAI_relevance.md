The relevance map identifies high-risk convergence points where demand-side rejection could cascade through supply-side assumptions.

## Conclusion: Convergence of Demand-Side and Supply-Side Analysis

The Time extraction reveals a **fundamental asymmetry** in CRIS research:

- **Supply-side (Active Ideas):** Focus on solving technical/organizational problems to scale AI deployment. Assumes demand-side friction is manageable.
- **Demand-side (Time extraction):** Focus on fundamental concerns about human formation, labor dignity, infrastructure justice, and political autonomy. These are not friction factors; they are binary rejections.

The most important convergence: **Infrastructure opposition is the material instantiation where demand-side opposition becomes supply-side constraint.**

When communities block data centers, the entire edifice of supply-side optimization (coordination tax solutions, work intensification management, verification clarity) becomes operationally irrelevant. AI companies may solve every technical problem and still find they cannot build the infrastructure to deploy solutions.

This suggests a fundamental research shift: rather than optimizing for deployment in a cooperative environment, CRIS should model deployment in an **adversarial-distributed-resistance environment** where infrastructure, talent, and communities are actively opposing deployment.
