## 1. EMPLOYMENT BIFURCATION BY AGE & AI EXPOSURE

### Core Pattern
Young workers (22-25) in AI-exposed roles experience 6-20% employment declines while older cohorts (35+) gain 6-9%. Effect size: 15 log-point decline in highest AI exposure quintile.

### Connected DPs
- **DP1:** Entry-level workers, AI-exposed roles, 16% relative decline vs. stable experienced workers
- **DP2:** Software developers 22-25: 20% decline from late 2022–Sep 2025; older developers growing
- **DP3:** Age 22-25: 6% decline in most AI-exposed occupations; age 35+: 6-9% increases
- **DP10:** Firm fixed effects show 15 log-point decline for 22-25 year-olds in highest AI exposure quintile

### Idea Connections
- **#2 Coordination Tax:** Bifurcation suggests misalignment between where AI delivers immediate productivity (automating codified junior tasks) and where human judgment still clusters (experienced workers). This is coordination cost manifestation—firms reduce junior hiring because integrating AI + junior workflow is harder than retaining experienced hires.
- **#13 Work Intensification Paradox:** Rather than reducing work, AI redistributes it upward (experienced workers gaining) and outward (juniors priced out). The 15 log-point effect shows AI concentrating work complexity among fewer, more senior people.
- **#6 2026 Adoption Bifurcation:** Clear evidence of binary outcome—those with tacit knowledge buffer disruption; those relying on codified knowledge face displacement. Market splitting in real time.

### Open Thread Relevance
- **OT#2 (Skill Portability Boundaries):** Why can older workers port to AI-augmented roles but juniors cannot? Suggests tacit knowledge threshold for portability.

## 2. AUTOMATIVE VS. AUGMENTATIVE TASK DISTINCTION

### Core Pattern
Employment declines correlate with automative AI uses (71.7% SWE-Bench improvement), while augmentative uses show muted employment impact. This replicates across LinkedIn and UK data independently.

### Connected DPs
- **DP5:** Employment declined in automative AI applications, stable/grew in augmentative uses
- **DP23:** Highest automation shares → sharply declining young worker employment; highest augmentation shares → no similar pattern
- **DP8:** AI capability surge in coding: 4.4% → 71.7% SWE-Bench (2023-2024)
- **DP9:** Current systems match/exceed 47% of professionals on economically valuable benchmarks
- **DP13:** LinkedIn and UK data confirm displacement pattern independently
- **DP22:** Anthropic Economic Index distinguishes automative vs. augmentative based on millions of Claude queries

### Idea Connections
- **#3 Implementation Chasm (6 dimensions, 85% use case desert):** This bifurcation IS the chasm manifest—augmentative use requires behavioral integration, workflow redesign, trust-building; automative use is plug-and-replace. The "85% use case desert" maps to occupations where augmentation hasn't achieved product-market fit while automation has.
- **#5 Design's Value:** The fact that augmentative uses don't kill employment suggests design mediates outcome. Automative uses succeed because they require no human redesign; augmentative uses require deep integration design (and that design work hasn't happened at scale).
- **#10 Observability:** Automative displacement is immediately visible (junior hiring stops); augmentative benefit is hidden in productivity metrics no one tracks. This asymmetry drives policy blindness.

### Open Thread Relevance
- **OT#1 (Measurement Framework):** We have employment data proving automation works but lack productivity data proving augmentation works. This is a measurement gap that distorts adoption signals.

## 3. CODIFIED VS. TACIT KNOWLEDGE SUBSTITUTION

### Core Pattern
AI disproportionately substitutes for codified knowledge (rules, documented processes) vs. tacit knowledge (experience-accumulated judgment). Young workers supply proportionally more codified, exposing them to greater replacement.

### Connected DPs
- **DP14:** AI substitutes preferentially for codified knowledge; less capable against tacit knowledge
- **DP15:** Young workers supply relatively more codified than tacit knowledge → face greater task replacement
- **DP18:** Non-college workers experience less experience buffering; divergence visible through age 40 (suggesting tacit knowledge accumulation takes time)

### Idea Connections
- **#4 Core Capability Endures (bimodal skills, apprenticeship collapse, 17-point comprehension reduction):** This is the mechanism of apprenticeship collapse—you can't transfer codified knowledge (AI does it) but you still need tacit knowledge accumulation. Without codified knowledge work as scaffolding, apprentices have no ramp. The "17-point comprehension reduction" likely reflects this: junior developers learning through reading code (codified) rather than doing it.
- **#11 Data Quality:** Tacit knowledge is invisible in data. We measure employment declines but can't measure tacit knowledge loss. This creates a data quality crisis for training the next generation.

### Open Thread Relevance
- **OT#2 (Skill Portability Boundaries):** Tacit knowledge is non-portable by definition; codified knowledge is portable. This boundary explains why experienced workers can shift roles (they have tacit knowledge across multiple contexts) while juniors cannot (they haven't accumulated tacit).

## 4. LABOR MARKET ADJUSTMENT MECHANICS

### Core Pattern
Markets adjusted via employment quantity reductions, not wage decreases (wage stickiness). Hiring reduction at junior level emerged as lowest-friction adjustment margin due to wage ladder structures and mobility concerns.

### Connected DPs
- **DP4:** Adjustments via employment rather than compensation; concentrated in automative tasks
- **DP6:** Minimal salary variation by age/exposure quintile despite large employment divergences
- **DP16:** Reduced junior hiring = lowest-friction margin; wage ladder locks in, mobility risk discourages training investment

### Idea Connections
- **#13 Work Intensification Paradox:** Wage stickiness (DP6) reveals the paradox mechanism: total work redistributes upward (experienced workers) without corresponding wage adjustment, intensifying senior workloads while juniors price into unemployment. The market can't clear via wage because wage is institutionally sticky (ladder, union floors, etc.).
- **#2 Coordination Tax:** Wage stickiness creates coordination cost. Firms would happily hire juniors at lower wages, but institutional structures prevent it. This is coordination tax in its purest form—inability to reorganize labor contractual structures fast enough.
- **#6 2026 Adoption Bifurcation:** Bifurcation isn't just capability-driven; it's institutionally locked via wage structures. Those with power (experienced workers, senior roles) maintain terms; those without (juniors) exit market.

### Open Thread Relevance
- **OT#3 (Work Intensification Governance):** Wage stickiness + junior hiring cuts = uncontrolled work intensification at senior levels. System has no governors on senior workload growth.

## 5. TEMPORAL COMPRESSION & MARKET SPEED

### Core Pattern
AI employment effects emerged sharply late 2022/early 2023 (ChatGPT proliferation), not gradually. Coding capability surge (4.4% → 71.7%) compressed into 12 months. This suggests technological adoption speed far exceeds institutional adjustment speed.

### Connected DPs
- **DP12:** Effects sharply visible starting late 2022/early 2023 (ChatGPT timing)
- **DP8:** SWE-Bench capability surge: 4.4% (2023) → 71.7% (2024) = 16x improvement in ~12 months
- **DP24:** Historical technology adoption creates heterogeneous effects requiring "adjustment periods"; this is the tension—AI adjustment period compressing faster than expected

### Idea Connections
- **#8 Timeline Collision:** Direct evidence of collision. Technological capability jumped 16x in one year; institutional capability (hiring practices, skill development, career ladders) changes in 5-10 year cycles. Collision point is late 2022.
- **#7 Agent-Native Dev:** Speed suggests native integration into development workflow (agents/Copilot in IDE) matters more than training. If integration is native (you get the tool), adoption is immediate; if adoption requires retraining, adjustment period extends.
- **#12 Infrastructure-App Divergence:** Capability jumped (app layer); employment structure didn't adjust (infrastructure/HR layer). This divergence is visible in the lag: capability surge in 2023, employment effects sharp in 2024-2025.

## 6. SCOPE & GENERALIZATION EVIDENCE

### Core Pattern
Effects robust across: firm-size heterogeneity, college vs. non-college occupations, gender, technology sector vs. non-tech, telework vs. non-telework. Independent replication (LinkedIn, UK data).

### Connected DPs
- **DP11:** Patterns hold across teleworkable and non-teleworkable occupations
- **DP17:** Hold across high/low college-share occupations (rules out COVID-19 education hypothesis)
- **DP19:** Results robust excluding tech occupations and IT firms; effects span sectors
- **DP21:** Patterns consistent across gender
- **DP13:** LinkedIn and UK data independently confirm findings
- **DP20:** Interest rate exposure uncorrelated with AI exposure (rules out macro shock explanation)
- **DP25:** Measurement gap: need firm-level AI adoption data to sharpen causal inference

### Idea Connections
- **#15 Verification Clarity:** Strong independent verification (LinkedIn, UK, multiple controls) gives confidence that this is real, not artifact. Clear verification accelerates disruption because it removes ambiguity—firms can confidently move on junior hiring reduction.
- **#1 Tool-Coworker Duality:** Sector-agnostic effect suggests the tool (AI in IDE, Claude, etc.) is genuinely coworker-like across domains, not domain-specific. If it only worked in tech, we'd see confined effects.

### Open Thread Relevance
- **OT#1 (Measurement Framework):** Evidence is strong on employment (negative effect) but completely absent on productivity (augmentative benefit). We measure what we can see; we're blind to augmentation value. This measurement asymmetry drives policy failure.

## 7. EQUITY & DISTRIBUTIONAL EFFECTS

### Core Pattern
Disruption is regressive: entry-level workers (who typically have fewer resources) lose employment access; experienced workers (who have accumulated resources, networks, knowledge) gain. Non-college workers experience weaker experience buffering than college-educated workers.

### Connected DPs
- **DP15:** Young workers supply more codified knowledge → greater replacement exposure
- **DP18:** Non-college workers show weaker experience buffering; divergence visible through age 40 vs. college cohorts
- **DP21:** Effects consistent across gender (not gendered, broadly distributed)

### Idea Connections
- **#16 Cost Accessibility Stratification:** Labor market now stratifies by tacit knowledge accumulation time (experience) and education-level. Junior entry-level positions have effectively increased tacit knowledge requirement (because codified pathway closed). Non-college workers face longer buffering period (age 40) vs. college workers. This is cost stratification: only those with existing capital (education, networks, prior experience) can absorb cost of entry.
- **#13 Work Intensification Paradox:** Paradox hits hardest at low-capital workers. High-capital (experienced, educated) workers get intensified work but can absorb it; low-capital workers get priced out entirely.

## 8. RESEARCH GAPS & GOVERNANCE IMPLICATIONS

### Core Pattern
Firm-level AI adoption data lacking; employment effects clear but causality underspecified. Productivity measurement framework entirely absent (DP22 methodology exists for automative/augmentative distinction but no measurement of actual productivity impact from augmentative uses).

### Connected DPs
- **DP25:** Future work needs firm-level AI adoption data for causal identification
- **DP22:** Anthropic Economic Index methodology exists (distinguishes automative vs. augmentative)
- **DP4, DP6:** Employment and wage data available; productivity metrics missing

### Idea Connections
- **#11 Data Quality:** Data quality crisis manifests as absent data. We have: employment (binary, observed), wages (sticky, not capturing value), productivity (unmeasured, unknown). The system is flying blind on augmentative value creation.
- **#14 Orchestration Infrastructure:** Current infrastructure measures employment and payroll; it cannot measure task orchestration value (whether teams with AI work better, faster, with higher quality). Without that measurement, orchestration remains invisible to decision-makers.
- **#9 Observability:** Automative effects are visible (employment stops); augmentative effects are invisible (productivity unmeasured). This asymmetric visibility drives all distorted incentives.

### Open Thread Relevance
- **OT#1 (Measurement Framework):** This gap IS the open thread. Without productivity measurement for augmentative use, we cannot distinguish real from imagined value; policy builds on fiction.
- **OT#3 (Work Intensification Governance):** Without observability of work intensity changes, no governance framework can constrain senior workload growth.

## KEY INSIGHTS FOR ACTIVE IDEAS REFINEMENT

### Emergent Patterns
1. **Measurement is destiny:** The system optimizes what it can see. Employment decline (visible) drives junior hiring cuts; productivity gains (invisible) drive nothing.
2. **Institutions lock outcomes:** Wage stickiness and hiring ladder create coordination tax. Technology can move 16x faster than institutions can restructure.
3. **Bifurcation is real:** Not imagined. 15 log-point effect size shows this is structural, not noise.

### Ideas That Gain Specificity
- **#3 Implementation Chasm:** Chasm is real: automative is in use, augmentative is not. 85% use case desert is validated.
- **#8 Timeline Collision:** Validated empirically. Capability improved 16x in 12 months; disruption effects visible within 24 months.
- **#15 Verification Clarity:** Strong independent verification removes ambiguity, which accelerates market action (junior hiring cuts confirmed by multiple sources = confident firm response).

### Ideas Needing Deeper Investigation
- **#13 Work Intensification Paradox:** Confirmed in employment data (older workers gaining despite no wage increase = intensification). But actual workload intensity metrics are absent—need separate measurement.
- **#11 Data Quality:** Crisis confirmed. Tacit knowledge loss, productivity changes, work intensity changes are all invisible in available data.
