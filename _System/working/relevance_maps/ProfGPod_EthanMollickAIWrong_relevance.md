## 6. Critical Patterns

### Pattern 1: Measurement as Governance Forcing Function
The most consequential insight across this extraction is the **measurement vacuum creating governance necessity**. Mollick opens with "nobody knows what's going on"—not as an observation but as a statement that forces organizational choice.

The specific mechanism: Individual productivity gains (DP1, DP21: 40-50% documented) ARE happening but remain invisible to organizational systems. When a company measures (early adopter: 33% more publications, DP21), it bifurcates (DP13): some companies optimize for expansion (Walmart: keep workforce, expand capability), others for cost-cutting (Amazon: reduce headcount). Mollick's framing of valuations (DP12) as "unsustainable without 50% cuts or massive labor gains" suggests that measurement framework CHOICE determines trajectory, not vice versa.

This validates the "Measurement Framework Emergence" open thread but with specificity: measurement isn't emerging—it's FORCING governance choices that are currently obscured by "no playbook" language. The real choice is not "how do we measure" but "what do we measure to justify our trajectory."

### Pattern 2: Infrastructure Constraints Are Redefining Competitive Advantage
Mollick's statement that "data no longer bottleneck" (DP11) + "energy is the big one" (DP11) + "5+ year datacenter timelines" (DP11) represents a major inflection in competitive framing. For the 2020-2024 period, advantage accrued to companies with training data access and compute budgets. Post-2024 (and visible in this Feb 2026 assessment), advantage accrues to companies with energy infrastructure access.

This reshapes multiple active ideas. The "Infrastructure-Application Divergence (#12)" becomes more acute: hyperscalers control energy/grid access (5+ year lag), so application-layer companies cannot compete on model capability alone. This is why "open-weights dumping" (DP10) works: Chinese state backing absorbs zero-revenue loss that commercial app-layer companies cannot. Scaling laws (DP7) don't just favor large models—they trap competitors in energy-dependent races where geography and grid access matter more than algorithmic innovation.

### Pattern 3: Apprenticeship Collapse as Mechanism for Work Intensification Redistribution
The most mechanistically clear pattern in the extraction is DP15 (apprenticeship collapse) as the **redistribution engine** for work intensification (DP13). Mollick explicitly describes the loop: interns previously learned via assigned task work; AI now allows managers to have interns produce AI-assisted output (appearance of learning) without actual skill transfer. This forces middle managers to choose: either teach manually (time-intensive) or use AI and accept skill loss downstream.

This is work intensification not because managers do more work—but because the *type* of work shifts from "delegate to teach" to "execute directly" (no delegation option remains if interns cannot learn). Combined with DP25 (careers require exploration, not planning), this suggests the workforce is experiencing a **simultaneity crisis**: entry-level cannot learn via traditional apprenticeship; mid-career must execute directly without training talent; late-career must remain flexible because planning fails.

### Pattern 4: Trust as Governance Bifurcation
Model differentiation (DP9: Anthropic for ethics/writing, OpenAI for balance, Gemini for "neurotic behavior") combined with open-weights strategy (DP10: free, state-backed) suggests emerging **two-tier trust architecture**: proprietary-differentiated (Anthropic, OpenAI claiming distinct personalities) vs open-source-distributed (Chinese models claiming accessibility).

This isn't just vendor strategy—it's governance signaling. Anthropic's explicit "ethics + writing" positioning = Constitutional governance claim. OpenAI's "balanced" = commercial-neutral claim. Chinese dumping = state-backed accessibility claim. These are distinct trust models, not just product differentiation. The "Trust Multiplier" active idea (#9) is validated but with complexity: trust is NOT binary multiplier on model capability. Rather, trust is binary multiplier on *governance model selection*, and governance model determines which use cases are viable.
