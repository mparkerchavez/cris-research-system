## Key Patterns

### Measurement Framework Emergence as Explicit Gap
The report directly validates Thread 1: "78% acknowledge traditional metrics insufficient." Nielsen Norman explicitly calls for new frameworks in four dimensions:
1. **AI-to-AI performance** — undefined metrics space
2. **Human-AI collaboration** — team productivity, confidence
3. **Data quality for AI** — representativeness, bias, freshness, structuring
4. **Human trust/control** — multidimensional, distinct from satisfaction metrics

This represents a framework-level rebuild, not incremental metric addition.

### Trust as Multidimensional Construct
The report articulates trust as having at least three distinct dimensions:
- **Comfort with task delegation** — willingness to hand off work
- **Confidence in decision quality** — belief outputs are correct
- **Sense of control** — ability to understand/override decisions
- **Override capability** — practical ability to reverse agent decisions

Current measurement frameworks (NPS, CSAT) cannot capture this structure.

### Data Quality Elevation to Service Metric
Data representativeness, bias, freshness, and structuring move from technical infrastructure concern to customer-facing service quality metric. Organizations must measure and optimize these for AI service quality.

### Control Perception Independence from Actual Control
The distinction between "sense of control" and actual control capability suggests design of *perceived* control (transparency interfaces, override communications) becomes valuable independent of mechanical override capability. This validates design value thesis.

### Employee vs Customer Trust Bifurcation
Internal (employee) trust differs from external (customer) trust; organizations need separate measurement frameworks for each. This creates new measurement infrastructure requirements.

---

## Critical Insight

The report's core assertion—"The technology may not be ready and I don't have all the answers"—signals that measurement framework definition is not yet mature. This creates competitive opportunity for organizations that develop proprietary measurement frameworks ahead of industry standards.

The explicitly undefined nature of AI-to-AI metrics is particularly significant: organizations deploying multi-agent systems do so without frameworks to measure inter-agent collaboration efficiency. This is the observability gap made concrete.
