## Synthesis Notes

This source provides the **incumbent firm transformation case study**—McKinsey's explicit strategy to survive AI by:

1. **Workforce composition shift** (40K+20K agents by 18 months) validating agent-worker paradigm
2. **Pricing model migration** (33% outcomes-based already, targeting majority) away from per-seat advisory
3. **Hiring profile overhaul** (resilience/teamwork/aptitude over credentials) to select for adaptability not specialization
4. **Governance investment** (~$1B) recognizing that culture/ethos cannot scale without explicit controls
5. **Org redesign** (flattening, consolidation) as 50%+ of value realization
6. **Client risk framework** (multi-dimensional) validating observability requirements
7. **Value chain repositioning** (commoditized work to clients; firm moves to strategy + leadership + outcomes)

Critical distinction from SaaS cases: McKinsey survives because:
- Advisory model allows natural value-chain progression (commoditized execution → strategy → outcomes)
- Partnership structure avoids quarterly pressure enabling long-term transformation
- Leadership/judgment/aspiration are core deliverables, not add-ons
- Can pivot pricing from per-seat to outcomes without cannibalizing existing business

The key risk: McKinsey's transformation assumes continued demand for strategic transformation work across economic cycles. If macroeconomic contraction occurs, board-level appetite for McKinsey-style engagement collapses faster than their transformation timeline. The $2T Big Five AI investment (from prior source) could also make proprietary AI capability more valuable than consulting advice, creating new form of competition.

The organizational change insight is crucial: McKinsey discovered that >50% of transformation value comes from org redesign, not technology. This validates that multi-dimensional implementation chasm is real; technology is necessary but insufficient condition.
