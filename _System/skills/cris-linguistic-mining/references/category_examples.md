## 1. FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

### Example 1: Tool-Coworker Duality (BCG)

**Structure:** Agentic AI simultaneously exhibits tool properties (predictable, scalable, owned like assets) AND worker properties (adaptive, judgment-based, requiring oversight like employees)

**Application:** Explaining why traditional management frameworks fail; organizations forcing AI into one category create friction

**Evidence:** 76% of executives view AI as coworker, not tool; organizational tension data [BCG_EmergingAgenticEnterprise DP2]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/BCG_EmergingAgenticEnterprise_2026-02-04.md;dp=2 -->

**Why It Works:** Names the fundamental category confusion causing adoption challenges

**Related:** Four Operational Tensions

---

### Example 2: Four Operational Tensions (BCG)

**Structure:** Four unavoidable tensions when integrating agentic AI:
1. **Scalability vs Adaptability** - Scale like tools OR adapt like workers (incompatible optimization targets)
2. **Supervision vs Autonomy** - More autonomy requires more supervision (paradox)
3. **Experience vs Expediency** - Fast deployment conflicts with learning/refinement
4. **Retrofit vs Reengineer** - Embed in existing workflows vs rebuild from scratch

**Application:** Diagnosing organizational friction, explaining why adoption is hard

**Evidence:** Cross-organizational survey identifying these tensions regardless of industry [BCG_EmergingAgenticEnterprise DP5]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/BCG_EmergingAgenticEnterprise_2026-02-04.md;dp=5 -->

**Why It Works:** Specific, memorable, actionable (can map org challenges to tensions)

**Usage Note:** Don't present as solvable—frame as "embrace, don't resolve"

---

### Why These Work:
- **Named** (not just "AI creates tensions")
- **Structured** (clear components, relationships)
- **Memorable** (can recall and explain after one read)
- **Actionable** (can apply to diagnose real situations)
- **Evidence-backed** (research or operational proof)

### Non-Examples (DON'T Capture):
- "Agile methodology" (generic, everyone knows)
- Unnamed conceptual structure without clear name
- Overly complex framework requiring extensive training

## 2. ANALOGIES & METAPHORS

### Example 1: Coordination Tax (DeepMind)

**Core Comparison:** Cost agents pay when coordinating vs working independently (economic tax metaphor)

**Usage Context:** Explaining multi-agent inefficiency to business audiences

**Effectiveness:** Very High - tax metaphor intuitive, economically framed

**Transfer Risk:** Low - economic framing universally understood

**When to Use:** CFO conversations, architecture decisions, explaining why "agent swarms" fail

**Quantification:** 200-300% optimal overhead, >400% over-coordination

**Evidence:** [Google_DeepMind_ScienceOfScalingAgents DP8]

### Example 2: Use Case Desert (Section)

**Core Comparison:** 85% of workforce lacks value-driving use cases despite widespread tool access (empty landscape metaphor)

**Usage Context:** Describing proficiency crisis vividly

**Effectiveness:** Very High - memorable, surprising, visual

**Transfer Risk:** Low - self-explanatory metaphor

**When to Use:** Any discussion of enterprise AI struggles; immediately communicates the problem

**Quantification:** 85% lack value-driving use cases; only 15% likely to generate ROI

**Evidence:** [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP12]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026_2026-02-04.md;dp=12 -->

---

### Why These Work:
- **Concrete imagery** (tax, desert) makes abstract concept tangible
- **Familiar reference point** (everyone knows what tax/desert means)
- **Emotionally resonant** (tax = burden, desert = emptiness)
- **Quantifiable** (can attach specific numbers)
- **Transferable** (works across contexts)

### Non-Examples (DON'T Capture):
- "AI is a tool in your toolbox" (cliché, doesn't clarify)
- Throwaway metaphor used once without development
- Analogy requiring 3 paragraphs to explain

**Definition:** Non-engineers coding through feel/iteration with AI assistance (49.5% of non-technical professionals report coding work)

**Usage Context:** Describing democratization of coding capability beyond traditional engineers

**Adoption Level:** Widely recognized (mainstream media coverage)

**Competitor Terms:** "AI-assisted development", "no-code/low-code" (but less distinctive)

**Caution:** May sound informal for executive audiences; use "AI-assisted development" if needed

**Adoption Level:** Emerging (research community)

**Related:** The 45% Threshold, Coordination Tax

## 4. COMPARISON STRUCTURES

### Example 1: Tool-Heavy vs Knowledge-Heavy Tasks (DeepMind)

**Distinction:** Tool-heavy tasks (requiring external APIs, databases, calculators) suffer 2-6x efficiency penalties from multi-agent coordination (β=-0.267); knowledge-heavy tasks show less penalty

**Strategic Implication:** Agent architecture should vary by task type; tool-heavy domains stay single-agent longer

**Data Support:** Quantified interaction effect from empirical study [Google_DeepMind_ScienceOfScalingAgents DP11]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Google_DeepMind_ScienceOfScalingAgents_2026-02-04.md;dp=11 -->

**When to Use:** Architecture planning, task decomposition strategy

---

### Example 2: Interactive vs Autonomous Performance (Anthropic)

**Distinction:** Interactive performance (ChatGPT makes me feel productive) doesn't predict autonomous deployment success (Claude can run this process unattended)

**Strategic Implication:** Benchmarks optimized for chat UX don't measure autonomous capability; ROI expectations based on interactive experience will disappoint

**Data Support:** METR scaffolds show no autonomous advantage despite interactive usability [METR_TimeHorizon DP5]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/METR_TimeHorizon_2026-02-04.md;dp=5 -->

**When to Use:** Explaining why pilots feel successful but deployments fail

---

### Why These Work:
- **Clear A vs B structure** (not vague "there are differences")
- **Strategic implication** (explains why this matters)
- **Data-backed** (not opinion)
- **Actionable** (can use to make decisions)

### Non-Examples (DON'T Capture):
- "AI vs humans" (too obvious)
- "Fast vs slow" without meaningful insight
- Comparison without strategic implication

## 6. ANTI-PATTERNS / WARNINGS

### Example 1: Don't Add Agents After 45% Threshold (DeepMind)

**The Mistake:** Assuming more agents improve performance once single-agent capability is "good enough"

**Why It Fails:** Coordination tax dominates gains; capability saturation means negative returns

**What to Do Instead:** Improve single-agent performance OR verify task is genuinely decomposable before adding agents

**Evidence:** Empirical threshold from cross-validated model [Google_DeepMind_ScienceOfScalingAgents DP1]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Google_DeepMind_ScienceOfScalingAgents_2026-02-04.md;dp=1 -->

---

### Example 2: Don't Measure Only Time Savings at Scale (AI Daily)

**The Mistake:** Using initial "time saved" metrics to project long-term ROI

**Why It Fails:** Time savings collapse from 77% to 20% as usage intensifies; value shifts to output multiplication

**What to Do Instead:** Track output quantity/quality, new capabilities unlocked, task complexity handled

**Evidence:** Survey data showing inverse relationship between usage intensity and time-savings emphasis [AIDaily_TimeSavingsEraOverVibeCoders DP7]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/AIDaily_TimeSavingsEraOverVibeCoders_2026-02-14.md;dp=7 -->

---

### Why These Work:
- **Specific mistake** (not vague "don't do bad things")
- **Explains why it fails** (mechanism of failure)
- **Actionable alternative** (tells you what to do instead)
- **Evidence-backed** (research or operational proof)

### Non-Examples (DON'T Capture):
- "Don't ignore AI" (too obvious)
- Warning without alternative
- Generic advice everyone gives

## 7. NUMERICAL RULES-OF-THUMB

### Example 1: The 45% Threshold (DeepMind)

**The Rule:** Once single-agent baseline exceeds ~45% accuracy, coordination yields diminishing or negative returns

**What It Means:** Capability ceiling for multi-agent value; above this, don't add agents

**When to Quote:** Architecture decisions, optimization conversations, debunking "agent swarms"

**Precision:** Use ~45% or "approximately 45%" (empirically derived threshold)

**Conversation Hook:** "Your single-agent hit 50%? Don't add agents—you're past the 45% threshold where coordination turns negative."

**Evidence:** [Google_DeepMind_ScienceOfScalingAgents DP1]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Google_DeepMind_ScienceOfScalingAgents_2026-02-04.md;dp=1 -->

---

### Example 2: The 77-to-20 Collapse (AI Daily)

**The Rule:** Time savings as primary benefit drops from 77% (initial adopters) to 20% (10+ hours/week users)

**What It Means:** Early productivity gains don't sustain; value proposition evolves with skill

**When to Quote:** Explaining why pilot ROI doesn't predict scaled deployment outcomes

**Precision:** Use exact numbers (77% → 20%) for credibility

**Conversation Hook:** "Everyone's measuring time savings. That metric collapsed from 77% to 20% for power users. They're not saving time—they're doing 10x more work."

**Evidence:** [AIDaily_TimeSavingsEraOverVibeCoders DP7]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/AIDaily_TimeSavingsEraOverVibeCoders_2026-02-14.md;dp=7 -->

---

### Why These Work:
- **Memorable number** (sticks in conversation)
- **Quotable** (can use as verbal shorthand)
- **Specific threshold** (not vague range)
- **Interpretation provided** (explains what it means)
- **Conversation hook** (know how to deploy it)

### Non-Examples (DON'T Capture):
- "AI adoption is growing" (vague, no specific number)
- One-off statistic not reusable as rule
- Number without interpretation

### EXCLUDE Examples:

❌ "Synergy between AI and humans" - platitude, not distinctive
❌ "Industry-specific jargon" - domain-locked, not transferable

## Extraction Best Practices

1. **Read complete extraction** - Don't skim; context matters
2. **Look for named concepts** - Must have explicit name or memorable phrase
3. **Verify evidence** - Every asset needs citation
4. **Apply quality filters** - Better 5 excellent assets than 15 mediocre
5. **Check distinctiveness** - Is this truly unique or just rephrasing common knowledge?
6. **Test memorability** - Will you remember this tomorrow?
7. **Assess transferability** - Does it work across contexts?
