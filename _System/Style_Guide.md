# CRIS Style Guide: Tone of Voice and Writing Standards

**Applies to:** All CRIS outputs (Weekly Learnings, Talking Points, Current Synthesis, Active Ideas, User Observations, Deliverables)

**Last Updated:** February 7, 2026

---

## Audience and Purpose

CRIS documents serve two modes, and every document leans toward one or the other.

**Processing mode** is for internal sense-making. You have already consumed the source material. The writing helps you connect dots, recall impactful insights, and gather your thoughts before communicating outward. Processing-mode documents are your thinking tool.

**Communication mode** is for client-facing credibility. The writing demonstrates how you process information and form positions. Communication-mode documents are how your internal thinking becomes a public-facing idea.

| Document | Primary Mode | What It Optimizes For |
|----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Weekly Learnings | Processing | Connecting dots, recalling insights, capturing how your thinking shifted this week |
| User Observations | Processing | Recording personal pattern recognition grounded in experience |
| Active Ideas | Processing | Tracking signal strength on potential trends, maintaining an inventory of what you are watching |
| Current Synthesis | Communication | Turning internal thinking into a clear, reader-facing position statement |
| Talking Points | Communication | Preparing narratives for live conversation with authority and clarity |
| Monthly/Quarterly Synthesis | Communication | Laddering weekly snapshots into a durable, trend-level narrative |

**The relationship between the two modes:** Processing-mode documents feed communication-mode documents. Weekly Learnings capture a snapshot of understanding. Current Synthesis distills those snapshots into a position. Talking Points make that position conversational. The quality of the communication depends on the honesty and clarity of the processing.

---

## Core Voice

CRIS writing is the voice of a **practitioner-strategist**: someone who has done the work, read the research, and formed a clear position. The tone is confident without being arrogant, direct without being blunt, and precise without being academic.

**Three words that define the tone:** Clear. Grounded. Actionable.

## Narrative Over Data

Data points serve the narrative. The narrative does not serve the data points.

This is the most important principle in the style guide. When writing any CRIS synthesis, the goal is to build a clear narrative that makes a clear point. Data points play two supporting roles:

1. **Credibility:** They give the reader (or listener) reason to trust the narrative.
2. **Navigation:** They point toward sources that expand on the ideas, trends, and narrative being communicated.

Data points are not the substance of the argument. The narrative is the substance. A paragraph with a clear insight and one well-placed statistic is stronger than a paragraph with four statistics and no insight.

### What This Means in Practice

**Build individual trend narratives, not collective narratives.** The AI space moves fast, and the noise-to-signal ratio is high. Do not try to weave everything into one grand story. Instead, build separate, clear narratives around individual trends. Each narrative should have its own momentum: what is happening, why it matters, where the signals point, and what is driving the shift.

**One claim, one data point, one sentence.** When a data point supports a claim, pair them directly. Do not stack multiple statistics into a single sentence. Do not introduce a claim and then follow it with a barrage of numbers. Let each data point breathe.

**Avoid these patterns:**

| Pattern | Problem | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Compound claims | Two or three ideas compressed into one sentence. The reader cannot tell which part is evidence and which is interpretation. | Split into separate sentences. State the claim first, then the evidence. |
| Concept-stacking | A sentence introduces a new framework, applies it, and draws a conclusion all at once. | Introduce the concept in one sentence. Apply it in the next. Draw the conclusion in a third. |
| Numeric density | Three or four statistics appear in rapid succession without breathing room. | Choose the single most important statistic. Use it. Move on. Add supporting data only if the reader needs it to believe the claim. |

**Before and after examples (drawn from actual CRIS outputs):**

Compound claim (before):
> "Two colliding timelines define this moment: markets are repricing software based on 2026 capabilities (Opus 4.6, Codex 5.3, agent autonomy) at timescales measured in months, while enterprise adoption operates on 3-5 year cycles."

Fixed (after):
> Markets are repricing software on a timeline measured in months. Opus 4.6, Codex 5.3, and emerging agent autonomy have reshaped expectations since December 2025. Enterprise adoption, however, operates on three to five year cycles. These two timelines are colliding.

Numeric density (before):
> "85% of workers lack value-driving AI use cases; 26% can't identify any work-related use case at all. Meanwhile, 75% report using AI tools, but only 2.7% reach practitioner-level proficiency, and training only moves people from 28% to 70% (novice to experimenter), leaving a massive gap."

Fixed (after):
> The most striking finding is the gap between tool access and meaningful use. Seventy-five percent of knowledge workers report using AI tools (Section, January 2026), yet 85% of them cannot identify a value-driving use case for their work. Training helps, but only to a point: it moves people from novice to experimenter, not from experimenter to practitioner. Fewer than 3% of users reach practitioner-level proficiency.

## Writing for Your Future Self

CRIS documents ladder over time: weekly to monthly, monthly to quarterly, quarterly to yearly. This means every Weekly Learnings entry is not just a snapshot of what you learned. It is a breadcrumb in the trail of how your thinking evolved.

### In Weekly Learnings: Mark What Changed

Each Weekly Learnings entry should make it easy for future-you to see:

- **What is new this week.** Which insights, connections, or signals appeared for the first time?
- **What shifted.** Did any existing ideas change direction based on new evidence? Did something you believed last week get challenged?
- **What held steady.** Which trends continued to accumulate supporting evidence without changing direction?

This does not require a mechanical changelog. It can be woven into the synthesis naturally: "Last week, the coordination cost story was primarily about technical overhead. This week, three sources reframed it as an organizational design problem." That sentence tells future-you exactly how the idea moved.

### Across the Compression Chain: Traceable Lineage

As ideas ladder from weekly to monthly to quarterly, each level should reference the layer below it. A Monthly Synthesis cites the Weekly Learnings that fed it. A Quarterly Synthesis cites the Monthlies. This creates a navigable chain: future-you can start at the latest synthesis and trace backward to see where an idea originated and how it evolved. See `_System/Data_Point_Normalization.md` for detailed lineage conventions.

## Punctuation Rules

### Em Dashes: Never Use

Do not use em dashes (\u2014) or en dashes (\u2013) as punctuation in any CRIS output.

**Instead, use:**

- A period and a new sentence (for emphasis or contrast)
- A colon (for explanation or elaboration)
- Parentheses (for brief asides)
- A comma (for natural pauses in flow)

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| The gap is not a problem to solve\u2014it's the terrain to navigate. | The gap is not a problem to solve. It is the terrain to navigate. |
| This is not theoretical\u2014it's production capability. | This is not theoretical: it is production capability. |
| Organizations over-invest in Technology and Data\u2014while neglecting People and Process. | Organizations over-invest in Technology and Data, while neglecting People and Process. |
| Enterprise AI has shifted to organizational readiness \u2014 across 62 sources. | Enterprise AI has shifted to organizational readiness (across 62 sources). |

### Oxford Comma: Always Use

In any list of three or more items, always include the comma before "and" or "or."

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| Technology, People and Process | Technology, People, and Process |
| CTOs, HR Leaders and Boards | CTOs, HR Leaders, and Boards |
| reading, synthesizing and writing | reading, synthesizing, and writing |

### Other Punctuation Standards

- **Colons:** Use to introduce explanations, lists, or elaborations. Capitalize the first word after a colon only if what follows is a complete sentence.
- **Semicolons:** Use sparingly to connect closely related independent clauses. Prefer two sentences when in doubt.
- **Parentheses:** Use for brief clarifications, source context, or measurement units. Keep parenthetical content under 15 words.
- **Quotation marks:** Use only for direct quotes from sources or to introduce a term for the first time. Do not use for emphasis (use bold instead).
- **Ellipses:** Do not use in CRIS outputs. They introduce ambiguity.
- **Exclamation marks:** Do not use in CRIS outputs. The content should carry its own weight.

## Sentence and Paragraph Standards

### Sentence Construction

- **Lead with the claim, follow with evidence.** Do not bury the insight at the end of the sentence.
- **Active voice by default.** Use passive voice only when the actor is unknown or irrelevant.
- **One idea per sentence.** If a sentence needs a semicolon or a dash to hold together, split it.
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Use short sentences (5-10 words) for emphasis after longer analytical sentences. This creates rhythm.

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| It was found that 85% of workers lack value-driving AI use cases. | Eighty-five percent of workers lack value-driving AI use cases. |
| There is interesting data that suggests coordination costs may increase. | Coordination costs increase as agent count rises. |
| What's worth noting is that the skills transfer rate is actually quite high. | Seventy percent of current skills transfer across automation boundaries. |

### Paragraph Construction

- **Open with position, close with implication.** Each paragraph should state what you believe and end with why it matters.
- **Limit paragraphs to 3-5 sentences** in synthesis documents. Talking Points sections may use shorter paragraphs.
- **No orphan sentences.** If a paragraph has only one sentence, either expand it or merge it with an adjacent paragraph.

### Hedging Language

Minimize hedging. When you have evidence, state the position directly.

| Hedged (avoid) | Direct (prefer) |
|----------------|-----------------|
| This may suggest that... | The evidence indicates... |
| It seems like enterprises might be... | Enterprises are... |
| It's possible that the gap could widen. | The gap is widening. |
| This is arguably the most important factor. | This is the most important factor. |

**When to hedge:** Use measured language ("the evidence suggests," "early data indicates") only when evidence is genuinely thin (fewer than two supporting sources) or when the claim involves a prediction about the future.

## Formatting Standards

### Bold Text

Use bold for:

- **Concept names** on first introduction ("the **use case desert** persists")
- **Key metrics** in evidence sections ("**85% lack value-driving use cases**")
- **Section labels** within structured entries (e.g., "**Key stat:**")

Do not use bold for:

- General emphasis in prose (rewrite the sentence to be clearer instead)
- Entire sentences or phrases longer than 8 words

### Headers

- Use headers to organize, not to decorate. Every header should help a reader scanning the document find what they need.
- Weekly Learnings: organize by tag (### #tag-name)
- Talking Points: organize by topic (## Talking Point N: Topic)
- Current Synthesis: organize by insight (## Insight Title)
- Active Ideas: organize by idea (## Idea N: Title)

### Lists vs. Prose

- **Use lists for:** Evidence chains, source tables, quick reference cards, and structured entries with labeled fields.
- **Use prose for:** Synthesis, analysis, position statements, reflections, and any content where the reader needs to follow a line of reasoning.
- **Never use a list when the items need connective logic.** If the reader needs to understand how items relate to each other, write a paragraph.

## Vocabulary Standards

### Preferred Terms

| Instead of | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| leverage (as a verb) | use, apply, build on |
| utilize | use |
| in order to | to |
| at this point in time | now, currently |
| going forward | from here, next |
| it's important to note that | (delete and state the point directly) |
| synergy | (be specific about what connects) |
| best practices | effective patterns, proven approaches |
| game-changing | (be specific about what changed) |

### Jargon Policy

- **Define on first use** in any document. Example: "agent-native architecture (structuring project documentation so AI agents understand purpose, constraints, and context)"
- **After first use,** the term can appear without definition within the same document.
- **Across documents,** assume the reader knows terms defined in Active Ideas and Current Synthesis. Weekly Learnings and Talking Points should still define terms on first use since they may be read standalone.

---

## Contractions

- **Talking Points:** Contractions are acceptable ("don't," "isn't," "here's") since these are conversational in nature.
- **Weekly Learnings, Current Synthesis, Active Ideas:** Avoid contractions. Use the full form ("do not," "is not," "here is").
- **User Observations:** Contractions are acceptable since these capture personal voice.

## Numbers and Metrics

See also: `_System/Data_Point_Normalization.md` for detailed rules on presenting and normalizing data from multiple sources.

### General Number Rules

- **Spell out numbers one through nine** in prose. Use digits for 10 and above.
- **Always use digits for:** percentages (5%), dollar amounts ($4K), measurements, and data points.
- **Use digits in tables and evidence sections** regardless of size.
- **Percentage points vs. percentages:** Always write "percentage points" when describing the difference between two percentages. Never abbreviate to "points" or "pp."
  - Wrong: "a 53-point gap"
  - Right: "a 53 percentage point gap (81% vs. 28%)"

### Metric Presentation

- **Always include the comparison frame.** A number without context is noise.
  - Wrong: "85% lack use cases"
  - Right: "85% of knowledge workers lack value-driving AI use cases (Section AI Proficiency Report, January 2026)"
- **Anchor to a reference.** When citing a percentage, state who was measured and when.
- **Round consistently.** If one source says 74.6% and another says 75%, use the same precision level when presenting them together. Prefer whole numbers in synthesis unless decimal precision matters.

## Document-Specific Tone Notes

### Weekly Learnings

- **Mode:** Processing.
- **Tone:** Analytical, synthesizing. You are connecting dots across sources and capturing how your understanding shifted this week.
- **Person:** Third-person analysis by default. First-person ("I noticed," "my read is") only in the Reflection section.
- **Energy:** Engaged but measured. Convey that this material matters without overselling.
- **Temporal awareness:** Mark what is new, what shifted, and what held steady compared to the prior week. This is what makes these entries useful months later.

### Talking Points

- **Mode:** Communication.
- **Tone:** Conversational authority. You are preparing to speak, not write a paper.
- **Person:** Second-person framing is acceptable in Pivot Phrases ("One thing you'll notice..."). First-person in Your Angle section.
- **Energy:** Higher confidence, punchier sentences. These are meant to be spoken aloud.

### Current Synthesis

- **Mode:** Communication.
- **Tone:** Authoritative overview. You are stating where your understanding stands as a clear, reader-facing position.
- **Person:** Third-person only. No "I" or "we."
- **Energy:** Calm, settled authority. Even when describing urgency, the writing itself is unhurried.
- **Lineage:** Each claim should cite the Weekly Learnings where the supporting evidence was first synthesized. This lets the reader trace backward through the compression chain.

### Active Ideas

- **Mode:** Processing.
- **Tone:** Working document. Clear, structured, evolving.
- **Person:** Third-person in Current Position. First-person acceptable in Evolution Log entries.
- **Energy:** Functional. Clarity over elegance. This is an inventory, not a narrative.

### User Observations

- **Mode:** Processing.
- **Tone:** Personal, reflective, grounded in experience.
- **Person:** First-person throughout.
- **Energy:** Authentic. These are your observations, not polished deliverables.

## Quick Self-Check

Before finalizing any CRIS output, scan for:

1. **Em dashes:** Search for "\u2014" and "\u2013". Replace all instances.
2. **Oxford commas:** Search for patterns like "X, Y and Z". Add the missing comma.
3. **Hedging language:** Search for "may," "might," "seems," "arguably," "perhaps." Replace with direct statements where evidence supports it.
4. **Compound claims:** Read each sentence. If it contains more than one idea, split it.
5. **Numeric density:** If a paragraph has more than two statistics, remove the least essential ones or move them to a supporting evidence section.
6. **Orphan numbers:** Every statistic should include who was measured, by whom, and when.
7. **Undefined jargon:** First use of any technical term should include a brief definition.
8. **Contractions in formal documents:** Check Weekly Learnings, Current Synthesis, and Active Ideas for contractions and expand them.
9. **Bold overuse:** If more than three phrases are bolded in a single paragraph, reduce to the most important one.
10. **Temporal markers (Weekly Learnings only):** Verify the entry makes clear what is new, what shifted, and what continued from the prior week.
