At a glance, the two large B2B companies were almost identical. Both sell complex, multi-year technology services; they compete for many of the same enterprise customers. Sales stages, forecasting cadence, and executive review rhythms were the same. Going by their customer relationship management (CRM) systems, their processes looked indistinguishable.

In practice, however, they diverged in ways that materially affected revenue, risk, and speed.

The first company serves regulated, mission-critical industries such as utilities, financial institutions, and public infrastructure. Over time, its sales organization has evolved a distinctive way of working. Sellers routinely pause deal advancement while delivery leaders review feasibility assumptions in parallel documents. Solution architects compare staffing models and exception histories from prior deals before committing scope. Risk and legal teams are looped in early, even when commercial terms are still fluid. Escalations tend to occur only after multiple internal validations.

The second company serves digitally driven enterprises undergoing rapid transformation, competing on speed of rollout and time to value. Early momentum matters more than perfect certainty. Salespeople pull delivery and solution leaders into exploratory conversations before scope is finalized. Documents are revised midstream. Signals of customer urgency trigger escalation, even when information is incomplete. Leaders intervene early to shape direction rather than validate certainty.

In both cases, none of this behavior is captured in the CRM. The system records stage changes and forecast updates. It does not record how feasibility was debated, which risks were weighed, or why escalation was deferred. Those decisions live in emails, chats, spreadsheets, working documents, and conversations and largely disappear once the deal moves forward.

Yet this context, ephemeral as it may be, makes up the fabric of an organization. These patterns of judgment, coordination, and trade-offs shape how work actually unfolds. They’re repeated across hundreds of deals and the thousands of little decisions that make up each one. This context reflects what an organization has learned, through experience, about which behaviors lead to success in its market.

As companies search for how AI might give them an edge, they need to realize that this context is where their competitive advantage really lies. When everyone has access to the same AI models, the same AI-enabled tools, and the same vendor ecosystem, context becomes the differentiator.

For the last five years, we’ve worked on building technology to analyze how teams work across hundreds of global organizations. In doing so, we have examined more than 200 distinct work patterns across more than 50 large enterprises, mostly Fortune 500 or European equivalents. We found that even when companies operated in the same industry, performed the same functions, and used the same systems, their execution consistently diverged. Context, not access to tools, explained the variation.

Here’s what leaders need to understand about why context has become a decisive source of competitive advantage, and how they can capture and operationalize it.

## What do we mean by “context”?
Context is demonstrated execution: the workflows teams actually follow across systems, the signals they respond to, the order in which roles get involved, the exceptions that trigger action, and the judgment calls that repeat across real work. These patterns are visible only in execution, not in stated process.

In the regulated-services firm, context dictates that account executives, delivery leaders, and risk stakeholders establish delivery certainty and defensibility before the deal advances. In the digitally driven firm, context favors early signal capture and speed, even if information is incomplete. These rules rarely live in policy manuals. They live in behavior, reinforced by what gets approved, delayed, escalated, or blocked.

For decades, management systems tried to eliminate variation. Standardization promised efficiency and control. It delivered cost savings, but it also erased nuance. AI changes the equation: because models are general, they require explicit contextual grounding to reflect what actually matters inside a specific organization.

Strategy experts have long argued that sustainable advantage rests on resources that are valuable, rare, difficult to imitate, and non-substitutable. Organizational context meets all four criteria.

It is valuable because it shapes revenue, risk, speed, and customer trust. It is rare because it reflects a unique mix of customers, constraints, and accumulated decisions. It is difficult to imitate because competitors can copy processes, but not years of tacit learning embedded in execution. It is non-substitutable because without context, even the most advanced AI systems flatten differences that matter. Its impact compounds through repetition: when context consistently guides everyday decisions across roles and workflows, performance differences become durable.

## AI makes context more valuable, not less.
As AI becomes cheaper, faster, and more widely available, access to frontier models is no longer differentiating. Many companies use the same models and platforms, yet results vary dramatically. The reason is not model quality. It is context.

AI systems are general by design. They cannot infer which signals matter, which trade-offs take precedence, or how risk and speed are balanced unless those patterns are made explicit. When AI is layered onto generic processes, it standardizes behavior. When it is grounded in organizational context, it amplifies what makes that organization distinctive.

This also explains why many AI pilots fail to scale. Early experiments show promise in controlled environments, but when deployed across real workflows, impact plateaus. Without grounding in how teams actually operate, AI struggles to navigate institutional trade-offs and coordination norms. What works in a demo environment rarely survives contact with operational complexity. Leaders often respond by tuning prompts, adding documents, or expanding training data. Those steps can help at the margins, but they rarely change the core problem: the model lacks the organization’s operating logic. Until AI systems are grounded in how work is actually done, improvements remain local and short-lived.

Historically, organizations embedded context by redesigning workflows and systems. Today, that same contextual knowledge can ground models and agents directly. The leverage shifts from redesigning systems to amplifying judgment. What is new is not context itself, but the ability to capture it systematically and apply it in the moment, inside the tools people already use.

## Context in action: How it shapes decisions
To see how context becomes actionable in practice, consider the following example. In one large enterprise organization we studied, a sales director overseeing a $10–15 million enterprise account reviewed an opportunity marked “Negotiation” in the CRM. The seller’s notes were positive. On paper, the deal looked healthy.

As she scanned recent activity, several subtle signals stood out. A delivery manager asked in a chat thread whether the proposed scope could be phased. A solution architect quietly updated a pricing model to remove two optional modules. A customer email referenced “internal budget alignment” rather than “legal review.”

In isolation, none of this is alarming. Whether it matters depends on the company’s context. In a risk-averse organization, this pattern may be routine; escalating now would create noise. In a speed-oriented organization, the same pattern has historically preceded slippage unless leadership intervenes early.

That distinction does not live in the CRM. It lives in the organization’s accumulated experience across many deals and roles. When surfaced in the moment, it does not replace the director’s judgment. It calibrates it, helping her decide whether to escalate, reframe the deal, or stay the course in a way consistent with how the organization has learned to win. Repeated across hundreds of decisions, that calibration produces better forecasting, faster intervention, and more consistent execution. Over time, this reduces forecast variance, improves the timing of leadership intervention, and increases close-rate predictability, outcomes that translate directly into measurable performance gains.

This dynamic is not unique to selling. In procurement, systems of record capture purchase orders and approvals but not how exceptions are negotiated or supplier risk is interpreted. In customer service, ticketing systems log resolution codes but not the coordination patterns that prevent escalation. In finance, ERP systems record transactions but not the judgment behind credit decisions or capital allocation trade-offs. Across functions, systems of record capture outcomes. They rarely capture how execution unfolded.

Context becomes competitive advantage when it is coherent, aligned with strategy, and reinforced through daily action.

## What CXOs should do now
AI ROI is not determined by model capability alone. It is determined by how precisely intelligence is grounded in the organization’s operating context. Organizations that deploy models without this grounding may see promising pilots but struggle to translate them into durable operational gains. Improvements remain localized.

To convert AI investment into measurable return, leaders must build a context layer that grounds intelligence in operational reality. This is context engineering: capturing how work unfolds, encoding those patterns into usable signals, and delivering the right context to models and agents at the moment of decision. It is not documentation. It is enterprise infrastructure. Implementing this context layer requires four deliberate actions.

1. **Instrument work to capture context**
Invest in technologies that map how work unfolds (the work graph) across systems and collaboration environments, creating a continuously updated view of decision sequences, coordination patterns, and trade-offs. Use this to build a firm-wide context library: a validated repository of recurring execution patterns linked to outcomes. Every function contributes, because every function generates contextual signal.

2. **Make the context library a runtime layer for AI**
Every AI system should connect to the context library. That requires technology capable of inferring user intent and retrieving the precise slice of context relevant to the task. Too little context produces generic output. Too much introduces noise and increases the likelihood of error. Precision is what turns context into performance, and it is often the difference between pilots that stall and deployments that improve outcomes.

3. **Establish governance and trust**
Context engineering requires clear privacy, security, and access controls. Sensitive information must be managed or redacted. Define validation, retention, and update protocols. Governance should also clarify which teams can access which contextual layers and for what purposes. Trust is foundational; without it, context will not be captured honestly or used confidently.

4. **Monitor impact and close the ROI loop**
Instrument outcomes alongside AI usage. Where forecasting accuracy improves, deal velocity increases, risk events decline, or cycle times compress, contextual grounding is working. Where it does not, refine the context layer and the retrieval logic. Monitoring requires linking AI interactions to business metrics and continuously updating contextual models based on performance data. Over time, this creates a compounding feedback loop in which institutional judgment becomes increasingly precise.

This shift changes the CXO mental model. Historically, enterprise technology was primarily designed, governed, and maintained by centralized IT or engineering functions, even when business units sponsored the investment. In a model-driven enterprise, context becomes infrastructure: a continuously updated representation of how the organization works. Competitive advantage and AI ROI depend on how tightly these two layers are integrated.

Access to models will continue to expand. Context will remain organization-specific and hence a competitive advantage.
