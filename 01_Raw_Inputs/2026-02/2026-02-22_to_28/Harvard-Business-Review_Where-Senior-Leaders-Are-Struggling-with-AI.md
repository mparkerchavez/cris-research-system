# Where Senior Leaders Are Struggling with AI Adoption, According to Research

As AI becomes embedded across organizations, senior leaders are navigating large-scale change and are under pressure to demonstrate impact. AI is no longer confined to pilots; it is shaping everyday decisions, workflows, and client delivery.

Despite increasing investment, many organizations are struggling to translate experimentation into scaled value. In a 2026 survey of global AI and data leaders, 93% identified human factors as the primary barrier to adoption. Yet, leaders remain confident in AI’s potential.

To understand these dynamics in practice, The Positive Group conducted an in-depth qualitative study with senior leaders from large global organizations across professional services, financial services, consumer brands, aviation, and life sciences. Through interviews and focus groups with 35 CEOs, CHROs, chief innovation officers, and functional leaders accountable for AI strategy, workforce impact, and risk, we explored how leaders are experiencing AI adoption in real time: the conversations shaping direction, how adaptability is being tested, and the behaviors sustaining progress.

## Where Tensions Are Emerging
Despite consensus that AI is a strategic priority, leaders described mounting pressure as continuous disruption reshapes expectations at every level of the organization.

### “The target’s always moving”
> “A key leadership skill moving forward is change resilience. Unlike past episodic transformations, AI brings continuous disruption, which means resilience has to be built enterprise-wide.” —CHRO, technology company

Several leaders contrasted AI adoption with previous large-scale transformations. Unlike episodic initiatives, new systems, restructures, or operating-model shifts, AI is causing continual disruption with no clear end point.

> “The target’s always moving. I know what we said six months ago, but actually the target’s moved. That’s hard, because credibility starts to wobble when direction keeps changing, even if the change is justified.” — Senior leader, professional services firm

The pace of change also distorted how progress was perceived.

> “Change rarely happens gradually; it’s more like climate change. Nothing seems to shift, and then suddenly the world looks completely different. Leading through that kind of sudden disruption is one of the hardest challenges.” —Head of learning and development, professional services firm

For many organizations, AI arrived on top of years of overlapping transformation.

> “We’ve been through years of major change, and there’s a real tiredness that comes with that. Even when the case for change feels obvious, people absorb it very differently depending on where they are emotionally and in their careers. Pushing too hard, too fast can backfire.” —Innovation strategy lead, global law firm

### Everyone wants AI success, but no one agrees what “value” means
Leaders described navigating different conversations depending on their audience, with expectations varying sharply across stakeholders.

> “One of the biggest challenges for me has been communication around AI, because there are layers to it. With shareholders, everything gets collapsed into one label of ‘AI.’ It doesn’t really matter what it is or how it’s being used. The question is simply, ‘What are you doing in AI?’ And when you try to go deeper, you can see people lose interest.
> 
> At the executive committee level, it often becomes, ‘If it’s got AI in it, we need to rush into it,’ with very little discussion about how early some of this technology still is, or what the risks might be to brand, clients, data, or IP.

> Employees have actually been the most positive. That’s where we’ve been clearer about what AI can and can’t do, and where it genuinely helps. But moving between those conversations is one of the hardest parts of leading this work.” —Chief technology officer, financial services firm

Leaders described pressure from boards to demonstrate visible progress, even when the underlying problem was unclear.

> “The biggest challenge isn’t resistance, it’s leadership FOMO. Everyone wants an update on AI before they understand the problem.” —Senior executive, global retail firm

This dynamic shaped how value was discussed internally.

> “A lot of AI applications won’t have a neat, immediate return. A tool that helps people write clearer emails or reduces reconciliation time might save 10 or 30 minutes a day. That doesn’t cut a role, but it compounds. And when leadership over-indexes on short-term ROI, people stop bringing forward ideas altogether.” —Senior vice president, financial services firm

Leaders noted that this narrow framing risked discouraging experimentation.

> “Leaders need to be mindful of how AI helps humans flourish, not just chase short-term gains. That means broadening the definition of return on investment beyond time and cost savings to include strategic impacts like employer value proposition, brand strength, and talent retention.” — Head of human-centered AI, financial services firm

In practice, organizations faced two pressures: a demand to move quickly and an absence of shared criteria for success. Leaders found themselves managing not resistance to AI, but uncertainty about what constituted meaningful value.

### Excitement, fear, and identity threat
Several leaders observed that anxiety was particularly pronounced among experienced professionals whose authority had been built on deep expertise.

> “What tends to get in the way isn’t the technology itself. A lot of it is about people worrying what this means for their role, saying things like, ‘That’s my job, you can automate that bit, but not this.’

> It’s easier to keep doing things the way you always have and ask, ‘What’s wrong with how I do it now?’ Real progress happens when someone who understands the technology is working closely with someone who understands the service or the client, but day-to-day urgency keeps pulling people back to what feels most immediate.” —Senior executive, global consulting firm

Several leaders described early missteps where fear or skepticism was treated as something to be overridden or corrected. In practice, this often reduced engagement rather than accelerating it.

> “When AI is introduced with fear, it fuels resistance to change, lowers engagement, and limits adoption. But when people are prepared, included in the journey, and know their voices are part of the design process, they are far more likely to engage.” —Senior digital leader, aviation industry

## What Leaders Are Doing About It
Leaders described how they were adjusting their approach in practice: how they communicated about AI, how they made their own learning visible, and how they created conditions for progress while uncertainty remained high.

### Broadening buy-in across the organization
In response to fragmented expectations and emotional reactions, many leaders described investing more deliberately in how they talked about AI. Several emphasized the need to strip away jargon to make it accessible enough for people to engage without fear.

> “Storytelling and simplicity are powerful drivers of adoption. We need to explain AI in plain language, simple enough that even my six-year-old could understand it, because accessibility builds curiosity and trust.” —Senior leader, consumer sector

Several leaders reflected that early AI conversations often leaned too heavily on technical detail, unintentionally signalling that AI was solely the domain of specialists.

> “The risk is treating AI as a narrow tech project. In reality, it’s a cultural shift and we need to embed it into how we think, manage, and lead, not just into workflows.” —Executive partner, law firm

Giving non-technical functions direct access to tools was described as both a learning mechanism and a way of securing buy-in.

> “One of the most disruptive things we’ve done is focus on functions that are usually late to engage with AI. Instead of assuming deep technical expertise is required, we’ve given teams like HR, legal, and finance tools they can experiment with directly. The people closest to the work are often best placed to see where AI can help. When routine tasks are taken care of, those teams have more time to focus on what really matters. This more federated approach has been one of the most meaningful shifts we’ve made.” — Senior technology leader, financial services firm

Several leaders also emphasized that clarity about AI was not just about tools, but about helping people think differently about work.

> “One of the hardest day-to-day challenges is getting people to step back and think about how work itself is going to change. We’ve spent years rewarding efficiency and productivity, and that mindset naturally pushes people to layer AI on top of existing processes. But that won’t get us where we need to be. The real shift is asking: six months from now, a year from now, how should this work fundamentally look different?” — Senior AI and people leader, global technology company

### Shaping norms in conditions of uncertainty
Several leaders noted that early hesitation around AI was less about capability and more about ambiguity. Unclear norms around risk and accountability led people to wait for reassurance that engagement was permitted. In response, some leaders deliberately used their own behavior to set those norms and make their learning visible.

> “I made a point of using the tools myself in visible ways. I’d take a 100-plus-page board paper and show how I used ChatGPT to summarize it. Not because it was perfect, but to show that you don’t need to be technical to get value. It’s about asking the right questions. That changed the conversation. People stopped asking, ‘Is this allowed?’ and started asking, ‘Could this help with the decisions I’m making?’” —Senior vice president, technology, insurance sector

When senior leaders experimented publicly, sharing prompts, acknowledging limitations, or explaining how outputs were validated, it reframed AI use as learning rather than compliance.

Leaders emphasized that creating safe conditions meant being explicit that early AI efforts were not expected to be perfect. This meant resisting the instinct to wait until tools, data, or use cases were fully validated, even in regulated industries.

> “We had to move very quickly [on an AI initiative], and from the start we were clear it wasn’t going to be perfect. We said, ‘We’ll put it out, we’ll iterate, we’ll take feedback.’ It’s an 80–20 approach, not 100%. That made me nervous, because people in our world don’t like that. But at the speed we’re operating at, if we wait for everything to be fully nailed down, we won’t get anywhere.” —Chief innovation officer, global law firm

Without visible leadership ownership of decisions to pause or cancel initiatives, organizations defaulted to running pilots that generated activity but little learning.

> “A lot of what we’re testing doesn’t come from senior leaders like me who are a bit removed from day-to-day client work. It comes from people who are doing this every day, seeing what’s changing and trying things. We’ve also had to get more comfortable saying, ‘This didn’t work, let’s stop it and try something else.’ Being willing to cancel things that aren’t delivering has been an important part of how we’re learning.” —Senior executive, international professional services firm

Leaders stressed that leading visibly was not about technical fluency, but signalling curiosity, judgement, and adaptability. By making experimentation visible, leaders helped teams feel permitted to engage with AI in their own work.

### Building trust through transparency
A key topic of discussion was the emotional response to AI. Fear about job security, discomfort with uncertainty, and concern about professional identity were common alongside excitement. What mattered, leaders argued, was not eliminating those reactions, but how they were handled.

> “In partnerships, you can’t simply tell people what to do. Change has to come through influence and example. That’s challenging when people are moving fast, feel pressure to deliver, and are understandably anxious about how their roles might evolve.” —Head of learning and development, professional services firm

Trust, leaders suggested, was built by being explicit about what was known, what was still unresolved, and how decisions would be revisited as evidence emerged.

> “When AI is introduced with fear, it fuels resistance to change, lowers engagement, and limits adoption. But when employees are prepared, included in the journey, and know their voices and perspectives are part of the design process, they are far more likely to embrace innovation.” —Senior digital leader, aviation industry

Leaders noted that this transparency helped maintained trust even when decisions changed. Rather than projecting certainty they could not sustain, they focused on consistency: explaining trade-offs, naming risks, and showing how learning informed next steps.

. . .
Across interviews, leaders described the psychological and relational challenges of AI integration: explaining what was changing, clarifying what mattered, and responding to mixed reactions across their organizations. In this environment, leadership behavior became more visible. How leaders framed priorities and role-modeled adaptability influenced how others engaged. While investment often focuses on technical capability, leaders shape adoption through foundational skills: judgement, empathy, and adaptability. AI may be transforming how work is done, but leaders can shape how that transformation is experienced. AI does not diminish the role of leadership, it raises the standard for it.
