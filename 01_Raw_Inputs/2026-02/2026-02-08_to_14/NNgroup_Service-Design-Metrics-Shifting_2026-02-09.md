## Transcript

AI agents will eventually become central players in our service deliveries, and this is going to force businesses to shift how they measure success. While traditional service metrics have served us well, this introduction of AI into our service ecosystems is going to shift what "good service" means.

We're all familiar with the traditional service metrics: user satisfaction, time to resolution, cost per transaction, error rate, customer effort—these are just naming a few. These have traditionally helped us understand service design quality through the lens of human-to-human and human-to-technology interactions. But as AI comes and reshapes all this, these metrics are going to change. Think of a future where a user's personal AI agent handles tasks like scheduling appointments or making reservations and interacts directly with your organization's AI agent. We're going to need new metrics.

So, some things we need to think about: how are we going to assess AI-to-AI system performance? Just as we evaluate human teams working together, we'll need to assess how AI teams collaborate. This will mean tracking how efficiently AI systems handoff tasks, measuring response times, and identifying where AI systems might create unexpected bottlenecks or redundancies. We'll need ways to understand those response times, completion rates, and the smoothness of these automated exchanges.

We're also going to need a metric for human and AI collaboration. It's going to be crucial for us to understand if AI is supporting our internal teams efficiently. This is going to potentially include things like measuring team productivity, tracking how confidently employees use AI tools, and evaluating the quality of the decisions made through this human and AI partnership.

Third, we'll need stronger metrics around data quality. As AI becomes central to our service delivery, the quality of our data is going to become critical to how it does that. We'll need to measure not just the accuracy and completion, but how well our data represents our diverse users and contexts, how fresh it is, if it includes any biases, and ensure that any data we use is properly structured for the AI system.

Maybe the most complex metric is how we understand human trust and control. As AI takes on more responsibilities, measuring confidence in these systems is going to become key. For users, we're going to need ways to evaluate their comfort level with handing off AI tasks, their sense of control over AI decisions, and their understanding of when and how AI is being used. For employees, we're going to have to measure things like trust in delegating tasks to AI and then their confidence in feeling in control overriding AI decisions when necessary.

While the technology may not be ready and I don't have all the answers, we as an industry can help lead this shift. Service designers should be actively collaborating with business leaders and starting the conversation on what success looks like in the future.
