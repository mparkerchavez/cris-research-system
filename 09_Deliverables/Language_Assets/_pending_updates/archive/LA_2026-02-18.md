### FRAMEWORKS & MENTAL MODELS

**Framework 1: Plan-Delegate-Assess-Compound (PDAC)**
- **Description:** Four-phase engineering productivity cycle where planning is commonly omitted, preventing compounding
- **Components:**
  1. Plan (goal-setting, task decomposition)
  2. Delegate (to agents via prompts/context)
  3. Assess (measure output quality)
  4. Compound (apply learnings to next cycle)
- **Distinctive Element:** Emphasis on missing planning phase as root cause of failed compounding
- **Evidence:** [Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie DP8, DP9] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie_2026-02-05.md;dp=8 -->
  - Engineers using complete framework generate "two weeks of work effectively in an afternoon"
  - Directly contradicts ad-hoc prompting approaches

**Framework 2: Savile Row Prompting (Customization Continuum)**
- **Description:** Spectrum of prompt customization from generic templates to institutional-knowledge-encoded systems
- **Components:**
  1. Generic prompts (transferable but low-value)
  2. Data-format-specific customization (how numbers appear, figure structures)
  3. Institutional-thought-pattern encoding (metric definitions, internal terminology)
  4. Savile Row level: "so specific the way figures show up, the way they think internally"
- **Distinctive Element:** Moves beyond "better prompts" to organizational epistemology encoding
- **Evidence:** [Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie DP6, DP7] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie_2026-02-05.md;dp=6 -->
  - "Here's how we think about what MRR is—that's where the value is"
  - Creates moats through knowledge codification

**Framework 3: Four Maturity Levels of AI Adoption**
- **Description:** Spectrum from ChatGPT-as-search through agent-as-native-interface
- **Components:**
  1. Level 1: ChatGPT as search replacement (summarize, draft)
  2. Level 2: Custom agents with permissions (like managing junior intern)
  3. Level 3: Autonomous agents with data connections
  4. Level 4: Agent as development environment (Claude/Claude Code as primary interface)
- **Distinctive Element:** Maturity anchored to locus-of-control shift: human→agent, chat→environment
- **Evidence:** [Every_NextChapterConsulting DP1-DP4] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_NextChapterConsulting_2026-02-05.md;dp=1 -->
  - Most companies stuck at Level 1 despite wanting Level 4
  - Rare Level 4 companies embrace experimentation, operate in small nimble environments

**Framework 4: Allocation Economy Model**
- **Description:** Workforce value shifts from subject-matter expertise to resource management capability
- **Components:**
  1. Old economy: Judged by depth of domain knowledge
  2. New economy: Judged by effectiveness allocating AI resources
  3. Key skills: Same as good management (goal-setting, feedback, learning)
- **Distinctive Element:** Reframes "AI proficiency" as management discipline, not technical specialty
- **Evidence:** [Every_NextChapterConsulting DP6, DP7] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_NextChapterConsulting_2026-02-05.md;dp=6 -->
  - "Won't be judged by limits of subject matter expertise, but by managing AI resources"
  - Directly challenges skill retraining narratives

**Framework 5: Vision-Blueprint-Furniture-Strategy Model**
- **Description:** Sequencing framework for technology adoption that reverses conventional approach
- **Components:**
  1. Vision = What future state the organization becomes
  2. Blueprint = How vision translates to organizational capabilities
  3. Furniture = Technology tools that enable blueprint
  4. Strategy = Assembly process connecting them
- **Distinctive Element:** Explicitly rejects starting with technology ("buying furniture before designing the house")
- **Evidence:** [FTSG_HotTrendsArentStrategy DP9, DP11] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/FTSG_HotTrendsArentStrategy_2026-02-05.md;dp=9 -->
  - When vision leads adoption: "Data becomes insight. IoT becomes infrastructure. AI becomes augmentation."

**Framework 6: Risk Coping Styles Framework (Silicon Valley vs. Enterprise)**
- **Description:** Structural differences in how innovation ecosystems manage risk produce linguistic and operational divergence
- **Components:**
  1. Silicon Valley: Abstract, speculative, future-tense (venture portfolio risk distribution)
  2. Enterprise: Cautious, bounded, present-tense (direct accountability, legal/reputational stakes)
  3. Interface problem: "Pilot purgatory" when promising tech must justify using mature-business standards
- **Distinctive Element:** Reframes apparent stylistic/personality difference as structural incentive incompatibility
- **Evidence:** [FTSG_HowWeTalkAboutAI DP4-DP6, DP12-DP13] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/FTSG_HowWeTalkAboutAI_2026-02-05.md;dp=4 -->
  - Venture "explicitly spreads risk across portfolio; failure is priced in"
  - Enterprise "failure carries legal, reputational, organizational consequences"

**Framework 7: Documentability Heuristic (Automation Readiness)**
- **Description:** Simple binary test for whether work can be automated
- **Components:**
  1. Documented tasks (like generating report from data) → Easy to automate
  2. Judgment tasks (like "what makes good investment") → Resist automation
- **Distinctive Element:** Provides clear diagnostic for AI feasibility without technology discussion
- **Evidence:** [Every_NextChapterConsulting DP9] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_NextChapterConsulting_2026-02-05.md;dp=9 -->
  - Separates procedure work from judgment work with single testable criterion

### ANTI-PATTERNS / WARNINGS

**Anti-Pattern 1: The CEO Delegation Problem**
- **What NOT to do:** Assume CEO can delegate AI adoption responsibility to others
- **Evidence of failure:** [Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie DP3] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie_2026-02-05.md;dp=3 -->
  - "You will go as far as your CEO has gone in AI adoption. It's not something CEO can delegate."
- **Why it fails:** Organizational adoption ceiling tracks personal executive capability, not reported readiness
- **Alternative:** CEO personal AI adoption → organizational adoption through top-down modeling
- **Transferability:** High; suggests direct accountability structures

**Anti-Pattern 2: Building Agentic Workflows Without Creative Time**
- **What NOT to do:** Expect non-technical staff to develop agent workflows during standard operational hours
- **Evidence of failure:** [Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie DP10] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie_2026-02-05.md;dp=10 -->
  - Staff needed to start workday 3 hours early to "vibe code" and develop workflows
- **Why it fails:** Operational work crowds out exploratory learning; habit patterns prevent experimentation
- **Alternative:** Dedicate protected time for capability building outside production constraints
- **Transferability:** High; applies to all enterprise learning transformations

**Anti-Pattern 3: 85% Completion Multiple Times (Workflow Architecture Iteration)**
- **What NOT to do:** Expect first agent architecture to work; plan for complete rebuilds
- **Evidence of failure:** [Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie DP12] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie_2026-02-05.md;dp=12 -->
  - "Got 85% of the way there three times and had to scrap it"
- **Why it fails:** Combining domain expertise and technical architecture knowledge requires iterative discovery
- **Alternative:** Build iteration and scrapping into project timelines; expect multiple complete restarts
- **Transferability:** High; sets realistic expectations for agentic workflow development

**Anti-Pattern 4: Bifurcated Adoption (High-Powered Users vs. Floundering Staff)**
- **What NOT to do:** Allow organic AI adoption without coordinated effort; creates power-user elite
- **Evidence of failure:** [Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie DP2] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_HowWeBuiltClaudie_2026-02-05.md;dp=2 -->
  - "Few high-powered users get ton of leverage; everyone else floundering"
- **Why it fails:** Without coordination, adoption tracks individual initiative rather than organizational need
- **Alternative:** Top-down coordinated strategy; capability building for entire relevant population
- **Transferability:** High; predicts adoption equity dynamics

**Anti-Pattern 5: Starting with Technology Instead of Vision**
- **What NOT to do:** Select AI tools, IoT infrastructure, data platforms before defining organizational future
- **Evidence of failure:** [FTSG_HotTrendsArentStrategy DP4, DP6] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/FTSG_HotTrendsArentStrategy_2026-02-05.md;dp=4 -->
  - "Starting with technologies leads to incrementalism, not transformation"
  - Expensive infrastructure becomes cost center without blueprint connection
- **Why it fails:** Tools without vision direction don't cumulate into capability; they create maintenance overhead
- **Alternative:** Vision first; blueprint of required capabilities; then furniture selection
- **Transferability:** High; reverses typical procurement patterns

**Anti-Pattern 6: Measuring Innovation Using Mature-Business Standards**
- **What NOT to do:** Evaluate early-stage capability programs using profitability, efficiency, or customer metrics
- **Evidence of failure:** [FTSG_HowWeTalkAboutAI DP13, DP16] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/FTSG_HowWeTalkAboutAI_2026-02-05.md;dp=13 -->
  - "Pilot purgatory"—promising technology forced to justify using mature-business standards
  - Kills innovation before capability can be built
- **Why it fails:** Early phases will look "wasteful" by mature metrics; measurement framework must shift
- **Alternative:** Measure by capability accumulation (what org can now do); deferred margin evaluation
- **Transferability:** High; reframes success metrics

**Anti-Pattern 7: Viewing Data as Cost Center Rather Than Growth Engine**
- **What NOT to do:** Build data infrastructure as compliance/operational burden without strategic mission
- **Evidence of failure:** [FTSG_HotTrendsArentStrategy DP5] <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/FTSG_HotTrendsArentStrategy_2026-02-05.md;dp=5 -->

- **Transferability:** High; applies to all data-heavy organizations

**Anti-Pattern 8: Assuming High Infrastructure Investment Justifies Itself**
