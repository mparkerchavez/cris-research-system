# What Engineers Are Teaching Us About the Future of White-Collar Work

In February, I continued the research I started at the beginning of this year, tracking what is happening across AI adoption, workforce transformation, and organizational readiness. Close to 180 sources in the month of February alone: surveys, research papers, industry reports, practitioner accounts, podcasts, and primary data from Deloitte, HBR, YouGov, Figma, The Economist, and others.

Coming into February, we already knew something significant was underway. Opus 4.5 in November 2025 was the moment people experimenting with Claude Code started seeing a major shift in agentic capabilities. Codex 5.2 followed in late December. Then in quick succession: Claude Cowork in January, and on February 5th, Opus 4.6 and Codex 5.3 released within 15 minutes of each other, followed by the Codex App. Combined with the OpenClaw incident that rattled the agentic community, it was clear that the landscape had shifted meaningfully from where we were even a few months prior. February confirmed and deepened that shift in ways I did not fully anticipate.

What I keep coming back to, though, is not the technology. It is the people. Technology is advancing quickly, and the capability story is real. But the bigger question, the one that sits between what businesses want and need and how people are actually dealing with the rapid change to their understanding, their careers, and how they work, is where my thinking keeps anchoring.

Three patterns stood out. I want to share them here, not as conclusions, but as observations from someone still in the middle of making sense of it all.

---

## 1. Engineers are rewriting their jobs in real time, and the rest of us should be paying attention.

The most striking signal from February was watching a profession begin to redesign what it means to do its work, in weeks, not years.

Software engineers have gone from writing code to managing agents that write code. In a Y Combinator video featuring several of their portfolio companies, developers described managing three to eight AI instances simultaneously, implementing features, fixing bugs, and researching solutions [YCombinator_TheNewWayToBuildAStartup DP1]. At OpenAI, Sherwin Wu shared on Lenny's Podcast that engineers on his team manage 10 to 20 concurrent agent threads as an operational baseline, with 95% of engineers using Codex daily [LennysPodcast_SoftwareDevelopmentFutureShewinWu DP3, DP1]. Four percent of all public code commits globally now come from Claude Code [YCombinator_BorisChernyClaudeCode DP14]. Ninety percent of Claude Code's own codebase was written by Claude Code itself [AINewsStrategyDaily_JuniorDevJobsDropped DP3].

This is not incremental. This is a fundamental shift in what it means to be an engineer. The job moved from authorship to orchestration. From writing the code yourself to specifying what needs to be built, managing the agents that build it, evaluating whether the output is right, and applying the taste and judgment to know when "working" is not the same as "good."

Here is why this matters beyond engineering.

The skills that now define a good engineer, the ability to specify intent clearly, to evaluate output quality, to coordinate multiple autonomous processes, to make judgment calls about tradeoffs, are not engineering-specific skills. They are knowledge work skills. And the tools that enabled this shift are not staying inside engineering. They are already moving into design, product management, marketing, operations, and customer service.

My hypothesis: what happened to engineering in early 2026 will happen to most white-collar roles before the year is out. Not because the same tools will be used, but because the same pattern will repeat. The work will shift from execution to specification and evaluation. The people who adapt to that shift will see their value increase. The people who don't will find the gap between them and their peers growing wider every month.

This is not a prediction about mass layoffs. The Economist reported that white-collar employment has actually expanded by 3 million jobs [TheEconomist_AIWontWipeOutWhiteCollarJobs DP1]. But the composition changed. Back-office roles contracted 20% while coordination roles expanded by 30% or more [TheEconomist_AIWontWipeOutWhiteCollarJobs DP10, DP9]. The work is not disappearing. It is transforming. And the transformation is happening at engineering speed, which means it is happening faster than most organizations are prepared for.

I will be honest about my own experience here. I did not touch Claude Cowork or the Codex App until mid-January, well behind the people at the frontier who had been using Claude Code over the holidays and earlier. In the middle of that, OpenClaw was making an explosion into the world, and I still have not tried it going into March. I have hesitations around technical capability and potential cost. I am not at the frontier, and that is ok.

But I say that to make a point: even for someone actively tracking this space, the pace creates a gap. I can feel the distance between where I am and where the most advanced practitioners are. It is not comfortable, and that discomfort is informative. If I am feeling it, with the level of attention I am giving this space, imagine what it feels like for someone who is not tracking it at all.

The professionals who leaned in, who reorganized their workflows around these new capabilities, who treated AI as a collaborator rather than a threat, did not just survive. They became dramatically more capable. The ones who waited are already feeling the gap.

That pattern will likely repeat across most knowledge work domains. The question is less about whether and more about when. And based on what I have seen in February, for many roles, when is now.

## 2. The growing resistance to AI is real, it is personal, and dismissing it will cost us.

While engineers were rapidly adopting new ways of working, a very different story was unfolding across the broader workforce and the American public.

Fifty-eight percent of Americans distrust AI systems [YouGov_AmericansAIJobReduction DP4]. That number has not moved since September 2025 [YouGov_AmericansAIJobReduction DP7]. Six months of accelerating AI capability, six months of new product launches and breathless headlines, produced zero shift in public sentiment. The US ranks dead last internationally on AI trust. Time's March 2026 cover story, "The People vs. AI" by Andrew R. Chow, captures the scale of what is happening: a 2025 Pew poll found five times as many Americans are concerned about AI as are excited by it [Time_ThePeopleVsAI DP2]. And this is not just sentiment. Activists have stalled $98 billion in data center projects through grassroots organizing [Time_ThePeopleVsAI DP4]. Resistance is producing real economic consequences.

It is easy, from inside the AI conversation, to dismiss this as a knowledge gap. People just don't understand what AI can do. If they tried it, they would come around. But the data tells a more complicated story, and I think we owe it to people to take their concerns seriously rather than assuming we know better.

The resistance is not uniform. It comes from distinct places, and Time's reporting makes this visible [Time_ThePeopleVsAI DP1; TheAIDailyBrief_AntiAIMovement DP8]. Artists worried about intellectual property. Healthcare professionals demanding testing rigor before AI touches patient care. Communities opposing data centers being built in their neighborhoods, making rational local political choices. Religious communities concerned about relational AI. And the broadest category: people worried about what AI means for their jobs, their identity, and their sense of agency at work.

Here is what struck me most. HBR's February 2026 research found that employees experiencing AI anxiety actually use AI more than their non-anxious peers, 65% of their work versus 42% [HarvardBusinessReview_WhyAIAdoptionStalls DP8]. That seems counterintuitive until you understand the mechanism: people are using AI not because they find it valuable, but because they are afraid of what happens if they don't. Usage is not adoption. It is compliance driven by fear [HarvardBusinessReview_WhyAIAdoptionStalls DP9].

Forty percent of employees simultaneously believe AI has business value and fear what it means for their personal security [HarvardBusinessReview_WhyAIAdoptionStalls DP4]. Seventy-four percent of individual contributors, the people doing the most automatable work, report feeling anxious or overwhelmed by AI [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP15]. Meanwhile, manager support for IC AI usage has declined 11 percentage points since May 2025, with only 7% of ICs reporting that their managers expect daily AI use [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP14]. The people with the most to gain are getting the least help.

And organizations are largely missing this. Eighty-eight percent report regular AI use [HarvardBusinessReview_WhyAIAdoptionStalls DP2]. Leaders see usage dashboards going up and assume adoption is working. But 84% of organizations have not redesigned jobs around AI (Deloitte State of AI 2026) [Deloitte_StateOfAI2026 DP7]. They have given people tools without changing the work. The result is not productivity. It is intensification. More output, same processes, growing exhaustion.

This is not an argument against AI adoption. It is an argument for taking the human dimension seriously. The concerns people have are not irrational. They are responding to real uncertainty about their future, often without adequate support, training, or honest conversation from their leaders. CEO rhetoric framing humans as inefficient compute actively hardens resistance [TheAIDailyBrief_AntiAIMovement DP22, DP23]. It confirms people's worst fears and narrows the window for building genuine trust.

The research suggests that roughly 80% of current opposition is implementation-specific and addressable [TheAIDailyBrief_AntiAIMovement DP25]. Most people are not opposed to AI in principle. They are opposed to how it is being deployed, communicated, and governed. The 75% centrist majority has not yet hardened into polarized factions. But that window is closing.

For organizations, the implication is that capability alone will not drive adoption. You can buy the best tools and deploy the most powerful models, and still fail if your people do not trust the process, do not see themselves in the future you are building, and do not have genuine support in making the transition. For individuals who are resistant, I think the honest conversation is not "you need to get on board" but rather: the evidence suggests that the nature of knowledge work is shifting, and the gap between those who engage with that shift and those who don't is widening in measurable ways. That is worth understanding, even if you ultimately choose a different path.

What concerns me most is not the people who are actively resisting. It is the people who are quietly disengaging, using AI just enough to look compliant while falling further behind in genuine capability. That silent middle is where the real risk lives, both for organizations and for the individuals themselves.

## 3. The spectrum of AI readiness is splintering, and we need a better way to understand where people actually are.

This is where the first two observations converge.

Section's AI Proficiency Report, based on a survey of over 5,000 knowledge workers, defines four personas: novices (28% of the workforce), experimenters (68%), practitioners (3%), and experts (0.1%) [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP4, DP5; Section_StateOfAIProficiency DP18]. The numbers are stark. More than two-thirds of knowledge workers are stuck in what Section calls a "use case desert" [Section_StateOfAIProficiency DP6]. They know how to use AI. They can write a prompt, summarize a meeting, rewrite an email. But they cannot identify meaningful applications in their actual work. Only 15% have what the report calls value-driving use cases. Less than 1% build automations [Section_StateOfAIProficiency DP3].

Even reaching practitioner level is not the ceiling. Expert-level proficiency, where individuals are building automations, designing AI-native workflows, and creating organizational capability, sits at 0.1% of the workforce. The distance from novice to expert is not a single gap. It is a ladder with widening rungs.

The gap between experimenters and practitioners is not a training gap. Companies that have invested in AI training see employees score an average of 40 out of 100 on proficiency. They are still experimenters [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP9]. Foundation investments, policy, tools, training, move people from novice to experimenter. They do not solve the leap from experimenter to practitioner [Section_StateOfAIProficiency DP16]. That leap requires something fundamentally different: role-specific use case development, manager support, continuous reinforcement, and time.

Here is what I believe is happening. When ChatGPT launched in November 2022, AI was new to nearly everyone. For a brief moment, the field was level. Everyone was a beginner. That resetting moment created an illusion of equal starting positions.

That illusion is gone. As capabilities have advanced, people, teams, and organizations are not splitting into two groups. They are splintering across a widening spectrum, each at a different point, each moving at a different speed, each facing different barriers. At one end, practitioners who are saving 8 to 12 hours per week and fundamentally changing how their function operates [Section_StateOfAIProficiency DP12]. AI practitioners are 20 times more likely to save 4 or more hours per week than novices [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP8]. At the other end, 28% of the workforce who represent, in Section's framing, "a net risk" to their organizations, with 79% unable to protect sensitive information when using AI and 86% lacking official access to AI tools at work [Section_StateOfAIProficiency DP4, DP5, DP24].

This splintering is not just about individuals. It operates across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Organizationally, Deloitte's State of AI 2026 survey of 3,235 respondents across 24 countries found 34% deep transformers, 30% moderate implementers, and 37% surface-level users [Deloitte_StateOfAI2026 DP5]. Temporally, small firms operate on two-hour AI decision cycles while large enterprises face 18-month deployment timelines [AINewsStrategyDaily_CapabilityDissipationGap DP13, DP14]. In the workforce, back-office roles are contracting while coordination roles expand [TheEconomist_AIWontWipeOutWhiteCollarJobs DP10, DP9]. Entrepreneurially, 532,000 new business applications were filed in January 2026, with formation costs down 70 to 80% [InTheTrenches_The2028GlobalIntelligenceBoom DP7, DP8]. And at the skill level, there is a 31-point gap between senior and junior hiring across design and engineering [Figma_DemandForDesignIn2026 DP2].

This is structural, not transitional. The gap is not closing. It is compounding.

So what do we do about it?

I think the answer starts with honest assessment. Before you can close a gap, you have to see it. Most organizations cannot accurately gauge where their people actually are. Section's research found that executives believe their AI deployments are succeeding while individual contributors disagree by margins of 20 to 32 percentage points [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP11]. The C-suite thinks the problem is solved. The workforce knows it is not.

What we need is something like an AI readiness audit, not a one-time assessment, but an ongoing practice. Where are your people on this spectrum? Not where do they say they are, but where do their behaviors and outcomes place them? Where do they want to be? What is the gap between current state and target state? What specific interventions will bridge that gap for each persona? And critically, how do you keep reassessing as the technology advances?

This last point matters more than it might seem. The proficiency bar keeps rising. What counted as AI proficient two or three years ago, writing good prompts, is just table stakes now. In 2026, proficiency means meaningful workflow integration, identifying real use cases, and delivering measurable value. We are already seeing the discipline evolve from prompt engineering into context engineering, intent engineering, and what some are calling specification engineering, each layer requiring deeper organizational engagement than the last [AINewsStrategyDaily_FourDisciplinesOfPromptingPost2026 DP6]. Next year, the bar will be somewhere else entirely.

This is not a one-time training initiative. It is a continuous organizational discipline. Moderna's example is instructive: they achieved 80% AI proficiency across 5,000 workers, but it took 4 to 5 years, a dedicated head of AI, appropriate budget, and thousands of hands-on sessions [Section_StateOfAIProficiency DP19]. Most organizations are treating this as a quarterly initiative when it requires a multi-year commitment.

## What I keep coming back to.

These three observations are not separate stories. They are one story.

Engineering shows us the pattern: when people engage deeply with AI capabilities, the nature of their work transforms. The resistance data shows us the human cost of that transformation when it is poorly managed, poorly communicated, or simply ignored. And the proficiency spectrum shows us the current state of the gap, who is where, and how fast the distance between groups is growing.

One thing worth naming about engineers: they embrace new tools, capabilities, and change. That is part of the culture. Experimentation is the default. That is a significant reason why their adaptation has happened so quickly. But you do not have to be an engineer to have that orientation. The underlying trait is comfort with change and ambiguity, a willingness to experiment before you have all the answers, to try something new even when you are not sure it will work.

That, I think, is the deeper human question. Not "how do we train people on AI tools" but "how do we help people become more comfortable with change itself?" And for leaders, how do you create environments where that kind of experimentation feels safe rather than threatening? Because the evidence suggests that organizations and individuals who engage with this shift are gaining measurable advantage. And the gap will keep widening.

The through-line, for me, is that this is fundamentally a people challenge. Not a technology challenge, not a strategy challenge, not even an adoption challenge in the way most organizations frame it. It is about understanding where people actually are, what they are actually experiencing, and building genuine pathways forward that respect both the reality of what is changing and the legitimacy of what people are feeling.

I do not have this figured out. I am still tracking, still researching, still forming my perspective. But the evidence from February was too significant to sit on. The pace of change is accelerating, the gap is widening, and the window for getting the human side right is not infinite.

If any of this resonates, or if you see it differently, I would genuinely like to hear your perspective. That is what this stage of the work is for.
