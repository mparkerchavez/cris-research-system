I spent February tracking close to 180 sources on AI adoption, workforce transformation, and organizational readiness. Three patterns kept surfacing, and they tell one story.

1. Engineers are rewriting their jobs in real time. They have gone from writing code to managing fleets of AI agents. At OpenAI, engineers manage 10-20 concurrent agent threads. 4% of all public code commits now come from Claude Code. The job shifted from authorship to orchestration, specification, and taste. My hypothesis: this same pattern will reach most white-collar roles in 2026. Not the same tools, but the same shift from execution to specification and evaluation.

2. The growing resistance to AI is real, personal, and consequential. 58% of Americans distrust AI, unchanged for six months (YouGov). Time's March 2026 cover story documents $98 billion in stalled data center projects from grassroots organizing. Meanwhile, HBR found that anxious employees actually use AI more (65% vs. 42%), not because they find it valuable, but because they fear what happens if they don't. Usage is not adoption. It is compliance. And 84% of organizations have not redesigned jobs around AI (Deloitte 2026). We owe people more than "get on board."

3. AI readiness is splintering. Section's AI Proficiency Report found 28% novices, 68% experimenters, 3% practitioners, and 0.1% experts. The ChatGPT moment in 2022 leveled the field briefly. That illusion is gone. People, teams, and organizations are spreading across a widening spectrum. The proficiency bar keeps rising: from prompting to context engineering to intent engineering. This is not a one-time training problem. It is a continuous discipline.

The through-line: this is a people challenge. Not a technology challenge. It is about understanding where people actually are, what they are experiencing, and building pathways forward that respect both what is changing and what people are feeling.
