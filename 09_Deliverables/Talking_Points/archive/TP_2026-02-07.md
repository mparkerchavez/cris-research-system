## Talking Point 1: The Capability-Implementation Gap

### The Point

The primary barrier to enterprise AI ROI is not model capability, integration complexity, or prompting skill. It is a multi-dimensional gap spanning Technology, Data, People, and Process, where most organizations invest seriously in only the first two. Eighty-five percent of knowledge workers lack value-driving AI use cases despite 75% actively using AI tools (Section, January 2026). Training helps, but only to a point: it moves people from novice to experimenter, not from experimenter to practitioner.

### Why It Matters

- Organizations spending millions on AI infrastructure are not capturing value because they have not redesigned workflows or developed the people side.
- McKinsey's $2.9 trillion in projected economic value is contingent on workflow redesign, not technology deployment. Someone has to identify which workflows to redesign and how.
- Shadow IT deployment of autonomous agents is now systemic. Employees are adopting frontier tools without governance, creating a new barrier dimension that compounds the existing gap.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| CTOs and CIOs | Their AI investments are underperforming because the People and Process dimensions are underfunded |
| CHROs | Workforce capability building is the bottleneck, and training alone does not solve it |
| CEOs | The gap between investment and value is widening, not closing |
| Consulting buyers | Organizations need someone who can bridge the gap across all four dimensions |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** 85% of knowledge workers lack value-driving AI use cases despite 75% using AI tools (Section, January 2026)
- **Source credibility:** Section surveyed across industries, January 2026. McKinsey's analysis spans multiple enterprise surveys.
- **Supporting data:** 90% of companies have invested in AI but fewer than 40% see measurable gains (McKinsey, November 2025). Only 2.7% of users reach practitioner-level proficiency (Section). 20% of ClawHub skills contain vulnerabilities (VentureBeat, February 2026), demonstrating the shadow IT security risk.

### Your Angle

I have seen this gap from the inside. At Capital Group, heavy investment in technology and data infrastructure produced experimenters, not practitioners. The missing dimensions (People and Process) required different interventions: mental model shifts toward non-deterministic systems, workflow redesign for AI collaboration, and psychologically safe environments for codifying tribal knowledge. The four-dimensional framework comes from two years of observing what actually works versus what organizations assume will work.

### Pivot Phrases

- "One pattern I keep seeing across the research is..."
- "There is a number from Section's January report that stops people in their tracks..."
- "The gap is not where most people think it is..."

## Talking Point 2: SaaS Repricing and the Two-Timeline Problem

### The Point

The SaaS market is repricing what software is worth in real time, and the aggregate correction has reached $800 billion. Per-seat pricing models are mathematically broken when AI enables 10 people to do 100 people's work. But enterprise adoption operates on three to five year cycles, while markets are pricing in disruption on a timeline of months. This gap is not a problem to solve. It is the terrain to navigate.

### Why It Matters

- Stock markets have moved from "Will AI disrupt software?" to "How fast?" and are pricing in aggressive timelines.
- Enterprises need to prepare governance infrastructure, workforce transition plans, and new pricing models now, not reactively.
- Those who understand both timelines (market panic and enterprise reality) will capture asymmetric value by positioning in the gap.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| CFOs and Boards | Portfolio exposure to SaaS companies needs reevaluation |
| Enterprise buyers | Vendor contracts and pricing models will shift within 12 to 18 months |
| Software companies | Per-seat pricing must evolve to outcome-based, subscription, or token-based models |
| Strategy leaders | The two-timeline gap creates a 12 to 24 month preparation window |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** $800 billion aggregate SaaS valuation correction (VentureBeat, February 2026)
- **Source credibility:** VentureBeat, The AI Daily Brief, and McKinsey all documenting the repricing. Stock data is public and verifiable.
- **Supporting data:** Salesforce down 21%, HubSpot down 36%, Snowflake down 23% (The AI Daily Brief, February 2026). Over 70% of enterprises prefer alternative pricing models (McKinsey). Peter Steinberger (OpenClaw creator) projects 80% of applications will become obsolete.

### Your Angle

The repricing is not speculative. I have been tracking the specific correlation between capability demonstrations (Claude Cowork legal plugin, Codex 5.3 release, Opus 4.6) and stock price reactions. The pattern: accessibility triggers repricing, not raw capability. The market did not panic when models got smarter. It panicked when Cowork made agent capabilities accessible to non-developers. If you understand both timelines, you can help organizations prepare before the disruption arrives at enterprise pace.

### Pivot Phrases

- "There has been an $800 billion correction in SaaS valuations in the past few weeks..."
- "The market is pricing in a two to three year disruption timeline, but enterprise adoption is a three to five year cycle..."
- "The gap between those timelines is where the opportunity lives..."

## Talking Point 3: Agent-Native Development Is Here

### The Point

Software development is restructuring around agents as first-class individual contributors, not as tools layered onto existing workflows. Five independent teams (Cursor, Anthropic, GitHub, Every, and OpenAI) converged on nearly identical architectural patterns within weeks. Agent Teams is now a first-party SDK feature in Claude Code. This is not a research trend. It is operational.

### Why It Matters

- Organizations optimizing for "AI-assisted" workflows will be outcompeted by those building agent-native architectures.
- The problem discovery bottleneck has shifted: the scarcest resource in software organizations is now identifying valuable problems, not implementing solutions.
- At $4,000 per month in token spend per engineer with a three to four times output multiplier, agent teams are economically viable at enterprise scale.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| Engineering leaders | Development processes need to be restructured around agents as primary developers |
| CTOs | The "AI-assisted" framing is already outdated. Agent-native is the paradigm. |
| Product leaders | Problem identification becomes more valuable than implementation capacity |
| Hiring managers | The hiring profile shifts from implementation skill to specification and evaluation skill |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** Cursor sustains 1M+ lines of code across 1,000+ files over weeks with planner-worker hierarchy
- **Source credibility:** Cursor, Anthropic, and GitHub are all production teams building at scale, not research demos.
- **Supporting data:** Anthropic's 16-agent C compiler generates production-grade code at $20,000 token cost. Agent Teams is productionized in Claude Code (v2.1.34+). Prompt engineering (not model capability) is the dominant variable in system behavior.

### Your Angle

I have been building with these tools during my transition out of Capital Group. The shift from "asking AI questions" to "assigning AI tasks" is visceral. The Level Up Matcher and CRIS system I am building right now use agent-native patterns. The experience confirms what the research says: the bottleneck is specification precision. Describing the system precisely is the new core skill.

### Pivot Phrases

- "Five independent teams arrived at the same architecture within weeks..."
- "This is already a first-party SDK feature, not a research project..."
- "The new bottleneck is not implementation. It is problem identification..."

## Talking Point 4: Skills Endure, Execution Gets Commoditized

### The Point

AI transformation does not make human skills obsolete. It changes how and where they are applied. Seventy percent of current skills transfer across automation boundaries (McKinsey, November 2025). The differentiator is not tool-specific knowledge (which has shrinking half-life) but judgment, taste, and specification precision. Design engineering (craft, the final 10%, behavioral animations) provides a practitioner-level example of what endures.

### Why It Matters

- Workers shift from executing tasks to framing problems, interpreting AI outputs, and making judgment calls on ambiguous decisions.
- The equity concern is real: Anthropic's finding of near-perfect correlation between human and AI education years means AI amplifies existing advantages rather than democratizing opportunity.
- Organizations need to invest in adaptive meta-skills (critical thinking, learning agility, resilience) not tool-specific training.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| CHROs and L&D leaders | Training programs need to shift from tool proficiency to judgment and specification skills |
| Individual contributors | Career resilience depends on adaptive skills, not tool mastery |
| Educators | Curriculum design needs to protect critical thinking while incorporating AI tools |
| Designers and creatives | Taste and craft become the moat when execution is commoditized |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** 70% of current skills transfer across automation boundaries (McKinsey, O*NET task analysis, November 2025)
- **Source credibility:** McKinsey's analysis uses O*NET occupational data, the standard for workforce analysis.
- **Supporting data:** Near-perfect correlation (r>0.92) between human and AI education years (Anthropic, January 2026). 17 percentage point reduction in conceptual mastery when junior developers use AI assistance (Anthropic study via Theo, February 2026). Design engineering craft and behavioral animations remain non-automatable (Koch, DuckDuckGo, February 2026).

### Your Angle

With 24 years in Product Design and Design Thinking facilitation, I have seen multiple technology transitions. The pattern is consistent: execution gets cheaper, judgment gets more valuable. This time is different in speed but not in direction. The design thinking toolkit (problem framing, rapid validation, stakeholder alignment) maps directly to the specification precision and evaluation rigor that now constitute 85% of post-AI engineering work.

### Pivot Phrases

- "The McKinsey data on skill portability reframes the whole conversation..."
- "Here is the counterintuitive part: the more powerful the tools, the more valuable the human judgment..."
- "Design engineering gives us a concrete example of what endures..."

## Talking Point 5: The Layoff Timing as Skills Arbitrage

### The Point

Being released from enterprise constraints during the December 2025 to February 2026 inflection creates a time-limited competitive advantage. Large enterprises restrict access to frontier AI tools for legitimate governance reasons. But this creates a growing skills gap: enterprise employees are trained to last year's bar while the proficiency bar rises every six months. People outside enterprise walls can experiment freely with the most powerful tools available.

### Why It Matters

- The window is two to six months: by Q2 2026, enterprise deployments will standardize enough that the arbitrage advantage narrows.
- Self-acceleration loops widen the gap: Codex 5.3 used in its own creation means capabilities expand faster than enterprises can evaluate and approve them.
- Deliberate project-based learning (building real products, not just exploring tools) compounds the advantage.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| Hiring managers | Candidates with frontier tool experience during this window bring skills that enterprise-locked teams lack |
| Enterprise AI leaders | The skills gap between locked-down employees and independent practitioners is structural, not temporary |
| Career transitioners | This is a window to build portfolio proof points that demonstrate agent-native capabilities |
| Consulting buyers | Independent practitioners bring frontier experience that internal teams cannot yet replicate |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** Training moves people to experimenter (40/100 proficiency) but not practitioner. Only 2.7% reach practitioner-level proficiency (Section, January 2026).
- **Source credibility:** Direct experience corroborated by Section's enterprise-wide data.
- **Supporting data:** ICs have least access (32% vs. 80% C-suite), least training, least reimbursement (Section). 12% of enterprises truly scaled; 50% planning significant investment but lagging (McKinsey). Self-acceleration loops are operational: Codex was used in its own creation.

### Your Angle

This is my current lived experience. I am building the Level Up Matcher, managing the CRIS research system, and developing consulting deliverables using Claude Code, Cowork, and multi-agent patterns that I could not access inside Capital Group. Every week of hands-on experience with these tools creates compounding returns. The question I can answer that most enterprise-locked practitioners cannot: what does it feel like to work with agents as team members, not as tools?

### Pivot Phrases

- "Something I am experiencing right now that most enterprise employees cannot..."
- "The skills gap between enterprise-locked and independent practitioners is structural..."
- "This is a window that closes within months..."
