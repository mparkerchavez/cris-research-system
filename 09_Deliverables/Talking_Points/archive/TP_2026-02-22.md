## TP1: "The Specification Crisis: Why Your AI Agents Fail Before They Start"

### The Point
Your AI agents are phenomenal at executing specifications. That's not actually helping you. Amazon's Cairo research shows the real bottleneck is specification writing, not code generation. Your developers can ask Claude to build anything—but organizations routinely ask it to build the wrong thing perfectly, then spend millions scaling the mistake. The crisis isn't agent disobedience. It's specification paralysis.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** Specification failure compounds at scale. A badly specified agent doesn't produce a bad result, it produces a bad result at 10x velocity with minimal cost. You've shifted the failure mode from slow, expensive mistakes to fast, cheap catastrophes.

**Strategic:** Organizations mastering specification will own the next decade of software development. Those that treat specification as an afterthought will experience a bifurcation: high-value specification workers commanding extraordinary pricing, and commoditized workers doing faster work but not different work.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Concern | Conversation Hook |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| Engineering VPs | Budget and schedule risk | "You're about to triple velocity on the wrong problem—how do you know what problem to solve?" |
| Product Leaders | Alignment between vision and execution | "Your agents will execute your spec flawlessly. Is your spec actually right?" |
| CTOs | Governance and technical debt | "The new debt isn't code. It's bad specifications you executed at scale." |
| Design Thinking Teams | Process and methodology | "This is where your facilitation and specification disciplines become your competitive moat." |

### Evidence
- Amazon's Cairo requires developers to write testable specifications before implementation—forcing intent clarity before code generation begins. [AINewsStrategyDaily_JobMarketSplit DP3: "forcing developers to write a testable specification before any code gets generated"]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/AINewsStrategyDaily_JobMarketSplit_2026-02-15.md;dp=3 -->

- AI-generated code produces 1.7x more logic errors than human code, yet Google DORA data shows 90% AI adoption growth correlated with 9% bug rate increase. The gap isn't execution fidelity; it's specification quality. [AINewsStrategyDaily_JobMarketSplit DP2: "AI-generated code produces 1.7 times more logic issues than human-written code"]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/AINewsStrategyDaily_JobMarketSplit_2026-02-15.md;dp=2 -->

- Dan Shapiro's five-level framework shows the psychological inflection point happens at Level 2 (pairing)—where developers feel productive—but the specification crisis emerges at Level 3, when developers become code reviewers, and Level 4, when they become specification arbiters. [DanShapiro_FiveLevelsAI DP4: "level 2 feels like you are done. But you are not done"]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/DanShapiro_FiveLevelsAI_2026-02-22.md;dp=4 -->

- Cost of production is collapsing while cost of specification is compounding. As Claude Code itself writes 90% of Claude Code, the real bottleneck shifts from "how do we generate code" to "how do we know what code to ask for." [AINewsStrategyDaily_JobMarketSplit DP5: "cost of not knowing what to build...is compounding much faster than production cost is falling"]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/AINewsStrategyDaily_JobMarketSplit_2026-02-15.md;dp=5 -->

### Your Angle
I've spent 24 years in design thinking and facilitation. This is where that discipline shifts from "nice to have" to existential. I've watched product teams struggle with specification because they're trained to be builders, not definers. That's ending. In every enterprise I'm talking to, the teams that succeed are the ones that treat specification writing like software engineering: acceptance criteria, testability, measurability, systems thinking.

At Capital Group, we did this in portfolio strategy: portfolios moved from living in decks to living in models with defined inputs, testable assumptions, measurable outputs. That pattern is now the only pattern that works when you're coordinating with agents. Your specification quality is your competitive moat. Teams that can't specify precisely will find themselves outpaced by teams that can.

### Pivot Phrases
- "Let's talk about what failure looks like when your agent executes the wrong thing perfectly."
- "Here's where your design thinking practice becomes your actual competitive advantage."
- "If you're not treating specification as engineering discipline, you're betting on getting lucky."
- "The bifurcation in your workforce isn't between engineers and non-engineers. It's between people who can specify precisely and people who can't."

## TP2: "Nobody Wants This: The Demand-Side Problem AI Can't Solve with Better Models"

### The Point
Your financial models for AI assume demand is a constant. They're wrong. Less than a third of Americans trust AI. Majority sentiment is negative. More than half believe AI will reduce jobs. More than half think AI negatively affects our ability to do things. That's not noise. That's foundational demand destruction occurring underneath record capital deployment. And it's bipartisan: organized opposition groups span 55% Republican, 45% Democrat. $64 billion in data center construction has been blocked by local opposition in under two years.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** Your TAM assumption is disconnected from reality. Modest shifts in population demand for AI (a 10-15% swing toward positive sentiment) could eliminate hundreds of billions in projected revenue. That's not being priced in. Supply-side constraints (chips, capital, energy) are well-covered in financial models. Demand elasticity is invisible.

**Strategic:** The greatest barrier to AI isn't technology. It's whether people actually want it. At that scale, marketing and communications become as important as capability. Zuckerberg attempted to hire the most credible engineer in agentic development (Peter Steinberger, OpenClaw creator) through personal engagement and technical credibility. He lost to OpenAI. That matters because it signals that even peak-credibility personal outreach doesn't overcome demand questions at the consumer level.

### Who Cares

| Board Members | Revenue assumptions and TAM | "Your AI revenue forecast doesn't account for demand destruction. Here's what that gap looks like." |
| Strategic Leaders | Market positioning | "If demand-side bottlenecks are real, your competitive advantage shifts from capability to trust." |
| Public Affairs Teams | Regulatory and reputational risk | "142 activist groups organized against data center construction. That's a governance signal, not noise." |
| Finance Teams | Risk-adjusted returns | "The sensitivity analysis on adoption rates should be your primary scenario, not a downside case." |

### Evidence

Peter Steinberger built OpenClaw and immediately proved credibility by routing sponsorship money to dependencies instead of pockets, demonstrating he wasn't in it for extraction.

That's the model that works in demand-constrained markets. For enterprises, the implication is clear: your communication strategy can't be "AI is amazing." It has to be "here's a problem we solved using AI, here's the result, here's how we built trust with stakeholders." That's storytelling rooted in design thinking—which is exactly what Super by Design can help nonprofits and enterprises do.

The bifurcation won't be between AI and non-AI companies. It'll be between companies that understand demand barriers and integrate that understanding into their adoption strategy, versus companies that treat adoption as a capital deployment problem.

## TP3: "The Productivity Paradox: 150% More Output, 19% Slower Per Task"

### The Point
Your organization will experience a profound paradox in AI adoption. Overall productivity will skyrocket: Anthropic achieved 150% productivity growth per engineer since Claude Code launched six months ago. Your revenue per employee could triple. But individual task execution will slow: Census research shows 1.3 percentage point average productivity drop during adoption, with some firms dropping 60 percentage points before recovery. You're experiencing work intensification, not efficiency. Output per unit increases, but time per unit of work increases too.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** This is where most adoptions fail. Your employees feel slower. Your managers see individual task duration increase. They conclude the tool isn't working and pull back, right when they should be redesigning the work. The J-curve is real, and it takes 18-24 months to compress.

**Strategic:** Organizations mastering this transition will experience unprecedented leverage: 10x-80x revenue-per-employee gaps between early adopters of agent orchestration and traditional organizations. Organizations that misread the paradox as tool failure will be outpaced.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Concern | Conversation Hook |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| CHROs | Employee experience and retention | "Your team will feel slower before they feel faster. How do you communicate that?" |
| Operations Leaders | Process redesign | "Productivity gains require end-to-end workflow redesign. What does that look like in your org?" |
| CFOs | ROI and patience capital | "The adoption J-curve compresses to 18-24 months. Is your capital structure patient enough?" |
| Project Managers | Timeline and milestone planning | "Individual tasks will take longer. Project structure needs to account for that." |

### Evidence
- Anthropic engineering productivity grew 150% since Claude Code launch (6 months), measured by pull request volume normalized for team size. This occurred as team size doubled, meaning 70% per-engineer productivity growth despite larger team. [YCombinator_BorisChernyClaudeCode DP11: "since Claude Code came out, productivity per engineer at Anthropic has grown 150%"]

Broader Anthropic ranges 70-90%, but 100% is achieved reality for high-skill users with current models, not aspirational. [YCombinator_BorisChernyClaudeCode DP12: "For me personally it's been 100% since Opus 4.5"]

- Census research shows AI deployment initially reduces productivity by average 1.3 percentage points, with some firms dropping as much as 60 points before recovery, following a J-curve pattern compressing into 18-24 months across the economy. [AINewsStrategyDaily_JobMarketSplit DP17, DP18: "AI deployment initially reduces productivity...some firms dropping as much as 60 points"]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/AINewsStrategyDaily_JobMarketSplit_2026-02-15.md;dp=17,18 -->

- While overall productivity grows, time per task increases: AI adoption correlates with work intensification where employees spend more time on individual tasks despite higher output volume. Early-stage AI users experience task duration increase (19% average) while output accelerates. [AIDaily_ProductivityBoomData DP16: "people using AI actually spent more time on task"]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/AIDaily_ProductivityBoomData_2026-02-17.md;dp=16 -->

### Your Angle
In my 24 years designing product and facilitating organizational change, I've seen this pattern before with major process redesigns. The difference is speed: adoption J-curves used to span 3-5 years. Now they're compressed to 18 months. That compression is where design thinking pays off.

The team that masters work intensification—that understands they'll produce more output but spend more time on individual tasks—wins. It's counterintuitive. Most organizations fight it. But the path through is organizational design: flattening hierarchies, reducing coordination overhead, building systems for rapid specification and feedback.

That's the exact problem I solved at Capital Group, and it's the exact problem Super by Design helps nonprofits and enterprises navigate. The productivity gains are real. But they require intentional redesign.

## TP4: "Level 2 Is the New Normal: Why 90% of Organizations Are Stuck"

### The Point
Dan Shapiro's five-level AI maturity framework maps cognitive transitions from tool-user (Level 0) to code-reviewer (Level 3) to specification-author (Level 4) to fully autonomous (Level 5). Ninety percent of "AI-native" developers are stuck at Level 2, where they're pairing with AI as an equal colleague. They feel productive. They think they're done. They're not even close. Level 5—fully autonomous systems—already exists at scale with small teams. The frontier is operating at that level. The infrastructure, governance, and skill architecture required to move from Level 2 to Level 4 is where competitive moats get built and where most organizations quit.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** SWE-Bench (coding task benchmark) improved 16x in 12 months. The frontier moved from 4% to 71% task completion in a single year. But enterprise adoption is still clustering at Level 2. That gap is where venture-backed startups with small teams will outpace large organizations. You have 18 months maximum to move up the maturity curve before the capability overhang becomes a competitive collapse.

**Strategic:** The Level 4-to-5 transition requires specification discipline, skill crafting for agents, and willingness to grant autonomy. Most organizations don't have that muscle. Building it is the work of this year.

### Who Cares

| Digital Transformation Leaders | Adoption benchmarking | "Where are your teams on the five-level scale? Most will say 3 or 4 but actually be at 2." |
| Innovation Leaders | Capability assessment | "The gap between what's possible and what you're using is enormous. Here's how to measure it." |

### Evidence
- Dan Shapiro's framework identifies Level 2 (pairing with AI as equal colleague) as psychological inflection point where developers feel productive but remain far from frontier capabilities. What separates frontier organizations from the rest is the willingness to go to Level 3 (where you feel slower as a manager of AI output), then to Level 4 (where your role fundamentally changes to specification and skill authoring). ## TP5: "Context Is the New Data: Why Your AI Strategy Needs a Context Engineer"

### The Point
In 2025, data quality was the constraint on machine learning systems. In 2026, context quality is the constraint on agentic systems. Thirty-two percent of agent interruptions stem from missing or insufficient context about customers, not model capability failures. Your AI agents are flying blind. Luxury clienteling sales (thinking through individual customer behavioral signals, timing, preferences, finite budgets) demonstrates the principle: an agent needs to process vastly more data than a human salesman can hold in memory, but only if that data is strategically filtered and curated. Most organizations flooding agents with irrelevant details will fail. Organizations mastering context engineering will own the agentic decade.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** Context engineering is the primary lever for scaling personalization from bespoke luxury service to software platforms. A Goyard sales rep doesn't memorize everything—she filters for what matters. That filtering discipline moves from art to science through strategic data curation, ML clustering to identify high-value customer attributes, and operational integration of context into agent prompts.

**Strategic:** Your competitive differentiation in 2026-27 shifts from model access to context quality. Everyone will have access to Claude Opus 4.5 or better. The organizations that win are the ones that understand their customers deeply enough to compress that understanding into context that amplifies agent capability.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Concern | Conversation Hook |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| CDOs | Data strategy and integration | "Your data strategy needs to shift from 'collect everything' to 'curate what matters.'" |
| Data Leaders | Context infrastructure | "Context engineering is as important as data engineering now. Are you building for it?" |
| Product Strategy Teams | Differentiation and moats | "Model parity is permanent. Context differentiation is your only moat." |
| Customer Experience Teams | Personalization at scale | "This is how you deliver Goyard-level personalization at software platform scale." |

### Evidence
- AI agents' primary limitation in 2026 is insufficient context about customers, not raw model capability. Anthropic research identifies context as the irreducible bottleneck in agent deployment. [Every_LuxuryHandbagsContextProblem DP1: "They're all missing something. You guessed it: context"]

- Luxury sales organizations like Goyard use behavioral context (knowing exact customer activity timing) to drive purchase decisions at psychological moments—a pattern now executable at scale through context engineering for AI agents. [Every_LuxuryHandbagsContextProblem DP2, DP6: "Your rep texted you on Friday at 8 because you told her you'd be out drinking cocktails" and context engineering acts as primary lever for scaling]
<!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_LuxuryHandbagsContextProblem_2026-02-20.md;dp=2,6 -->

- Strategic data curation is required to maximize signal without causing hallucination: agents have finite context budgets that degrade when overloaded. Machine learning techniques like K-means clustering identify which customer attributes correlate with high-value outcomes. [Every_LuxuryHandbagsContextProblem DP5, DP7: "Give them too much, and they get overwhelmed and start to hallucinate" and ML techniques identify which dimensions distinguish best customers]

### Your Angle
My design thinking and user research background prepared me exactly for this problem. Context engineering isn't a technical problem. It's a sensemaking problem: understanding what information actually matters in your specific domain, how to operationalize that understanding, and how to discipline data curation against the temptation to dump everything.

I've done this work at Capital Group in portfolio design—understanding which dimensions of market data actually drive portfolio performance, which are noise. That same discipline applies to customer context for agents. The Goyard example is perfect: they're not storing everything. They're filtering for what matters at that critical decision moment.

For Super by Design consulting work with nonprofits, this is urgent. Nonprofits operate on limited budgets. The ones that win in the agentic era will be the ones that get context right: understanding their constituents, their timing, their needs at enough fidelity to amplify agent capability without overwhelming systems. That's exactly the design discipline I can help you build.
