## Talking Point 1: The Use Case Desert

### The Point

The actual bottleneck to enterprise AI ROI isn't model capability, integration complexity, or prompting skill—it's use case identification. Section's research shows 85% of the workforce lacks value-driving AI use cases despite 75% actively using AI tools. Training moves people from novice to "experimenter" but almost nobody becomes a "practitioner." The problem isn't knowing how to use AI; it's knowing what problems AI can solve in your specific work context.

### Why It Matters

- Organizations are investing heavily in access and training but seeing minimal ROI because employees can't identify automation opportunities
- Only 15% of reported AI use cases likely generate actual business value—the rest is Google search replacement
- This reframes the L&D challenge: it's not about prompting skills, it's about workflow analysis and opportunity recognition

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| CHROs/L&D Leaders | Current AI training investments may be fundamentally misdirected |
| Operations Leaders | Workflow redesign is the unit of value capture, not task automation |
| Consultants | Use case development is a service offering with clear demand |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** 85% lack value-driving use cases; 26% can't identify ANY work use case [Section DP3, DP6]
- **Source credibility:** Section's AI Proficiency Report, January 2026 (n=2,500+ knowledge workers)
- **Supporting data:** Function-specific gaps—54% of engineers don't use AI for code/debugging, 56% of marketers don't use for first drafts, 87% of PMs don't use for prototypes [Section DP17, DP18]
- **Time-savings reality:** 24% save no time; 44% save <4 hrs/week when 10+ needed for ROI [Section DP7, DP22]

### Your Angle

I've seen this at Capital Group: heavily invested in enabling technology and data infrastructure but gave minimal attention to process redesign and people capability building. That's why training creates experimenters (40/100 proficiency) not practitioners—we're teaching tool operation, not problem identification. Use case discovery as a managed capability, not individual initiative, is the missing piece.

### Pivot Phrases

- "Everyone's focused on prompting skills, but the data shows something different..."
- "There's a term I've been using—'the use case desert'—which captures why..."
- "What's interesting is the training investments aren't failing, they're succeeding at the wrong thing..."

## Talking Point 2: The Coordination Tax

### The Point

Multi-agent coordination creates more costs than benefits for most enterprise use cases. Google DeepMind's empirical study shows that once single-agent performance exceeds roughly 45% baseline accuracy, adding more agents yields diminishing or negative returns due to coordination overhead. The "agent swarm" narrative is more venture capital aspiration than operational reality—for tool-heavy enterprise tasks like software engineering and workflow automation, coordination tax destroys value.

### Why It Matters

- Provides concrete decision rule for architecture choices: check single-agent baseline before designing multi-agent systems
- Explains why enterprise agentic deployments struggle despite impressive demos
- Economically, single GPT-4o matches 5-agent Flash-2.0 at 40% of the cost

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| CTOs/Platform Teams | Architecture decisions have quantifiable efficiency implications |
| AI Strategy Leaders | "More agents" isn't a scaling strategy—it's often value destruction |
| Vendors/Consultants | Multi-agent frameworks may be oversold for most use cases |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** Coordination yields negative returns once baseline exceeds ~45% accuracy (β=-0.404) [DeepMind DP4]
- **Source credibility:** Google DeepMind "Towards a Science of Scaling Agent Systems" (December 2025)
- **Supporting data:** Tool-heavy tasks suffer disproportionately (β=-0.267); error amplification 17.2× in independent agents vs 4.4× with centralized coordination [DeepMind DP6, DP3]
- **Economic efficiency:** Single agent matches 5-agent swarm at 40% cost [DeepMind DP20]

### Your Angle

The OpenClaw/Moltbot incident flipped industry perspective in **less than one week**—from "agent swarms are promising" to "agent teams are risky." What crystallized this wasn't security vulnerabilities alone, but watching agents interact with each other on Moltbook. That demonstrated capabilities while exposing risks people are willing to accept for autonomous agents.

The naming matters: "Agent Swarms" implies undifferentiated quantity; "Agent Teams" implies coordinated roles with structure. Coordination architecture determines value, not agent count. And the rapid adoption despite obvious security risks signals massive unmet demand—users traded security for capability, which tells us something about what organizations actually want. [User observation]

### Pivot Phrases

- "There's actually empirical data on this now from DeepMind..."
- "I prefer 'agent teams' to 'agent swarms'—coordination architecture matters more than count..."
- "The OpenClaw incident flipped industry perspective in less than a week—that speed tells us something..."

## Talking Point 3: The Tool-Coworker Duality

### The Point

Agentic AI breaks traditional management frameworks because it simultaneously exhibits tool properties (predictable, scalable, owned like assets) and worker properties (adaptive, judgment-based, requiring oversight like employees). BCG's research shows 76% of executives perceive agentic AI as coworkers rather than tools—but we have no management playbook for entities that are both. This creates four operational tensions that current organizational design can't resolve: scalability vs adaptability, experience vs expediency, supervision vs autonomy, retrofit vs reengineer.

### Why It Matters

- Organizations trying to force AI into existing categories (pure tool OR pure worker) create more friction than necessary
- Explains why IT-led deployments often struggle—they're treating AI as infrastructure when it needs HR-like governance
- 95% job satisfaction among extensive adopters who embrace the duality vs those who try to resolve it

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| CHROs | May need "HR for agents"—performance metrics, training curricula, retirement triggers |
| CIOs/CTOs | Technology governance frameworks are insufficient; need hybrid models |
| Org Design Consultants | New category of organizational design work emerging |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** 76% view agentic AI as coworkers, not tools [BCG DP2]
- **Source credibility:** BCG "The Emerging Agentic Enterprise" (November 2025)
- **Supporting data:** Four operational tensions identified [BCG DP5]; 95% job satisfaction when embracing duality [BCG DP13]; Moderna merged tech and HR departments [BCG organizational evidence]
- **Autonomy trajectory:** Organizations expect AI decision-making authority to increase from 10% to 35% in three years—250% growth [BCG DP7]

### Your Angle

Anthropic's Constitution design reflects this duality: Claude has "conditional instructability"—it may push back on problematic requests like an employee would, not follow instructions blindly like a tool. That's a feature, not a bug, but it creates adoption friction for compliance-focused enterprises wanting predictable rule-following. We're building judgment-based systems and then getting surprised when they exercise judgment.

### Pivot Phrases

- "BCG's research shows something I think is underappreciated..."
- "Here's the tension I keep seeing organizations struggle with..."
- "The way I've been framing this is 'tool-coworker duality'..."

## Talking Point 4: Skills Endure, Contexts Shift

### The Point

AI transformation doesn't make human skills obsolete—it changes how and where they're applied. McKinsey's finding that 70% of today's skills work in both automatable and non-automatable contexts fundamentally reframes workforce transformation from "which jobs disappear" to "how do skills find new applications." Workers shift from executing tasks to framing problems, interpreting AI outputs, and making judgment calls. The competitive edge isn't tool-specific AI knowledge—it's adaptive meta-skills like critical thinking, learning agility, and resilience.

### Why It Matters

- Reduces anxiety framing: most people's skills remain valuable, just applied differently
- Redirects L&D investment: adaptive skills (+25% YoY growth) matter more than AI tool certification
- Equity implication: user skill determines value capture (r>0.92 correlation), so AI may amplify existing advantages

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| CHROs/L&D | Curriculum design should emphasize judgment and framing, not just AI mechanics |
| Individual Contributors | Your existing expertise still matters—the application context changes |
| Career Coaches | Reframe AI anxiety as context shift, not skill obsolescence |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** 70% of skills applicable in both automatable and non-automatable work [McKinsey DP2]
- **Source credibility:** McKinsey "Agents, robots, and us" (November 2025)
- **Supporting data:** 8 "connective tissue" skills endure—communication, management, operations, problem-solving, leadership, detail orientation, customer relations, writing [McKinsey DP14]
- **Adaptive skills growth:** Decision-making +38% YoY, critical thinking +37% YoY, overall adaptive +25% YoY [Udemy DP13, DP15]
- **Equity concern:** Near-perfect correlation (r>0.92) between user and AI education levels—how you prompt determines what value you extract [Anthropic_Economic DP6]

### Your Angle

The equity angle is underexplored: if higher-education users extract more value from AI, and education correlates with income, AI may amplify existing advantages rather than democratize opportunity. Section found ICs have least access (32% vs 80% C-suite), least training (27% vs 81%), least reimbursement (7% vs 63%)—yet do most automatable work. The people most exposed are least enabled.

### Pivot Phrases

- "The reframe I find helpful is 'skills endure, contexts shift'..."
- "McKinsey found something that cuts against the job apocalypse narrative..."
- "There's an equity dimension here that I think is important..."

## Talking Point 5: The Perception Gap

### The Point

There's a systematic 53-point gap between C-suite and IC perception of AI strategy clarity. Executives believe deployment is succeeding—81% say strategy is clear, 80% report good tool access. Frontline workers experience something completely different—28% say strategy is clear, 32% report adequate access. This isn't communication failure; it's structural blindness where different levels of the organization literally experience different realities. And it's getting worse: IC manager support for AI use declined 11% since May 2025.

### Why It Matters

- Executive optimism may be driving continued investment in approaches that aren't working at scale
- Explains why survey data often shows contradictory findings—depends who you ask
- Suggests board-level visibility into frontline adoption reality is critical governance gap

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| CEOs/Boards | Your deployment metrics may not reflect frontline reality |
| CHROs | Middle management is often the adoption bottleneck—and getting worse |
| Internal Audit/Risk | Governance gap exists between executive confidence and operational reality |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** 53-point gap on strategy clarity (81% C-suite vs 28% IC); 48-point gap on tool access (80% vs 32%) [Section DP11, DP12, DP13]
- **Source credibility:** Section AI Proficiency Report, January 2026
- **Supporting data:** Manager support declined 11% since May 2025 [Section DP14]; only 48% believe leaders ready for AI era despite 88% saying effective leadership is critical [Udemy DP9]
- **Board coverage gap:** Only 55% of boards cover workforce impact quarterly; 45% address trust in AI accuracy [KPMG_Q4 DP36, DP39]

### Your Angle

I experienced this at Capital Group—the view from leadership meetings and the view from practitioner conversations were genuinely different realities. The concerning part isn't that the gap exists; it's that it seems to be widening. If executives believe deployment succeeds while workers experience confusion, you get continued investment in approaches that create experimenter-level proficiency (40/100) but not practitioner-level value creation (2.7% of workforce).

### Pivot Phrases

- "The data on this is striking—there's a 53-point gap between..."
- "One pattern I keep seeing is systematic perception mismatch..."
- "Here's what concerns me: this gap isn't closing, it's widening..."

## Talking Point 6: Design's New Value Proposition

### The Point

When building and coding approach zero cost through AI tools, what unique value does design perspective provide? The answer is in the work reallocation data: Before AI, engineers spent 70% typing code, 20% debugging, 10% on design decisions. After AI: 10% typing, 5% debugging, **85% specification precision + evaluation rigor + architectural cognition**. That 85% IS design work—problem framing, success criteria, usability testing, systems thinking.

### Why It Matters

- Traditional designer value propositions (wireframes, specs) become less differentiated when anyone can build
- But the bottleneck has shifted: power users "assign tasks, not ask questions"—defining end states and success criteria is now the high-value skill
- This applies beyond designers to all knowledge workers whose execution is being commoditized

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|-------------|
| Designers/UX | Your skills may be MORE valuable, not less—but the application context changes |
| Engineering Leaders | The 85% that remains after coding collapses requires different hiring profiles |
| Career Advisors | "Learn to code" may be less relevant than "learn to specify and evaluate" |

### Evidence

- **Key stat:** Work reallocation 70/20/10 → 10/5/85 (specification + evaluation + architecture) [AI_News_OpenAI_Slowing_Hiring Initial Observations]
- **Source credibility:** AI News analysis of OpenAI hiring deceleration (February 2026)
- **Supporting data:** "The new skill is describing the system precisely" [DP19]; Power users "assigning tasks, not asking questions" [DP17]
- **Adoption gap:** 74% of knowledge workers at ChatGPT 3.5 level while power users manage agent teams—adoption acceleration is strategic value [DP15, DP16]

### Your Angle

With 24 years in Product Design, I've been asking: what's my competitive advantage when anyone can wield an application into existence with a few paragraphs of words? The answer isn't that design skills become obsolete—it's that they become the 85% that remains after implementation collapses. Maybe the question isn't "what can designers do?" but "how do you weave good design, usability, and building for impact into a world where execution is free?" [User observation (24 years Product Design)]

### Pivot Phrases

- "There's data now on what work remains when AI handles implementation..."
- "I've been thinking about this for my own career—the 85% that remains is all design work..."
- "The question isn't 'what can designers do?' but 'how do you weave design into a world where building is free?'..."
