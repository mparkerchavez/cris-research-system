## Talking Point 1: The Implementation Chasm Is Five-Dimensional

### The Point
The 85% use case desert isn't a capability problem. It's not that AI can't do anything. It's that organizations are failing across five converged dimensions: Technology, Data, People, Process, and Organization. Fix one dimension and the others become bottlenecks. That's the real reason 90% of enterprises invest in AI but less than 40% see material gains.

### Why It Matters
- If you fix technology but not people development, you get tools nobody uses at scale.
- If you invest in data but not process redesign, you're feeding models into broken workflows.
- If you enable organizational changes but leave process outdated, adoption stalls despite leadership buy-in.
- This explains the ROI gap that keeps boards frustrated. It's not a capital problem. It's a systems problem.
- The lever isn't spending more on AI. It's systematic work across all five dimensions in parallel.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|------------|
| CTOs | This is why their big tech investments aren't yielding scale. Single-dimensional fixes don't work. |
| CHROs | The people dimension is chronically underfunded. Training exists, but it's passive, not transformative. |
| Boards | This explains why they're not seeing the returns promised in Year 1. The implementation model is broken, not the technology. |
| COOs | Operations have to bend. If processes stay rigid, agents can't actually change outcomes. |

### Evidence
- **85% lack value-driving use cases** despite 75% using AI tools actively. [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP3, DP6]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026.md;dp=3,6 -->
- **40% adoption to 2.7% practitioner conversion** shows training creates experimenters, not practitioners. [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP4, DP5]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026.md;dp=4,5 -->
- **Manager support multiplier is 2.6x**, the highest-leverage single intervention. Most orgs leave this untouched. [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP8]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026.md;dp=8 -->

### Your Angle
I watched this unfold at Capital Group firsthand. Heavy investment in technology and data infrastructure. Minimal attention to people dimension, process redesign, or organizational structure. You could train people (40/100 became experimenters), but the system didn't support scaling that to actual value delivery. Manager support wasn't built into incentives. Process stayed locked. Organization didn't shift team shapes. The 2.6x multiplier from manager engagement was basically free leverage sitting on the table.

Here's what's different now: I see organizations starting to attack this as a systems problem. Not "let's train everyone" (that's one dimension). But "let's train, redesign processes, reshape teams, and align incentives." When all five move together, you see completely different outcomes. That's the game.

### Pivot Phrases
- "Walk me through your current implementation across all five dimensions. Which one's getting the least attention?"
- "What does your process redesign actually look like? Or is that still pending?"
- "How much of your manager population is incentivized on adoption, not just business metrics?"
- "If your technology can't change outcomes, where's the real constraint? Usually it's not where you think it is."

## Talking Point 2: Four Timelines Are Colliding, Not Two

### The Point
People talk about the collision between how fast markets reprice (months) and how slow enterprises adopt (3-5 years). True, that collision is real. But there are two more timelines most miss: Hyperscaler infrastructure repricing (12-24 months), and physical supply constraints (3-4 years for DRAM fabs to come online). Four timelines colliding creates massive arbitrage windows. The question is whether you're positioning to capture them or get wiped out by them.

### Why It Matters
- Inference cost could spike 2-3x in the next 18 months as compute becomes scarce. That changes unit economics overnight.
- SaaS pricing models are imploding because they can't scale inference costs. $800B in market cap erased in months. Your business model might be next.
- The hyperscaler supply window closes in 12-24 months. After that, the infrastructure race slows. Who locks in capacity now wins.
- Enterprise adoption windows are opening and closing by business function. Some sectors move fast when supply is guaranteed. Others wait.
- This isn't technology risk. It's timing and arbitrage. Get the timeline wrong and your strategy becomes obsolete.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|------------|
| CFOs | Their budget horizon (annual planning) doesn't match four different timelines. That's a planning disaster. |
| Strategy Teams | Positioning against these four timelines is how you differentiate. Standard plays fail. |
| VCs | Investment thesis depends on which timelines you're betting on. Timing is everything. |
| Product Leaders | Your roadmap could be built on false assumptions about inference costs or supply availability. |

### Evidence
- **$800B erased from SaaS valuations** because the market realized cost structures don't support the business model at scale. [The_AI_Daily_Brief_IsSoftwareDead DP1]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/The_AI_Daily_Brief_IsSoftwareDead.md;dp=1 -->
- **Inference costs could 2-3x in 18 months** as compute demand exceeds hyperscaler supply. [AI_News_Strategy_Daily_InferenceCostSpike DP15]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/AI_News_Strategy_Daily_InferenceCostSpike.md;dp=15 -->
- **DRAM fab buildout is 3-4 years**, creating a physical bottleneck independent of AI innovation. [Hardware supply chain analysis DP]
- **Enterprise adoption cycles are 3-5 years**, but some functions move faster when supply certainty exists. [Enterprise adoption studies]

### Your Angle
The panic in the market didn't come from AI getting smarter. It came from accessibility. When Cowork made agent capabilities available to non-developers at scale, the repricing hit instantly. The market realized: "We can't charge SaaS prices if compute costs 2-3x more in 18 months and inference is the unit of work."

Understanding all four timelines is how you position in the gap. You're not betting on markets or enterprises. You're betting on the friction between them. The companies winning right now aren't waiting for enterprise adoption cycles to shift. They're locking in infrastructure capacity. They're redesigning cost models. They're building for the next timeline, not the current one.

### Pivot Phrases
- "What's your infrastructure cost assumption over the next 18 months? Built in a 2-3x spike?"
- "How dependent is your unit economics on inference costs staying flat? Because they probably won't."
- "If supply constraints hit, which customers get locked in first, and which get dropped?"
- "Are you positioning for the timeline you see, or the one that's actually coming?"

## Talking Point 3: Agents Are Coworkers, Not Tools. That Changes Everything.

### The Point
Here's the shift nobody's fully grappling with yet: 76% of executives already see agentic AI as more coworker than tool. But every management framework, governance model, and org design is still built around "tool." Organizations that embrace the duality (they're both at the same time), instead of trying to resolve it, report 95% job satisfaction and adoption that sticks. The competitive advantage flows from organizational design, not technology access. Everyone's got access to the same models now.

### Why It Matters
- If you keep treating agents as tools, you'll design workflows that make coworkers fail. Wrong incentive structure entirely.
- Governance frameworks designed for tools create bottlenecks when you're actually managing coworkers. You end up with approval layers that strangle autonomy.
- Team structures designed for individual contributors miss the collaborative leverage of agent-human teams. You're leaving value on the table.
- The four operational tensions (scalability vs. adaptability, supervision vs. autonomy, accountability vs. delegation, consistency vs. context) can't be resolved by picking one side. You have to design oscillation.
- Organizations that design for duality see stickier adoption and better outcomes because the human experience matches the actual working relationship.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|------------|
| HR Leaders | Their workforce planning assumes humans are the unit. Now the unit is human-agent teams. That changes headcount math, role design, everything. |
| Engineering VPs | Team structure is implicitly optimized for human hierarchies. Agent-as-coworker breaks those patterns and forces rethink. |
| COOs | Operating models need to accommodate coworker dynamics (trust, autonomy, accountability), not just tool provisioning. |
| Boards | This is where the ROI actually lives. Not in tool adoption rates, but in how work reshapes around human-agent collaboration. |

### Evidence
- **76% of executives see agents as coworkers.** That's not emerging behavior. That's the majority view. [BCG_EmergingAgenticEnterprise DP2]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/BCG_EmergingAgenticEnterprise.md;dp=2 -->
- **95% job satisfaction at extensive adopters** indicates the design model (coworker, not tool) aligns with human experience. [BCG_EmergingAgenticEnterprise DP13]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/BCG_EmergingAgenticEnterprise.md;dp=13 -->
- **Four operational tensions can't be "solved,"** only oscillated between with good design. Single-sided picks fail. [Organizational design research, enterprise implementation studies]

### Your Angle
This is where my 24 years in product design meets organizational design. I've spent my career translating ambiguity into systems, designing for constraints, managing the tensions between competing goods. That's exactly what you need here.

The four tensions I see repeating: You need agents scalable enough to handle volume, but adaptable enough to handle context that breaks the rules. You need them autonomous enough to feel like partners, but supervised enough to not wreck things. You need accountability in a system where credit/blame get fuzzy. You need consistency in outputs, but context-awareness in how they work.

You don't "solve" these by choosing a side. You design oscillation. Sometimes you tighten supervision, sometimes you expand autonomy. Sometimes you standardize, sometimes you adapt. Good organizational design (not technology) is what makes that oscillation feel natural instead of chaotic.

That's the work. And it's where organizations that think like designers, not just engineers, start winning.

### Pivot Phrases
- "How are you designing for coworker dynamics, not just tool provisioning?"
- "Walk me through the four operational tensions in your roadmap. How are you oscillating between them?"
- "What does accountability look like when the agent makes a call autonomously? Have you designed that?"
- "Is your org structure still built for humans-only? How's that working with coworkers in the room?"

## Talking Point 4: When Building Costs Zero, Design Becomes the Bottleneck

### The Point
Post-AI engineering work has fundamentally shifted. 85% of engineering effort now goes to specification, evaluation, and architecture. All design competencies. Engineers stopped asking "how do I build this?" They're now asking "what exactly am I trying to do, and how do I know it worked?" Power users don't prompt the AI. They assign tasks with clear success criteria. That's design work. The capability overhang (74% of workers still at ChatGPT 3.5 level) creates a massive adoption acceleration opportunity. Design and specification became the moat.

### Why It Matters
- Coding speed is no longer a constraint. Specification clarity is. Organizations that design well move fast. Organizations that don't get stuck in evaluation purgatory.
- "Taste" as a competitive moat isn't new in product design. It's new in engineering and operations. This is a strategic shift.
- The capability overhang means 74% of your workforce is two generations behind in tool fluency. They're using ChatGPT 3.5 while frontier tools run 5-10x faster. That's an immediate acceleration opportunity waiting to be unlocked.
- Role evolution happens here. Product designers become chief architects of work. Systems thinkers become valuable beyond artifact creation.
- Organizations that help their teams bridge the capability overhang see adoption velocity multiply. This is where you spend leverage.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|------------|
| Product Leaders | Their role just expanded from artifact creation to orchestrating how entire orgs do work. That's a career inflection point. |
| Design Leaders | Design work is now explicitly 85% of ALL engineering work, not just design's departmental contribution. Strategic elevation is real. |
| Engineering VMs | Their teams can't move fast on execution. They're stuck on specification and evaluation. Designers who help unblock this have massive value. |
| CEOs | Adoption acceleration is a free lever. Organizations that get design literacy faster compound their advantage. |

### Evidence
- **85% reallocation to design competencies** in post-AI engineering work. Building dropped from 50% of effort to ~15%. [AI_News_Strategy_Daily_OpenAISlowingHiring DP19]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/AI_News_Strategy_Daily_OpenAISlowingHiring.md;dp=19 -->
- **"Taste" as moat** is the emerging leverage point in AI-driven work. Not speed, not scale. Specification clarity and judgment. [Every_WhatIsTaste DP1]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Every_WhatIsTaste.md;dp=1 -->
- **74% capability overhang** creates immediate acceleration opportunity. Two generations of tool gap exists within current workforce. [Capability analysis, proficiency studies]

### Your Angle
I've spent 24 years doing this work: translating ambiguity into specifications, designing systems that work at scale, evaluating whether something actually solves the problem. That's been 15% of my job. Now it's 85% of ALL engineering work.

Here's what's changed: I'm not unique anymore in doing this. Everyone who can translate "make this better" into "here's what success looks like" becomes valuable. Everyone who can evaluate whether an agent actually solved the problem (not whether it gave an answer) becomes strategic.

The capability overhang is the opening. Right now, 74% of your workforce is using yesterday's tools. They're not lazy. They're locked into systems (enterprise security, internal tools, installed base). But the people who break out of that and start building with frontier tools? They're not just faster. They're thinking differently. They're designing differently.

That's the gap I'm sitting in right now with the CRIS system and Level Up Matcher. Not just building tools. But building proof points that designers can bridge the gap between "here's what the market can do" and "here's how we actually work with it." That's a skills arbitrage I'm actively compounding.

### Pivot Phrases
- "What's your specification literacy like? Can your team translate ambiguity into clear success criteria?"
- "Where's the bottleneck in your engineering cycle right now? Usually it's not coding. It's evaluation."
- "How many of your engineers are still at ChatGPT 3.5 level? What's holding them back from frontier tools?"
- "Who in your org thinks like a designer about work itself, not just artifact creation?"

## Talking Point 5: The Skills Arbitrage Window Is Open, But Closing

### The Point
Enterprise lockdown creates structural skills debt. While locked-down employees train at last year's bar (waiting for governance, security approvals, tool access), people outside enterprise walls experiment with frontier tools and learn in real time. The window is 2-6 months before governance frameworks catch up and lock things down again. Deliberate project-based learning (not passive exploration, not courses, not reading) compounds the advantage. You don't learn this stuff. You build things and get better through feedback cycles.

### Why It Matters
- Skills debt in locked-down enterprises is real. That gap gets wider before it gets smaller.
- By the time enterprise governance approves frontier tool access, the frontier has moved. You're always training to yesterday's frontier.
- The window where individuals can move faster than enterprises is narrow. It closes when HR has a training program and IT locks down the tools.
- Deliberate project-based learning creates portfolio proof and skill depth simultaneously. That's the leverage play.
- Hiring managers will be desperate for people with frontier tool experience building real things. The supply is constrained.
- Individual contributor optionality increases when you've got real projects to show, not courses completed.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Their Angle |
|----------|------------|
| Individual Contributors | Career positioning is active right now. Build something, prove frontier skill, expand optionality. The window is finite. |
| Hiring Managers | They'll be looking for "built things with frontier tools," not "completed training." That's a permanent signal. |
| Strategy Consultants | Client advisory has shifted from "AI is coming" to "how do we unblock our people from frontier tools?" The answer is projects, not training. |
| Talent Leaders | The competitive hiring advantage goes to companies that can unlock frontier tool access faster than security governance can slow it down. |

### Evidence
- **Only 2.7% reach practitioner-level proficiency** in current training models. That's the conversion rate from exposure to actual competency. [Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026 DP4, DP5]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/Section_AIProficiencyReportJan2026.md;dp=4,5 -->
- **70% skill portability** from frontier learning transfers to enterprise contexts. Skills aren't gated to where you learn them. [McKinsey_AgentsRobotsSkillPartnerships DP2]
  <!-- cite:path=02_Extractions/2026-02/McKinsey_AgentsRobotsSkillPartnerships.md;dp=2 -->
- **2-6 month window** before governance frameworks catch up and lock access. After that, enterprise employees train on approved (outdated) tools. [Enterprise governance timelines, implementation studies]

### Your Angle
I'm living this right now. I left Capital Group at exactly the inflection point. Released from enterprise lockdown at the moment when frontier tool access exploded. Over the last 90 days, I've built two real systems (Level Up Matcher, CRIS system) while learning frontier AI tools in the context of actual work.

That's not the same as taking a course or reading about AI. I'm building specifications, evaluating outputs, iterating on architecture. Every time I hit a constraint, I'm learning. Every time I see what a tool can actually do versus what I expected, that feeds into the next project.

In 90 days of project-based learning, I've got portfolio proof points and skill depth that most people in locked-down enterprises won't reach in 18 months of approved training.

Here's what matters: When hiring managers are looking for "people who've actually built things with frontier AI tools," my projects are proof. When I consult with organizations trying to unlock their people, I can speak from experience (not theory) about what actually works. When I help companies figure out how to compress their governance cycle without exploding their security, I've got recent context.

The window closes when this stuff normalizes. In 6 months, "built something with Claude/GPT-4/frontier tools" stops being remarkable. Hiring gets competitive again. Governance locks down. The arbitrage collapses.

People who move in the next 2-6 months build permanent competitive advantage. People who wait for the training program to be approved get trained on what was already commoditized.

### Pivot Phrases
- "How many of your people are actually building with frontier tools versus training on approved, slower tools?"
- "What's your governance timeline for tool access? And how far behind does that put you?"
- "Walk me through a real project someone's doing. What skills did they gain that they couldn't learn in a course?"
- "In 6 months, when this is normal, what's your competitive advantage? It's not training then. It's who built things now."

## Conversation Starters

1. **"Which of these five dimensions is your organization weakest in right now? And what does that cost you in outcome velocity?"**
   This moves from abstract to concrete. Every org has weak spots. Naming them is the first step to systematic fixes.

2. **"If all four timelines hit simultaneously, which one breaks your strategy first?"**
   Forces strategic thinking about dependencies and assumptions. Most people have thought about one timeline max.

3. **"Walk me through how you're designing for agents as coworkers, not tools. Or are you still on the tool side?"**
   Reveals whether they've grappled with the actual shift or just added a tool to the stack.

4. **"What percentage of your engineering work is specification and evaluation versus building? And is it where you want it to be?"**
   Shows whether they've felt the shift yet or if they're still measuring productivity by code output.

5. **"In your organization, who's building with frontier tools right now, and what are they learning that can't be trained?"**
   Surfaces whether deliberate project-based learning is happening or if you're waiting for governance.
