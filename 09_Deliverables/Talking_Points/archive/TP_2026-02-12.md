## TP1: "AI Does Not Reduce Work. It Intensifies It."

### The Point
The dominant narrative that AI reduces workload is wrong. We're looking at selective evidence and ignoring the full picture. AI intensifies existing work while adding new ambient layers of decision-making, monitoring, and context-switching. Executives celebrate cost reduction while workers experience cognitive overload. This is not a communication problem. This is a structural problem in how we're deploying AI.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** Cost reduction narratives drive Board approval and funding decisions. If your actual outcome is work intensification without corresponding wage investment, you're creating burnout, attrition, and hidden costs that don't show up in the P&L for 12 months.

**Strategic:** Organizations building sustainable AI adoption need to account for the cognitive tax. The companies that acknowledge this and invest in process, tools, and staffing will extract more value and keep their people. The companies that don't will optimize themselves into a retention crisis.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Concern | Conversation Hook |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| **CHRO** | Burnout, retention, legal liability | "Your AI productivity gains come with a hidden cognitive tax. Where are you measuring that?" |
| **CTO** | Productivity metrics are broken | "78% of organizations say traditional productivity metrics are insufficient. Are you in the 78%?" |
| **CEO** | Cost reduction narrative doesn't match reality | "If work is intensified, not reduced, then cost reduction is temporary. When does the bill come due?" |
| **Consultant** | New service: work rebalancing frameworks | "Organizations need help designing AI deployments that don't burn people out." |

### Evidence

- **HBR Study (AI Does Not Reduce Work, It Intensifies It)**
  - DP1: Core thesis: AI doesn't reduce workload; it intensifies existing work and adds ambient layers
  - DP3: Executive celebration of cost reduction contrasts with worker experience of cognitive overload
  - Source credibility: Harvard Business Review is gold standard for enterprise leadership

- **The AI Daily Brief (Why AI Leads to More Work)**
  - DP1: Workload intensification pattern documented across deployments
  - DP7: Specific mechanism: context-switching overhead increases
  - Citation context: Technology trend analysis

- **Gusto Governance Research**
  - Governance maturity gap: 82 points between leaders and laggards
  - Implication: Most organizations lack frameworks to manage the intensification

- **Survey Data: Productivity Metrics**
  - 78% of organizations acknowledge traditional metrics are insufficient
  - This is the admission: we can't measure what's actually happening

### Your Angle
I spent 24 years at Capital Group watching Technology and Data investments deployed without corresponding People and Process investment. The pattern is always the same: you automate the technical layer, workload compounds for the humans who integrate that automation, and nobody measures it because the P&L shows efficiency gains. The intensification is predictable from the four-dimensional framework: Technology without People investment will always intensity work. I see this pattern early now. That's the value I bring to AI adoption strategy.

### Pivot Phrases
- "That productivity number assumes the intensification doesn't have a cost. Does it?"
- "Your next AI investment should come with a work-rebalancing plan, not just a tech plan."
- "The intensification is structural, not cultural. No amount of change management fixes structural work intensity."
- "This is why productivity metrics are broken. You're measuring output while ignoring the cost to produce it."

## TP2: "The Real Bottleneck Is Not AI Capability. It Is Orchestration."

### The Point
Warp's research shows 60% of agent-generated PRs pass automated checks but less than 10% reach production. The gap is not capability. It's orchestration, verification, and governance architecture. The 80-20 planning-execution framework inverts traditional software economics: 80% of post-AI value comes from specification, architecture, and evaluation. Only 20% comes from generation. This completely changes what we need to hire for and how we structure teams.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** Organizations are investing in agents while optimizing for generation. They're leaving 80% of the value on the table because they haven't built the orchestration layer. This is massive competitive gap.

**Strategic:** The companies that figure out orchestration first will extract 4-5x more value from the same models. The technical capability is commoditizing. Orchestration is the differentiator.

### Who Cares

| **Hiring Manager** | Candidate profile changes | "You need people who can orchestrate agent teams and specify problems, not just debug code." |
| **CTO** | Architecture decisions around agents | "Your bottleneck isn't the model. It's your orchestration architecture." |

That's 80% of what post-AI engineering requires. The people who excel at orchestration aren't the people who excel at coding faster. They're the people who can hold complexity, involve the right stakeholders, and specify the problem so well that an agent can solve it correctly on the first try. This is where my product design and design thinking background becomes an unfair advantage in AI strategy. I can architect the orchestration layer because I've been architecting human-centered systems for decades.

The organizations that can measure contextually, iterate quickly, and capture organizational impact (including hidden costs like work intensification) will extract asymmetric value from commodity models. Everyone else is flying blind. This is the implementation chasm manifesting as measurement failure.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** If 60% variance exists across identical deployments, your procurement decisions are optimizing for the wrong variables. You might be choosing expensive models when a cheaper model with better orchestration would outperform. You might be choosing tools based on benchmark scores that have no correlation to your actual outcomes.

**Strategic:** The competitive advantage is no longer better models. It's better measurement and faster iteration. The companies that build continuous measurement frameworks will compress their learning cycles and stay ahead of organizations that evaluate AI once per year.

## TP4: "Seven-Person Teams Are the New Twenty-Person Teams"

### The Point
Agent augmentation is compressing organizational geometry at scale. Dive Club research shows 7-8 person teams operating at historical 20-30 person capability levels. VP-to-IC migration means senior talent is working directly on problems instead of managing people. McKinsey projects 40,000 humans plus 20,000 agents as the stable state for large enterprises. This isn't theoretical. It's starting to happen. Teams are getting smaller, operating at higher capacity, and the role of "individual contributor manager" is becoming the norm. This changes everything about hiring, staffing, and organizational design.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** Smaller teams at higher capacity = lower headcount, higher output. But it also means different hiring profiles, different retention challenges, and different leadership structures. Organizations that understand this shift early can design hiring and role frameworks that work. Organizations that don't will try to fit 7-person teams into 20-person org structures.

**Strategic:** Organizational geometry is a competitive advantage. Companies that compress team size while maintaining capability will have lower costs, faster decision-making, and better talent retention (because senior people want to work on problems, not manage people). This is a structural advantage that compounds.

### Who Cares

|----------|---------|-------------------|
| **CEO** | Organizational design and headcount planning | "Your next 50 hires might be agents, not people. How do you structure for that?" |
| **HR Leader** | Workforce planning and role redesign | "Seven-person teams need different hiring profiles and different role definitions." |
| **Product Leader** | Team structure and scaling | "Your team structure needs to change to support agent augmentation." |
| **Founder** | Hiring strategy and burn rate | "You can keep your burn rate low longer by augmenting teams with agents." |
| **Engineering VP** | Team geometry and management structure | "VP-to-IC migration means your best people work directly on problems, not manage teams." |

### Evidence

- **Dive Club Research (Hannah Hearth, Design AI)**

  - DP9, DP21: Specific examples of team geometry compression
  - Source credibility: Dive Club analyzes real-world AI augmented teams

- **Harvard Business Review & McKinsey AI Reinvention**

The teams compressing from 20 people to 7 people don't need more engineers. They need more specification and evaluation capability because the technical capability is being augmented by agents. That's exactly where my product design and design thinking background becomes critical. I can help executives understand what a 7-person team looks like, what role profiles change, and how to design organizations for agent-augmented capacity without burning out the humans in them.

## TP5: "The Self-Acceleration Loop Has Closed"

### The Point
Claude Opus 4.6's 1 million token context window is a structural inflection point. Agents can now hold full project state, including requirements, codebase, execution history, and decision context. This closes the self-acceleration loop: better models enable longer contexts, longer contexts reduce coordination overhead, reduced coordination overhead enables more agents in the same team, more agent-human collaboration justifies higher investment in better models. The loop is no longer theoretical. It's structural and permanent. Organizations that evaluate tools based on current capabilities will find themselves perpetually behind because the tools are self-accelerating. Continuous evaluation frameworks aren't optional anymore. They're required.

### Why It Matters
**Business:** If tools are self-accelerating, point-in-time procurement decisions are automatically outdated. You might buy a tool that's "good enough" today and find it's commoditized in 6 months. You need continuous evaluation and rapid upgrade paths built into your procurement and architecture.

**Strategic:** Organizations with continuous evaluation frameworks will systematically outpace organizations with annual evaluation cycles. The capability doubling every 7 months means the difference between evaluating every 6 months and every 12 months compounds to 2x performance difference. This is a structural disadvantage that accelerates over time.

### Who Cares

| Audience | Concern | Conversation Hook |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| **CTO** | Architecture decisions and evaluation strategy | "Your evaluation framework is outdated faster than you can implement the previous one." |
| **Board** | Investment timeline and competitive positioning | "The capability doubling every 7 months. Your evaluation cycle needs to match that pace." |
| **AI Strategy Lead** | Roadmap planning and tool selection | "Your 18-month roadmap will be obsolete in 12 months if you evaluate tools annually." |
| **Risk Officer** | Governance and security implications | "Continuous acceleration means governance frameworks need to be updated more frequently." |
| **VP Engineering** | Team augmentation and capability planning | "Your agent team size can accelerate in parallel with model capability." |

### Evidence

- **AI News & Strategy Daily (Claude Opus 4.6 Update)**
  - DP3, DP4: 1 million token context window enables full project state in context
  - This closes the self-acceleration loop
  - Agents can now hold coordination overhead reduction in context
  - Source credibility: Anthropic official updates

- **Anthropic Agentic Coding Trends Report**
  - DP11: Self-acceleration loop analysis from production agent deployments
  - Context: Anthropic observes this pattern across thousands of deployed agents

- **Matt Shumer Analysis (Something Big Is Happening)**
  - DP7: Capability doubling every 7 months projection
  - This means evaluation cycles compress automatically

- **Onboarding Compression Data**
  - 180x onboarding compression with longer context windows
  - Implication: Coordination overhead reduction is measurable and dramatic

### Your Angle
I've spent my career in organizations evaluating tools for adoption at scale. The pattern is always the same: you evaluate a tool, build your case for adoption, get Board approval, start implementation, and the tool landscape has changed dramatically. This used to be a 12-18 month cycle problem. Now it's a 6-month problem. The organizations that win are the ones that build continuous evaluation into their operating model, not the ones that evaluate tools once per year. This is where my AI adoption strategy perspective is crucial. I know how to design continuous evaluation frameworks that keep pace with accelerating capability. I can help organizations stay ahead of the self-acceleration loop instead of perpetually behind it.

### Pivot Phrases
- "If capability doubles every 7 months, your 12-month evaluation cycle puts you 5 months behind the market."
- "Self-accelerating tools require continuous evaluation frameworks, not annual review cycles."
- "The organizations winning on AI right now are the ones evaluating tools every quarter, not every year."
- "Your roadmap should be liquid, not fixed, to keep pace with capability acceleration."
