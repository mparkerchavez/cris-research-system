Last week I shared my February research reflections on what engineers are teaching us about the future of white-collar work. [LINK TO ARTICLE]

Today I want to go deeper on one piece of that: the skill shift happening inside engineering, and why it matters for everyone else.

The headline version is that engineers went from writing code to managing AI agents that write code. But that undersells what actually changed. The job did not get easier. It got fundamentally different.

Here is what the new workflow looks like. You specify what needs to be built with enough precision that an agent can act on it. You manage multiple agents in parallel, sometimes **10 to 20 concurrent threads** (OpenAI's Sherwin Wu on Lenny's Podcast). You evaluate output across those threads, catching things that technically work but are not good. And you apply taste, the judgment to know when something meets the bar and when it does not.

Specification. Coordination. Evaluation. Taste.

Notice what is missing from that list. Writing code.

The skill that defined the profession for decades is no longer the primary activity. The engineers who are thriving are not the fastest coders. They are the clearest thinkers. The ones who can articulate intent precisely, assess quality critically, and make judgment calls about tradeoffs under ambiguity.

Here is the part that should get non-engineers' attention: those are not engineering skills. They are knowledge work skills. Specifying clearly, evaluating quality, coordinating workstreams, exercising judgment. Product managers, strategists, designers, analysts, and operators use these skills every day.

The irony is that many non-technical professionals already have these muscles. They just have not had to apply them in this context yet.

My hypothesis from the full article: the same pattern that transformed engineering in early 2026 will reach most white-collar roles before the year is out. Not the same tools, but the same structural shift from execution to specification and evaluation.

The Economist's data supports this direction. **White-collar employment expanded by 3 million jobs**, but the composition changed. **Back-office roles contracted 20%** while **coordination roles grew 30% or more**. The work is not disappearing. The nature of the work is changing.

What I keep coming back to is that the people best positioned for this shift may not be who you expect. It is not necessarily the most technical people. It is the people who think clearly, communicate precisely, and know what good looks like in their domain.

That is what taste means in this context. Not aesthetic preference. Domain expertise applied as a quality filter. And that is something you build over a career, not something an AI replaces.
