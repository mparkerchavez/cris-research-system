Today: the other side of that shift. The people who are not thriving, and why most organizations are reading the situation wrong.

A number that should worry every leader tracking AI adoption: **65%**.

That is the percentage of work tasks where anxious employees are using AI, according to HBR's February 2026 research. Their non-anxious peers? **42%**. The people who are most afraid of AI are using it more, not less.

That seems like good news until you understand what is driving it. It is not curiosity or value. It is fear. People are using AI because they are afraid of what happens if they do not. HBR's researchers call it compliance, not adoption.

Meanwhile, **88% of organizations report regular AI use**. Leaders see usage dashboards climbing and assume the strategy is working. But **84% of those same organizations have not redesigned jobs around AI** (Deloitte State of AI 2026). They deployed tools without changing the work. More output, same processes, growing exhaustion. That is not productivity. It is intensification.

The result is a gap that does not show up in any dashboard. **Forty percent of employees simultaneously believe AI has business value and fear what it means for their personal security.** They are using the tools. They are hitting the metrics. And they are quietly disengaging from genuine capability building.

This is what I called the "silent middle" in my full February analysis. [LINK TO ARTICLE] Not the vocal resisters, who at least surface concerns you can address. Not the enthusiastic adopters, who are pulling ahead. The people in between, performing compliance while the gap between them and practitioners widens every week.

And the support structure is failing them. **Manager support for IC AI usage has declined 11 percentage points since May 2025.** Only **7% of ICs** report their managers expect daily AI use. The people with the most to gain are getting the least support.

The honest framing is not "people need to get on board." People are responding rationally to real uncertainty, often without adequate support or honest conversation from their leaders. **Fifty-eight percent of Americans distrust AI**, and that number has not moved in six months (YouGov). CEO rhetoric framing humans as inefficient compute does not help. It confirms people's worst fears.

The research suggests **roughly 80% of current opposition is implementation-specific and addressable**. Most people are not opposed to AI. They are opposed to how it is being done.

That is a solvable problem. But only if leaders stop reading dashboards and start reading the room.
