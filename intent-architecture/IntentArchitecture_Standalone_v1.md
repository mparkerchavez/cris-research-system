# The Intent Architecture
## A Framework for Organizations That Have Deployed Copilot and Want to Know What Comes Next

**Super by Design | Maicol Parker-Chavez**
**Version 1.0 — March 2026**

---

## The Story You're Already In

Here is what the data says about Microsoft Copilot:

85% of Fortune 500 companies adopted it. Only 5% of organizations moved from a pilot to a larger-scale deployment. Only 3% of total Microsoft 365 users are active paid users. [1]

This is not a story about a bad product. Copilot is a genuinely useful tool. Individual users who adopted it report real productivity gains. Some teams swear by it. The productivity is real.

And yet, at the organizational level, it mostly stalled.

Why? Because most organizations deployed a tool without first defining what the tool was supposed to accomplish for the business. As AI researcher Nate B Jones put it: deploying an AI tool across an organization without organizational intent alignment is like hiring 40,000 new employees and never telling them what the company does, what it values, or how to make decisions. [2]

The tool showed up. The strategy didn't.

This is not a critique of your Copilot deployment. It is a diagnosis of why AI deployments that feel successful at the individual level fail to produce measurable organizational results. And it is the starting point for doing something different.

---

## The Pattern Is Wider Than Copilot

The Copilot story is a specific version of a broader pattern playing out across every organization currently using AI.

Organizations that deployed ChatGPT Enterprise or Claude face the same deployment gap. In some ways, a sharper one. Copilot's built-in workflow touchpoints — drafting in Word, summarizing in Outlook, generating in Teams — at least give employees a starting context for where to apply the tool. ChatGPT Enterprise and Claude are more capable tools with a broader range of use cases, which means the blank canvas is wider. More possible applications do not resolve the question of which applications matter for this organization, on this team, in this role. That question is an intent question, regardless of which tool you deployed.

45% of businesses are paying for AI tools. Only 12% are using AI in their operations in ways that produce measurable business impact. [3]

85% of AI users have no ROI-driving use cases. Their top actual uses are rewriting emails, doing basic search, fixing formulas, and summarizing documents. [3]

74% of organizations report they have yet to see tangible value from AI, despite the fact that 57% of companies are allocating between 21% and 50% of their entire digital transformation budget to it. [4]

These organizations are not failing because AI doesn't work. They are failing because they are generating AI activity without having defined what they are trying to achieve. Activity is not impact.

And activity without intent has another cost: it is exhausting. Harvard Business Review's eight-month study of a 200-person technology company found that AI does not reduce work — it intensifies it. AI makes tasks easier to start, which means people start more of them, which means the work expands to fill all available time and then overflows into evenings and weekends. Without organizational intent behind the deployment, AI does not buy time. It consumes it. "Without intention," the study concludes, "AI makes it easier to do more — but harder to stop." [5]

The organizations that are winning are doing something different. Businesses with a coherent AI strategy and roadmap are 2x more likely to report significant productivity gains. Businesses using AI for business strategy itself are 6x more likely to report significant innovation. [6] The difference is not the tools they have. The difference is the clarity of intent behind how they use them.

## What This Framework Is

The Intent Architecture is a five-module framework for organizations that have started their AI journey and want to understand what it takes to get real organizational results.

The word "intent" is deliberate. In design, being intentional means making decisions on purpose — knowing what you are trying to accomplish, for whom, and why. Every other AI framework on the market asks: "Where are you on the maturity ladder?" The Intent Architecture asks something different: "What are you trying to build, and is your current AI deployment actually moving you toward it?"

This framework is built for organizations that:

- Have already deployed Copilot, ChatGPT, or another enterprise AI tool
- Are generating individual-level productivity but haven't seen team or organizational-level impact
- Are not sure what to measure, or are measuring things that don't connect back to business outcomes
- Want a practical path forward without scrapping what they've already built

The framework has five modules. They build on each other. Each one is designed to produce a decision, not just an insight.

## Module 1: Define Your AI Intent

*What is AI actually supposed to do for this organization?*

The first question is not "what AI tools should we use?" The first question is "what are we trying to accomplish?"

This sounds obvious. It is almost universally skipped.

Nick Bartlett of the Future Trends Strategy Group observed that most organizations are "overwhelmed by noise, misaligned internally, and uncertain about how trends connect to organizational goals." They treat AI as the strategy rather than as an input to strategy. The result is that they acquire sophisticated systems without a clear blueprint for how those systems create value. "Starting with technologies leads to incrementalism, not transformation." [7]

The Insight Partners research from early 2026 named the root cause precisely: "The constraint has moved upstream. The real bottleneck is human indecision." [8] AI can now build almost anything faster than ever before. The organizations that can't use that leverage don't have a technology problem. They have a strategy problem. "In 2026, AI won't be making decisions for product managers — but it will make it painfully visible where we have indecision and lack of strategy." [8]

Salesforce's enterprise AI team puts it this way: define your why before building any agent. Document who it serves, why it's needed, and what success looks like. [9] This is not a philosophical exercise. It is the prerequisite for every other decision in the framework.

**The workshop question:** If AI is working exactly as intended in your organization two years from now, what specifically is different? What decisions are faster? What work no longer needs to happen? What outcomes are measurably better?

This question surfaces your intent. Everything else is designed to close the gap between that intent and your current reality.

## Module 2: Audit the Current Reality

*Where are you actually? Tools, proficiency, and what you're measuring today.*

Before designing a path forward, you need an honest read on where you are. Most organizations have a much better story about their AI tools than their data supports.

The audit has three layers.

### Layer 1: What tools do you actually have?

List every AI tool your organization has deployed or licensed. For each one, ask: Is it embedded in the workflows where it is supposed to add value, or does it live in a separate tab that people open only when they remember? "AI tools that live in a separate tab get abandoned." — UKG [9] The tab is not a minor friction point. It is a signal that the tool was never integrated into how work actually flows.

For organizations using ChatGPT Enterprise or Claude, the separate-tab dynamic starts as the default, not a deviation. Those tools do not have native workflow integrations built in — they arrive as general-purpose interfaces. The audit question shifts: has your organization built any custom workflow integrations or use-case-specific configurations, or is the tool being used as a general-purpose chat interface with no connection to where work actually happens?

### Layer 2: How proficient is your workforce?

The most reliable data on workforce AI proficiency shows the following distribution: 28% Novice, 70% Experimenter, 3% Practitioner, and 0.1% Expert. [3]

The 70% majority — Experimenters — are the critical group. They are using AI enough to feel like they are progressing. They are not using it deeply enough to produce organizational results. They know how to open ChatGPT or Copilot. They do not know what to use it for in their specific job, on their specific team, connected to their specific business goals. "Employees who don't know how to use AI, or what to use it for, are more likely to never use it." [3]

The proficiency audit is not about identifying laggards. It is about identifying where the biggest leverage for organizational impact lives. The gap between Experimenter and Practitioner is the gap between individual productivity and organizational results.

### Layer 3: What are you measuring?

Name every AI metric your organization currently tracks. Be specific.

Most organizations discover that they are tracking tool deployment (what tools exist and who has a license), usage signals (who opened the tool, how often), and anecdotal wins (stories people share in Slack or all-hands). Almost no organizations are tracking workflow integration, changes in time-to-outcome, or measurable improvements in department-level KPIs.

The measurement gap is a strategy gap. Businesses that do not have a coherent AI strategy are likely to see pilot fatigue. [6] What you measure shapes what decisions you make next. Organizations measuring prompt volume are making different decisions than organizations measuring revenue per employee or case deflection rate.

**The audit output:** A clear-eyed picture of three things — what tools you have and whether they are embedded in real workflows, where your workforce falls on the proficiency spectrum, and what you are currently measuring versus what you would need to measure to know whether AI is working.

## Module 3: Name the Gap

*Tool ceiling, measurement gap, or intent gap — the diagnosis changes the prescription.*

The audit in Module 2 produces data. Module 3 turns that data into a diagnosis. There are three distinct types of gaps, and they require different interventions.

### Gap Type 1: The Tool Ceiling

The tool you deployed was the right starting point. Individual productivity is real. But the tool has reached its ceiling for your use case.

This is the most common Copilot pattern. Copilot is an excellent Phase 1 tool. It augments individuals. It helps with drafting, summarizing, researching, and generating first versions of things. These are real, valuable capabilities. They are also the capabilities of an individual assistant, not an organizational system.

GitHub Copilot's experience is instructive: it produced 55% gains in code completion speed. But it also produced larger pull requests, higher code review costs, and more security vulnerabilities. [10] "Copilot makes writing code cheaper but owning it more expensive." The tool reached its ceiling when the work it enabled created new costs downstream that were not accounted for.

The tool ceiling means: you need to move to the next phase of the transformation, not replace what you have. What comes after individual augmentation is workflow automation.

For ChatGPT Enterprise and Claude deployments, Gap Type 1 can present differently. The issue is not a ceiling — both tools have substantial capability well beyond individual augmentation. The issue is a blank canvas gap: the tool's openness makes it harder, not easier, to identify which workflows to prioritize. The prescription is the same: intent work first. Broad capability without a defined application is still a tool waiting to be pointed.

### Gap Type 2: The Measurement Gap

The organization is generating AI activity, but has no system to connect that activity to business outcomes. Nobody is wrong. Nothing is broken. The results are just invisible.

Dan Shapiro, whose five-level framework maps AI development maturity, identified a critically important psychological trap: Level 2 of AI adoption "feels like you are done. But you are not done." [11] 90% of self-described "AI-native" practitioners are living at Level 2. They are pairing with AI as an equal, experiencing significant productivity gains, and believing the transformation is complete.

It is not complete. Feeling productive is different from producing measurable organizational impact. The measurement gap makes the difference invisible.

The prescription for a measurement gap is the Section three-pillar framework: measure Usage and Proficiency first, then Automations (volume of workflows being automated), then Department-Level Impact. These move in sequence from individual to organizational, and from leading indicators to lagging ones. The measurement system turns invisible progress into visible results — and invisible stalls into visible decisions.

### Gap Type 3: The Intent Gap

The deepest gap. The organization deployed tools without defining what the tools were supposed to accomplish at an organizational level. The tools are being used. They are generating activity. But the activity is not connected to any organizational goal.

The Klarna case study is the clearest illustration of this gap in action. Klarna's AI customer service agent was extraordinary at its technical task. It resolved tickets at scale, faster than ever before. It was, by the measurable proxy, successful. And it destroyed customer relationships that drove lifetime value, because "resolve tickets fast" was the measurable objective and "build lasting customer relationships" was the actual organizational intent. The AI worked. The intent was never encoded. [4]

Nate B Jones summarizes the intent gap's root cause: "Most organizations have never had to do this. Their goals live in slide decks, in OKR documents that get half-read and referenced at personal reviews once a year." [4] Humans could always fill in the gaps between stated goals and day-to-day decisions. AI agents cannot. They execute the explicit objective, not the implicit one.

The prescription for an intent gap is Module 1 done properly — before any tool deployment. For organizations that have already deployed, it means going back and doing the intent work now, before expanding to the next phase.

**The diagnostic output:** A named gap with a clear prescription. Not "we need more AI" but "we have a tool ceiling and need to move to workflow automation" or "we have a measurement gap and need a three-pillar KPI system" or "we have an intent gap and need to define what we're actually trying to build before we go further."

## Module 4: Design the Path

*Short, medium, and long-term moves — with the measurement system that makes progress visible.*

The three-phase transformation gives organizations a sequenced path. The sequencing matters. Organizations that jump from Phase 1 (individual augmentation) to Phase 3 (core process reinvention) without Phase 2 (workflow automation) build on an unstable foundation. Organizations that stay in Phase 1 forever are the ones losing ground to competitors.

### Phase 1: Optimize (Augment Your Workforce)

Focus: Usage and Proficiency.

The goal here is to move the 70% Experimenter majority toward meaningful, repeatable AI use. This is where Copilot lives, and where the work of making Copilot work begins.

The ROI at this phase is expressed as realized productivity value per employee. The formula: 80% daily active users multiplied by a 10% productivity gain equals 7–8% realized headcount savings equivalent — which, for a 6,500-person organization, equals the productivity output of 460 employees. [3]

What drives the usage metric in practice: basic access to great tools, basic proficiency certification (employees who don't know what to use AI for are more likely to never use it), activation of VPs, managers, and champions to model adoption from every level of the organization, and regular AI touchpoints (office hours, hackathons, lunch and learns, shared channels). [3]

The Copilot correction happens here. Most Copilot deployments skipped the proficiency and champions work. The fix is not replacing Copilot. It is building the adoption infrastructure that should have accompanied the deployment.

### Phase 2: Accelerate (Automate Workflows)

Focus: Automations.

This is where ROI accelerates. The goal is to move from AI-augmented individuals to AI-automated workflows.

The ROI formula at this phase: 1,000 AI automations in a 1,000-person company, each saving 2 hours per week, equals 2,000 hours per week — equivalent to 50 full-time employees. [3]

The primary target is a 1:1 worker-to-agent ratio: the number of custom AI automations (GPTs, Gems, Copilot Studio workflows) that employees have built, measured against total headcount. This is a direct proxy for AI expertise and experimentation across the organization. [3]

The secondary target is 1–3 major agentic applications per business division — each running with consistent weekly usage. Every function (Sales, Marketing, Finance, HR, Operations) should have at least one meaningful workflow that AI is genuinely running. [3]

Zapier's approach is instructive: a single focused hackathon moved their proportion of weekly AI users from 10% to 50%. [9] The hackathon is not just a culture event. It is the fastest known intervention for converting Experimenters into Practitioners.

IBM's principle for this phase: structure ROI around workflows, not tasks. A single top-level workflow decomposes into hundreds of component tasks. The compounding impact is in the workflow, not the individual task. [9]

### Phase 3: Reinvent (Transform Core Processes)

Focus: Department-Level Impact.

This is where AI moves from productivity conversation to business strategy conversation. ROI measurement happens at the functional level, and it connects to the organizational outcomes that actually matter.

The framework: ask each department leader to define the KPI before automating with AI, establish a 4-week baseline of that KPI and its current cost, implement the AI-automated process for 4 weeks, and measure at 30, 60, and 90 days against baseline. [3]

Example: Sales team measures meetings booked via cold outbound. Baseline: 10 meetings per month at 25 hours at $80 per hour = $2,000 in labor. AI-automated SDR outreach for 4 weeks: 25 meetings per month at $350 in AI costs. The ROI is now visible, specific, and attributable. [3]

No baseline metrics yet? Start measuring now. A 4-week current-state baseline is better than nothing. Mid-deployment measurement is still valid. [3]

Siemens reached Phase 3 by deploying a vertically focused AI agent on production equipment diagnostics. The result: 85% reduction in downtime fix time, 6,000 hours saved. [9] The critical lesson from Siemens: horizontal agents (deployed broadly across many tasks) produced incremental gains. The vertical agent (deployed deeply on one specific high-value workflow) drove substantial business impact.

ServiceNow's internal deployment produced measurable results across multiple dimensions: 54% of "report an issue" tickets deflected by AI, approximately 20% of cases avoided entirely, $5.5 million per year in annual savings from avoided cases, and incidents closed in half the time. [3] These are not productivity statistics. They are business outcomes.

### The Four Principles for Maximizing Impact at Every Phase

1. Start with workflows tied to measurable business outcomes — revenue, cost, or time-to-market — not just efficiency gains.
2. Prioritize repeatable, high-volume workflows. AI impact compounds at scale. A task done 500 times per month creates more value than a complex task done twice.
3. Automate workflows that play to AI's strengths: content creation, synthesis, and data analysis.
4. Stack use cases within a single workflow. Map the whole workflow and find 3–4 AI touchpoints — research, then draft, then personalize, then QA — instead of automating a single step. [3]

## Module 5: Build for What's Coming

*AI is not a destination. It is a moving system. Your organization needs to be able to move with it.*

Here is what the next phase of AI looks like:

"We have left the era of AI that speaks with us — to enter the era of AI that works for us." — Brice Challamel, OpenAI [9]

The shift from AI-as-assistant to AI-as-worker is not a future scenario. It is happening now in the organizations at the frontier. Small teams of fewer than five people are producing outputs that previously required dozens of people, by deploying AI systems that run largely autonomously. [11]

Most organizations will not get there quickly. But the organizations that do not build the foundation for it will find themselves at an accelerating competitive disadvantage. Incumbents risk losing 15–20% of revenue to AI-native startups that are war-gaming how to attack their market right now. [9]

The foundation for what comes next has three components.

### 1. Organizational Intent Infrastructure

The organizations that win the next phase of AI competition will not necessarily have the best models. They will have the most clearly articulated organizational intent, encoded in a form that AI systems can act on.

"The race is an intent race. Not who has the smartest AI in their systems, but who has built the organizational infrastructure that lets AI operate with the fullest, most accurate, most strategically correct understanding." [4]

This means making organizational goals, values, decision frameworks, and tradeoff hierarchies discoverable, structured, and actionable by AI systems — not just by humans. OKRs were designed for humans who could fill in the gaps with judgment and osmosis. Agents cannot fill in gaps. They execute what they are given. The organizations that have done the intent work in Modules 1 through 4 are building this infrastructure even if they don't call it that.

### 2. Governance as Architecture

Governance is not a compliance function in this context. It is the architecture that allows AI to operate at scale without creating the risks — security, legal, reputational — that stop organizations from going further.

The Cisco framework is instructive: "discover, detect, protect." Understand what AI is being used for in your organization, detect where risk is concentrating, protect where it matters. Governance built this way is an accelerant, not a speed bump. [9]

The organizations that treat governance as a cost of doing business will find it slows them down. The organizations that build governance into the workflow architecture will find it enables them to go faster, with more confidence.

### 3. The Sustainability Imperative

The HBR finding is important here and is often overlooked in AI strategy conversations: AI intensifies work. It does not reduce it.

Organizations that deploy AI without thinking about what it means for the humans doing the work will generate a self-reinforcing acceleration cycle: AI speeds up certain tasks, which raises expectations for speed, which increases AI reliance, which expands the scope of work, which raises expectations further. [5]

Employees in this cycle feel more productive. They do not feel less busy. In many cases, they feel busier than before AI. The cycle is not sustainable. "Without intention, AI makes it easier to do more — but harder to stop." [5]

The organizations that build for what's coming are the ones that treat the sustainability of their human workforce as a design constraint — not an afterthought. AI governance includes governing how AI reshapes the pace and scope of human work, not just how AI handles data and security.

### The Long View

The allocation economy is emerging. Individuals are increasingly judged not by the limits of their subject matter expertise, but by how well they can allocate and manage AI resources to get work done. [12]

This is a different kind of workforce capability than what most organizations are currently building. It requires a different kind of development, a different kind of management, and a different kind of organizational culture.

The organizations that are building this now — in parallel with the tool deployment and the workflow automation — will have a durable advantage. The organizations that are treating AI purely as a productivity layer will find that the advantage evaporates as the tools commoditize.

## What The Intent Architecture Is Not

It is not a maturity ladder. Maturity ladders tell you where you are. The Intent Architecture starts with where you want to go.

It is not a tool selection guide. The framework applies regardless of whether you are primarily a Copilot organization, a ChatGPT Enterprise organization, or working with a custom deployment. The tool is Module 2. The intent is Module 1.

It is not a critique of your current deployment. Whatever you have deployed is the starting point. The framework builds from here.

---

## Getting Started: The First Conversation

The entry point for this work is a 90-minute facilitated session with your leadership team. The output is a completed Module 1 — a clear statement of your AI intent, grounded in your actual organizational goals, that the rest of the framework can be built on.

From there, the audit takes two to three weeks. The diagnosis is one session. The path design is a two-session workshop. The full framework can move from conversation to roadmap in four to six weeks.

Every organization's path is different. The framework is the same. What varies is where you are, where you want to go, and what gaps are standing between you and the impact you are trying to create.

That is what we are here to figure out together.
