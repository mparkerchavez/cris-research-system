## How to Use This Guide

This is a prep tool, not a script. Use it before conversations to identify which archetype you're likely entering, orient to the relevant data, and find the right pivot into the Intent Architecture framework.

**Before a meeting:** Scan the archetype names. Identify the one or two that fit. Read the full card.

**In the meeting:** Listen for the stated question and emotional state to confirm your read. One person can carry two archetypes simultaneously — that's normal.

**Customizing a deck:** Pull from the "Deck-ready" section of the relevant archetype. These are the 2-3 data points most likely to create "that's exactly it" recognition for that specific person.

**The leader/team gap:** Every card includes what leaders believe to be true AND what their teams are actually experiencing. Use this to create a specific moment: *"You might think X — but here's what we're seeing from the people doing the work."* This often earns more trust than any framework pitch.

## Archetype 1: The Tool Deployment Gap

**"We invested in [Copilot / ChatGPT / Claude] and I honestly can't tell you what we got from it."**

**Typical role:** CEO, COO, CTO, or Head of Digital/Innovation. The person who championed or approved the deployment.

**Emotional state:** Quietly frustrated, a little embarrassed, and wondering if they made the wrong call — or the right call at the wrong time. Not ready to admit the deployment didn't work, but privately asking themselves why they can't point to a clear win.

---

### What they're saying

> "Some people really love it. I get great testimonials in Slack. But when I try to quantify what we actually got from the investment, I can't give you a clean number. I think it's helping, but I can't prove it."

> "We rolled it out to everyone. Adoption is decent. But I'm not seeing it in our output. We're not shipping faster, we're not closing deals faster. Something's off."

---

### What they're actually worried about

They approved the investment — possibly a significant one. They've been publicly supportive. If the deployment isn't working, that's a leadership credibility question, not just a technology question. The real worry isn't "did we buy the wrong tool" — it's "did we do this wrong?"

---

### What they believe is true

Deployment equals adoption, and adoption equals value. The tool is deployed, people are using it (or at least not complaining), and something must be happening — it just hasn't shown up in the numbers yet. They're still in a "give it time" posture.

---

### What their teams are actually experiencing

70% of their workforce is stuck in the use case desert. They know how to open the tool. They don't know what to use it for in their specific job, on their specific team, connected to their specific business goals. The most common use cases: rewriting emails, replacing Google searches, summarizing meeting notes. Real, but not organizational value.

There is also a significant gap between what leadership sees and what the ground looks like. **50% of C-level executives believe their organization has widespread AI adoption. Only 26% of managers and frontline employees agree.** Leadership is looking at license deployment data. Employees are experiencing a use case desert.

And the 28% of the workforce who are still AI Novices represent a *net risk* right now — 79% of novices don't know how to protect sensitive information when using AI tools. This is rarely part of the deployment narrative.

---

### Data that names it

**"85% of Fortune 500 companies adopted Copilot. Only 5% moved from a pilot to a larger-scale deployment. Only 3% of total Microsoft 365 users adopted it as paid users."**
— Nate B Jones, AI News & Strategy Daily, February 24, 2026 (para. 33)

*This lands because it reframes a deployment failure as an industry-wide pattern, not a local one. They didn't do something uniquely wrong — almost nobody has cracked this yet.*

**"85% of the workforce does not have a value-driving AI use case."**
— Section, AI Proficiency Report, January 2026 (p. 1)

*This is the diagnosis of why deployment doesn't equal value. It's not about the tool. It's about the absence of use cases.*

**"Level 2 feels like you are done. But you are not done. 90% of AI-native developers are living right now at Level 2."**
— Dan Shapiro, "The Five Levels," January 23, 2026 (Level 2 section)

*This creates recognition of the false completion trap — the feeling of progress that isn't organizational transformation.*

---

### Tool-specific nuance

**If they deployed Microsoft Copilot:**
The Copilot story is the clearest example of this pattern because the numbers are so stark — 85% Fortune 500 adoption, 5% pilot-to-scale. What makes Copilot particularly instructive: the tool even has workflow integration built in. It shows up inside Teams, Outlook, and Word and suggests obvious entry points. It still stalled. The issue isn't integration friction; it's the absence of organizational intent and role-specific use cases. The tell: Udemy reported **3,400% year-over-year growth in Microsoft Copilot for Business learning courses in 2026**. Organizations are scrambling to learn how to use a tool they already deployed. The fix isn't more training on the tool — it's defining what the tool was supposed to accomplish at the organizational level.

**If they deployed ChatGPT Enterprise or Claude:**
These tools present a different and in some ways more acute version of the same problem — the **blank canvas paradox**. ChatGPT Enterprise and Claude are more capable and more flexible than Copilot, but that capability becomes a liability without intent behind it. Copilot at least shows up in your existing workflows and suggests entry points. ChatGPT and Claude are open canvases: they can do almost anything, which means the average employee has no clear starting point for *their* specific job. The use case desert is wider here, not narrower.

The people getting outsized value from these tools tend to be highly self-directed power users — the 3% who are already AI practitioners. The 70% Experimenter majority is no more equipped to use a more powerful tool without guidance. If anything, the capability gap between what the tool can do and what they know how to ask for creates more paralysis, not less.

**What's common to both:**
The tool is not the problem. The problem is that it was deployed without defining what organizational value was supposed to look like — what decisions should be faster, what work should stop happening, what outcomes should be measurably better. The Intent Architecture starts there.

---

### Framework entry point

**Primary:** Module 2 (Audit the Current Reality) — specifically Layer 2 (proficiency distribution) and Layer 3 (what they're currently measuring).

**Diagnosis leads to:** Module 3 (Name the Gap) — this typically presents as either a Tool Ceiling (maxed out Phase 1 individual augmentation, need to move to workflow automation) or a Measurement Gap (results are invisible because nothing is being measured against business outcomes).

**Underlying:** Module 1 (Define Your AI Intent) — often the intent was never defined, just assumed.

---

### Pivot phrase

> "What you're describing is actually the most common pattern in enterprise AI right now — and it's not a Copilot problem, a ChatGPT problem, or a people problem. It's a framing problem. Most organizations deployed the tool before defining what the tool was supposed to accomplish for the business. The good news is that's fixable without starting over. Can I show you what that looks like?"

---

### Deck-ready pulls

- **The headline stat:** "85% Fortune 500 adoption. 5% pilot-to-scale. The tool isn't the issue."
- **The recognition moment:** "70% of your workforce knows how to open the tool. They don't know what to use it for."
- **The executive/team gap:** "50% of executives think they have widespread adoption. 26% of their managers agree."
- **The blank canvas nuance (ChatGPT/Claude):** "The more capable the tool, the wider the use case desert without intent behind the deployment."

## Archetype 2: The Overwhelmed Executive

**"There's so much happening with AI, I don't know what to prioritize."**

**Typical role:** CEO, division head, or senior leader receiving AI pressure from multiple directions simultaneously — board, vendors, competitors, internal champions, and their own leadership team.

**Emotional state:** Overwhelmed by signal-to-noise, quietly paralyzed by the fear of making the wrong bet, and probably a little exhausted by the volume of AI pitches they've sat through. They know they need to move. They don't know what "moving" looks like.

---

### What they're saying

> "Every week there's something new. Every vendor has an AI story. My board is asking about it. My team keeps coming to me with ideas. I feel like I'm always reacting to the last thing I saw, not building toward something."

> "I've approved a few pilots. Some things are happening. But I couldn't tell you what my AI strategy is. I'm not sure I have one."

---

### What they're actually worried about

Moving too slowly (falling behind competitors) and moving in the wrong direction (burning capital and credibility on the wrong bet) — simultaneously. These two fears create paralysis. The real question underneath is: "How do I get to a clear decision and stop spinning?"

---

### What they believe is true

Their problem is primarily informational. If they just had better data about what's working, they'd know what to do. So they read more, attend more conferences, take more vendor meetings. The information keeps growing. The clarity doesn't arrive.

---

### What their teams are actually experiencing

Teams in this situation are often moving anyway — just in different directions. Individual contributors are running their own AI experiments. Teams are building shadow workflows. IT is trying to govern tools they don't have full visibility into. The absence of an explicit strategy doesn't mean nothing is happening; it means many things are happening without coordination, no shared infrastructure, and no way to know what's working.

Insight Partners named this precisely: "The real bottleneck is human indecision." The constraint isn't technology capability anymore — it's the absence of organizational direction.

---

### Data that names it

**"Starting with technologies leads to incrementalism, not transformation."**
— Nick Bartlett, Future Trends Strategy Group, January 16, 2026 (para. 2)

*This names the trap they're in: responding to AI technology one move at a time instead of leading with what they're trying to build.*

**"The constraint has moved upstream. The real bottleneck is human indecision."**
— Insight Partners, "The Patterns Shaping AI Adoption in 2026," February 2, 2026 (para. 2)

*This reframes the problem. It's not that AI is hard. Making decisions about AI is hard — and that's a strategy problem, not a technology problem.*

**"In 2026, AI won't be making decisions for product managers — but it will make it painfully visible where we have indecision and lack of strategy."**
— Rachel Weston Rowell, EVP, Insight Partners (para. 6)

*The most useful one for creating urgency without alarm: AI doesn't cause the problem, it exposes it.*

---

### Framework entry point

**Primary:** Module 1 (Define Your AI Intent) — the entire framing of the Intent Architecture is built for this person. The question isn't "what AI tools should we use?" but "what are we trying to build, and what does AI need to do to get us there?"

**The workshop question to offer:** "If AI is working exactly as intended in your organization two years from now, what specifically is different? What decisions are faster? What work no longer needs to happen? What outcomes are measurably better?"

---

### Pivot phrase

> "The clarity you're looking for won't come from more information — it already hasn't, and you've had access to plenty. It comes from answering a different question. Not 'what should we do with AI?' but 'what are we actually trying to build, and what would AI need to do to get us there?' That's a 90-minute conversation. Would you want to have it?"

---

### Deck-ready pulls

- **The reframe:** "The problem isn't AI. It's that AI will make your lack of strategy impossible to ignore."
- **The diagnosis:** "Most organizations treat AI as the strategy. The ones winning treat it as an input to strategy."
- **The question:** "If AI is working exactly as intended two years from now — what specifically is different?"

## Archetype 3: The ROI Pressure Cooker

**"My CFO / board wants proof and I don't have a good answer yet."**

**Typical role:** CTO, CIO, or Head of Digital Transformation — whoever owns the AI initiative internally. They're not the skeptic; they believe in AI. But they're being asked to prove it and can't.

**Emotional state:** Exposed and under pressure. They've been the internal champion. They got the budget. Now the accountability conversation is arriving and the results don't match the investment narrative. They're not looking for permission to slow down — they're looking for a measurement framework that helps them show results.

---

### What they're saying

> "I've been saying AI is going to change how we work. Now my CFO is asking me to show the numbers and I keep saying 'it's hard to measure' or 'we need more time.' I can feel the credibility window closing."

> "We're measuring things like how many people are using the tools, how many prompts are being sent. But I know that's not what my CFO actually cares about. I just don't know what the right metrics are."

---

### What they're actually worried about

The credibility of their entire AI program. If they can't answer the ROI question, the program gets defunded — or worse, deprioritized, and they lose the organizational momentum they've built. The hidden fear: they know the current metrics are vanity metrics. And they know their CFO knows it too.

---

### What they believe is true

The results are real — they just aren't being measured correctly. Which is partially true. But the deeper issue is usually that the measurement system was never built because the intended outcomes were never defined. You can't measure against a goal that was never specified.

---

### What their teams are actually experiencing

Teams are generating AI activity — prompts, summaries, drafts — but that activity has no connection to any business outcome. Nobody is comparing current performance to a baseline. No department has defined the KPI they're trying to move. The measurement gap makes progress invisible and stalls invisible too. Teams feel like they're doing something. The organization has no way to know whether it matters.

**74% of organizations globally report they have yet to see tangible value from AI — despite 57% of companies allocating between 21% and 50% of their entire digital transformation budget to it.** Their teams are in the 74%, not because they're doing something wrong, but because the measurement architecture doesn't exist yet.

---

### Data that names it

**"74% of organizations hope to grow revenue through their AI initiatives in the future. Only 20% are already doing so."**
— Deloitte AI Institute, "State of AI in the Enterprise," January 2026 (p. 10)
(Survey: 3,235 director-to-C-suite respondents, 6 industries, 24 countries)

*This lands because it shows the gap isn't unique to them — and it names exactly the transition they're trying to make.*

**Phase 1 ROI formula:** "80% daily active users × 10% productivity gain = 7–8% realized headcount savings equivalent."
**Phase 2 ROI formula:** "1,000 AI automations × 2 hours saved per week = 2,000 hours per week = 50 FTEs of capacity."
— Section, "How to Measure AI Success," Spring 2026

*These give CFO-ready language they've been missing. Concrete, attributable, specific.*

**"IBM has learned to structure their ROI around workflows — not individual tasks. IBM mapped 12 top-level workflows into hundreds of components. That's the level of specificity CFOs actually respond to."**
— Matt Lyteson, IBM CIO, Section AI:ROI Playbook, Spring 2026

*This reframes the measurement problem: the current metrics are at the wrong level of granularity.*

---

### Framework entry point

**Primary:** Module 4 (Design the Path) — specifically the three-phase transformation and the four-week baseline measurement methodology.

**The practical prescription:** Define the KPI before automating. Establish a 4-week baseline of that KPI and its cost. Implement the AI-automated process. Measure at 30, 60, and 90 days against baseline. This turns invisible AI activity into a specific, attributable business outcome.

**Secondary:** Module 3 (Measurement Gap diagnosis) — naming what type of gap they have gives them language for the CFO conversation.

---

### Pivot phrase

> "The metrics you're tracking aren't wrong — they're just at the wrong level. Your CFO doesn't care how many prompts were sent. They care whether something measurable changed: meetings booked, tickets resolved, time-to-close. The shift is from tracking AI activity to baselining and measuring business outcomes. That's a methodology question, not an AI question. Want me to show you what that looks like?"

---

### Deck-ready pulls

- **The gap:** "74% of organizations hope AI will grow revenue. 20% are already there. The difference is measurement architecture, not capability."
- **The formula:** "1,000 automations × 2 hours per week = 50 FTEs of capacity. That's a CFO conversation."
- **The reframe:** "You're measuring AI activity. Your CFO needs to see business outcomes."

## Archetype 4: The People-First Leader

**"I rolled out AI to help my team and somehow they seem more overwhelmed than before."**

**Typical role:** CHRO, People Operations leader, or a business leader who is genuinely team-oriented and measuring success partly by how their people feel. They believed AI would give time back. Something else happened.

**Emotional state:** Confused, a little guilty, and honestly a bit alarmed. They did the "right thing" — invested in tools to help their team — and the outcome is the opposite of what they intended. They're wondering if they missed something fundamental.

---

### What they're saying

> "I genuinely thought this would give people time back. They'd spend less time on the administrative stuff. But when I talk to my team, they're busier than ever. The work just keeps expanding."

> "Some people are happy about the productivity. But I'm hearing from others that they feel like they can never switch off. There's always more they could be doing."

---

### What they're actually worried about

Underneath the confusion is a harder question: have they made things worse? And the follow-on: is this a technology problem, a management problem, or a culture problem? They're looking for someone to explain the mechanism, not just prescribe a fix.

---

### What they believe is true

AI would compress time — that tasks which took two hours would take 30 minutes, and the remaining 90 minutes would belong to the employee. That's a reasonable assumption. It's also wrong.

---

### What their teams are actually experiencing

This card is unique: the team's experience *is* the core data. Harvard Business Review's eight-month study of a 200-person technology company found exactly what this leader is observing. **AI doesn't reduce work — it intensifies it.** When AI makes tasks easier to start, people start more of them. When AI makes tasks faster, expectations for speed increase. The result is a self-reinforcing acceleration cycle: AI accelerates certain tasks, which raises expectations for speed, which increases AI reliance, which expands the scope of what people are expected to do.

Employees in this cycle feel more productive. They do not feel less busy. In some cases they feel busier than before AI.

There is also an equity dimension here that often goes unspoken: **junior employees report feeling more anxious and overwhelmed about AI than excited, while senior employees report the opposite.** The leader is energized by the capability. The people further down the org chart are processing an existential question about their roles — and often doing so without support.

---

### Data that names it

**"AI tools didn't reduce work — they consistently intensified it."**
— Harvard Business Review, "AI Doesn't Reduce Work — It Intensifies It," February 9, 2026 (8-month study, 200-person tech company) (para. 2)

*This names the mechanism precisely. It's not that AI is bad for people — it's that AI without intent creates a predictable trap.*

**"A self-reinforcing cycle: AI accelerated certain tasks, which raised expectations for speed, which increased AI reliance, which expanded the scope of work."**
— HBR, February 9, 2026 (implications section, para. 1)

*This gives them the mechanism. It's not random — it's predictable. Which means it's designable.*

**"Without intention, AI makes it easier to do more — but harder to stop."**
— HBR, February 9, 2026 (final paragraph)

*This is the line that creates recognition. Every person who has experienced this will say "yes, exactly."*

---

### Framework entry point

**Primary:** Module 5 (Build for What's Coming) — specifically the Sustainability Imperative. The organizations that get this right treat sustainability of their human workforce as a design constraint, not an afterthought.

**Secondary:** Module 1 (Define Your AI Intent) — if "help my team" was the intent, it needs to be made specific: *what kind of help? What does a good outcome look like for the humans in this system? What work should stop, not just what work should accelerate?*

---

### Pivot phrase

> "What you're observing is actually well-documented — and it's not what most AI deployments plan for. When AI makes tasks easier to start, people start more of them. The work expands. Harvard studied this over eight months and found AI consistently intensifies work rather than reducing it — unless there's intentional design around what the AI is supposed to free people *from*, not just what it enables them to do more of. That's a different conversation than 'how do we deploy the tools.' Want to have it?"

---

### Deck-ready pulls

- **The finding:** "AI doesn't reduce work. It intensifies it. Eight months, 200-person company, HBR."
- **The mechanism:** "AI accelerates tasks → expectations rise → reliance increases → scope expands. It's a cycle."
- **The line:** "Without intention, AI makes it easier to do more — but harder to stop."
- **The equity signal:** "Junior employees feel anxious. Senior leaders feel energized. That gap is a design problem."

## Archetype 5: The Burned Skeptic

**"We ran a pilot, it didn't stick, and I'm not sure what to do with that."**

**Typical role:** Any senior leader who championed or was responsible for a previous AI initiative that underperformed, or who has watched others fail and is now being asked to try again.

**Emotional state:** Defensive, risk-averse, and quietly frustrated by vendor hype and internal pressure to revisit something that already cost them. They're not opposed to AI — they're opposed to repeating an expensive lesson.

---

### What they're saying

> "We did the pilot. We trained people. We bought the licenses. Usage dropped off in three months. Now there's pressure to try something new and I'm sitting here thinking — what's actually different about this time?"

> "I'm not saying AI doesn't work. I'm saying nobody has shown me convincingly why this time is different from last time."

---

### What they're actually worried about

Repeating the same mistake with better branding on it. They've been sold a "transformational" outcome before. They've seen the testimonials and the ROI projections. The question they need answered isn't "is AI real?" — it's "what specifically was wrong last time, and how do we know we've fixed it?"

---

### What they believe is true

The last pilot failed because of execution — the tool wasn't right, the training wasn't enough, the timing was bad, or people just didn't adopt. What's usually actually true: the pilot had no defined organizational intent, no baseline measurement, and no use case infrastructure. It produced individual-level usage and no organizational outcomes, which felt like failure even when individual users liked the tool.

---

### What their teams are actually experiencing

Teams often have more nuanced feelings than their leaders about a failed pilot. Some are quietly relieved — the pressure to use a tool they didn't understand is gone. Others are frustrated — they found it genuinely useful and want to continue, but organizational support evaporated. A meaningful group quietly kept using the tool on their own. The "failure" narrative at the leadership level often coexists with ongoing grassroots usage at the individual level that nobody is tracking.

---

### Data that names it

**"74% of companies globally report they have yet to see tangible value from AI."**
— Nate B Jones, AI News & Strategy Daily, February 24, 2026 (para. 29)

*Lead with this because it validates their skepticism rather than arguing against it. You're not trying to convince them AI works — you're diagnosing why it hasn't worked for them yet. This shows their experience is the industry norm.*

**"Without a coherent AI strategy in organizations, you are likely to see pilot fatigue."**
— Deloitte AI Institute, "State of AI in the Enterprise," January 2026 (p. 9)

*This names the trap. It's not that pilots are bad — it's that pilots without a strategy for what comes next become a permanent state.*

**"Level 2 feels like you are done. But you are not done. 90% of AI-native developers are living right now at Level 2."**
— Dan Shapiro, "The Five Levels," January 23, 2026 (Level 2 section)

*This reframes the failed pilot as a maturity pattern, not a unique failure. They probably got to Level 2 — which genuinely feels like progress — and stopped there.*

---

### Framework entry point

**Primary:** Module 3 (Name the Gap) — this is a diagnosis conversation before a prescription conversation. The skeptic needs to understand *what type of gap* they had before they can trust a proposed fix.

**The diagnostic question:** "Tell me about the pilot — what were you measuring? What was the tool supposed to accomplish for the organization, specifically? And where did it break down?" Almost always this surfaces a Measurement Gap or an Intent Gap, not a Tool Ceiling.

**The offer:** "Before we talk about what to do differently, let's name precisely what went wrong last time. Once we've done that, you'll know whether any proposed solution is actually addressing the real problem."

---

### Pivot phrase

> "I'd rather start by agreeing on what went wrong than pitching you a new approach. Walk me through the pilot — what were you measuring, what did success look like at the start, and where did it break down? My guess is we'll find one of three things: a tool ceiling, a measurement gap, or an intent gap. Each one has a different fix. And once we've named which one, you'll know whether what I'm proposing is actually addressing it."

---

### Deck-ready pulls

- **The validation:** "74% of companies globally haven't seen tangible value yet. Your pilot isn't the exception — it's the norm."
- **The diagnosis:** "Three types of gaps: tool ceiling, measurement gap, intent gap. Which one did you have?"
- **The pattern:** "Without a coherent AI strategy, organizations cycle through pilots indefinitely. The answer isn't a better pilot — it's a strategy."

## Archetype 6: The Urgency-Driven Leader

**"Our competitors are moving fast and I feel like we're already behind."**

**Typical role:** CEO or business unit leader in a market where AI-native competitors are emerging, or where board pressure about competitive positioning has become explicit. Often has more urgency than clarity.

**Emotional state:** Anxious, competitive, and possibly slightly panicked. There's genuine external pressure — real or perceived — and they're responding to it with motion rather than direction. They're moving, but not necessarily toward anything specific.

---

### What they're saying

> "I read about what [competitor] is doing. I see what AI-native startups are building. I know this is not a slow-moving trend. I feel like every month we wait is ground we're not getting back."

> "I don't want to be the company that did everything right for 20 years and then lost to a startup that started 18 months ago. That's my nightmare."

---

### What they're actually worried about

Irrelevance. Being disrupted while trying to be careful and deliberate. The fear underneath is that urgency is real and that moving thoughtfully is actually the same as moving too slowly. This is the archetype most likely to make fast, undirected AI investments — and most likely to end up in the Archetype 1 pattern six months later.

---

### What they believe is true

The primary risk is moving too slowly. Speed is the priority. "We can course-correct once we're moving." That's partially true for some decisions. It's not true for decisions about organizational intent — those course-corrections are expensive.

---

### What their teams are actually experiencing

Teams pushed to adopt AI quickly under competitive pressure experience HBR's work intensification pattern in its most acute form. The urgency transmits downward: move faster, adopt more, demonstrate results. Without intent or use case infrastructure, this produces exactly what the HBR study found — more work, higher expectations, no sustainable improvement. The teams doing the most visible "AI work" often feel the least supported.

There's also an equity dimension: junior employees pushed fastest to adopt AI report feeling more anxious and overwhelmed than excited, while senior employees are energized by the competitive opportunity. The urgency at the top creates stress at the bottom.

---

### Data that names it

**"Incumbents that don't war-game how AI-native startups will attack their business risk losing 15–20% of revenue before they even see it coming."**
— Scott Galloway and Greg Shove, Section AI:ROI Conference, Spring 2026
*(Speaker assertion at a conference, not cited research — use as framing to validate urgency, not as a statistic)*

*Lead with this to validate their urgency. You're not here to slow them down.*

**"Individual AI fluency delivers 30% productivity gains from bolting AI onto existing workflows. Organizational AI leverage delivers 300% gains from rethinking workflows around AI capabilities."**
— Nate B Jones, AI News & Strategy Daily, February 24, 2026 (para. 43)

*This is the key turn: the competitive advantage isn't speed of deployment alone — it's the difference between 30% and 300%. Undirected speed gets you 30%. Directed speed gets you 300%.*

**"The race is not who has the smartest AI in their systems. It's who has built the organizational infrastructure that lets AI operate with the fullest, most accurate, most strategically correct understanding."**
— Nate B Jones, AI News & Strategy Daily, February 24, 2026 (para. 63)

*This reframes what they're competing on. It's not a model race or a deployment speed race — it's an intent infrastructure race.*

---

### Framework entry point

**Primary:** Module 1 (Define Your AI Intent) — with urgency reframed, not dismissed. The argument: the way to move fast *effectively* is to define the destination first. Undirected speed is expensive.

**The key reframe:** "Speed is right. Direction makes speed effective. The Intent Architecture is how you make your urgency count."

**Secondary:** Module 4 (Design the Path) — the three-phase transformation gives them a sequenced roadmap that validates urgency at each phase while preventing the mistake of jumping from Phase 1 to Phase 3.

---

### Pivot phrase

> "You're right that the urgency is real — I'm not going to tell you to slow down. But there's a 10x difference between what organizations get from moving fast without direction and moving fast with it. One gets you 30% productivity gains. The other gets you 300%. The question isn't how fast — it's fast toward what. Let's spend 90 minutes defining that, so that everything you do after is pointed at something."

---

### Deck-ready pulls

- **The urgency validation:** "The competitive risk is real. Incumbents risk losing 15–20% of revenue to AI-native startups."
- **The reframe:** "30% gains from bolting AI onto workflows. 300% gains from rethinking workflows around AI. Speed alone gets you the smaller number."
- **The race:** "This isn't a model race. It's an intent race — who built the organizational infrastructure for AI to operate with the most strategically correct understanding."

## Archetype 7: The Internally Divided Room

**"We can't get alignment on what we're actually trying to do with AI."**

**Typical role:** The leader trying to build cross-functional AI strategy — often the CIO, CDO, or whoever has been handed "AI" as a responsibility — who is navigating different priorities, risk tolerances, and mental models across the leadership team.

**Emotional state:** Politically fatigued, frustrated that what seems like an obvious opportunity keeps stalling at the alignment layer, and possibly doubting whether the organization is actually capable of moving on this.

---

### What they're saying

> "IT wants to lock everything down. The business units want to move fast and experiment. Finance wants proof before any more investment. HR is worried about the job conversation. We've been in this loop for months."

> "We had a great strategy session. Everyone agreed in the room. Two weeks later we were back to the same debates. I can't figure out why the alignment doesn't hold."

---

### What they're actually worried about

That the organization is structurally incapable of making the decisions AI requires — and that this will become visible to competitors and to the board before they've found a way through it. The real fear is organizational dysfunction being exposed by the AI question, not the AI question itself.

---

### What they believe is true

The misalignment is primarily about competing priorities or risk tolerance differences — and that if they could just get the right people in a room with the right data, they'd reach a decision. The missing ingredient, in their model, is information or authority.

---

### What their teams are actually experiencing

The misalignment at the leadership level doesn't stay contained — it transmits downward. Teams get inconsistent signals about what's approved, what's encouraged, and what's off-limits. Some teams move aggressively; others wait. Shadow AI proliferates. IT discovers tools it didn't approve. HR fields questions nobody has answers to. The front line improvises in the absence of direction.

The data makes this concrete: **50% of C-level executives believe their organization has widespread AI adoption. Only 26% of managers and employees below them agree.** The misalignment isn't just horizontal (across functions) — it's vertical too. The gap between what the room thinks they've achieved and what the organization is actually experiencing is significant.

**Primary:** Module 1 (Define Your AI Intent) — framed explicitly as an *alignment mechanism*, not just a strategic planning exercise. The value of Module 1 in this context is that it creates a shared definition of what AI is supposed to accomplish — and that definition is what alignment sticks to. Without it, every new piece of information reopens the debate.

**The facilitation angle:** This is where the Intent Architecture's design thinking roots are most directly relevant. Cross-functional tension isn't solved by more data or more presentations — it's solved by a structured process that surfaces assumptions, makes tradeoffs explicit, and creates shared commitment. That's the native use case.

The Intent Architecture is built for exactly this situation. It's a facilitated process designed to answer those questions in a way that produces commitment, not just agreement in the room."

## Archetype 8: The Engineering-to-Org Bridge

**"Our engineering team gets it — how do we bring the rest of the org along?"**

**Typical role:** CTO, CIO, or technically-oriented CEO who has seen real AI gains in their engineering organization and is now trying to replicate or extend that pattern into non-technical functions. More AI-literate than most. Further along the adoption curve. Faces a genuine translation problem.

**Emotional state:** Energized and somewhat frustrated simultaneously. They have proof it works. They can't figure out why it's not spreading. There's sometimes an implicit assumption that engineering "gets it" and everyone else needs to catch up — which is partly right and partly a misread of the situation.

---

### What they're saying

> "Our engineers have been using Cursor / Claude Code / GitHub Copilot for 18 months. I can see the output difference. Velocity is up. Quality is better. But when I try to apply the same logic to our marketing team or our ops team, I get blank stares."

> "The engineering transformation happened kind of organically. I'm not sure I can articulate exactly why it worked there and why it's not working elsewhere."

---

### What they're actually worried about

Two things. First, that the engineering gains are a local phenomenon that don't generalize — that the model which worked for structured, documentable code work doesn't apply to less structured functions. Second, that they'll be asked to show org-wide AI impact and won't be able to, even though engineering is clearly already there.

---

### What they believe is true

That the reason it worked in engineering is the tools — that the right tools for other functions will produce the same result. This is partially true. What's more completely true: engineering worked because the work is structured and the output is verifiable. Code either runs or it doesn't. "What good looks like" is relatively clear. Non-technical functions need something engineering had naturally: a shared definition of what good output looks like.

---

### What their teams are actually experiencing

**Two very different realities exist simultaneously in this organization.** Engineering has moved. The tools have changed how work is done, roles are beginning to shift, and there's a genuine culture of AI experimentation. Meanwhile, other functions are watching from the outside and processing two very different responses.

Some are curious and want to participate. But many are anxious — they're watching their engineering colleagues' roles transform in real time and quietly asking: *when does that come for us?* Junior employees particularly report feeling more anxious than excited about AI, even in organizations where senior leaders feel energized. The anxiety isn't irrational. It's a reasonable response to watching a preview of what's actually coming.

---

### Data that names it

**"Copilot makes writing code cheaper but owning it more expensive."**
— AI News & Strategy Daily, "Junior Dev Jobs Dropped," February 18, 2026 (~18:45 min)

*This adds useful nuance to the engineering win — even in the function where AI has worked best, the gains create new costs and new skills requirements. Use this to show you understand the complexity, not just the headline.*

**"Tasks that can be documented — like generating a report from data — are easy to automate. Tasks that can't — like deciding what makes a good investment — resist it."**
— Natalia Quintero, "The Next Chapter of Every Consulting," Every, February 3, 2026

*This is the key insight for non-engineering functions: the automation frontier isn't defined by job category — it's defined by documentability. Every function has documentable tasks and judgment tasks. The transformation is about redrawing that line.*

**"We're moving rapidly to an allocation economy, where individuals won't be judged by the limits of subject matter expertise, but by how well they can allocate and manage AI resources to get work done."**
— Natalia Quintero, Every, February 3, 2026 (Introduction section)

*This names the deeper shift that's coming for all functions — and it's already visible in engineering.*

---

### The engineering pattern as a leading indicator

This deserves its own moment in the conversation.

**What happened in engineering:** Engineers moved from writing code to managing AI agents that write code. Their role is now to define requirements clearly enough for AI to execute, review and verify output, and communicate what "good" looks like — judgment and taste, not just technical production. The engineers who adapted fastest are the ones who could think at the system level: what needs to happen, and what does "done well" look like?

**What's coming for other functions:** The same transition — just slower, less structured, and harder to see. The marketer who used to write copy will direct AI that writes copy, and their value will be in knowing what "good" sounds like. The analyst who used to build models will direct AI that builds models, and their value will be in knowing what a well-structured question looks like and whether the answer is trustworthy. The operations person who used to run processes will design and oversee the AI workflows that run them.

**The critical question for this leader:** Is the organization building the capabilities that make this transition deliberate rather than chaotic? Because the transition is coming regardless — the only variable is whether people are ready for it or blindsided by it.

---

### Framework entry point

**Primary:** Module 5 (Build for What's Coming) — this is the one archetype that leads with the future, not a current problem.

**Secondary:** Module 1 (Define Your AI Intent) applied to non-engineering functions. Engineering's "intent" was clear: write better code faster. What is the equivalent statement for marketing? For operations? For finance? That's the work that hasn't been done.

**The bridge argument:** "Engineering worked because the work is structured and the output is verifiable. Non-technical functions need a different infrastructure to get there: explicit use cases for specific roles, clear definitions of what 'good output' looks like, and a measurement system connected to business outcomes. That's what the Intent Architecture builds."

---

### Pivot phrase

> "What you're seeing in engineering is actually a preview of what's coming for every knowledge worker function in your organization — and the reason it worked in engineering is that the work is structured enough that 'what good looks like' is relatively clear. The harder question is how you build that same clarity for marketing, operations, finance. That's not a tools problem — it's an organizational design problem. And the window to do it proactively, before the transition becomes chaotic, is probably narrower than you think."

---

### Deck-ready pulls

- **The pattern:** "Engineering moved from writing code to directing AI that writes code. That pattern is coming for every white-collar function."
- **The documentability insight:** "Tasks that can be documented are easy to automate. Tasks that can't — judgment, taste, values — resist it. Every function has both."
- **The allocation economy:** "Soon, people won't be judged by the limits of their expertise — but by how well they direct AI to do the work. Is your organization building for that?"
